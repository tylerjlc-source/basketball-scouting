## Section 3 — Scouting Narrative

**Identity.** Anfernee Simons is a shot-making combo guard whose calling card is elite pull-up and catch-and-shoot three-point volume at or above 37–40% efficiency, supported by a 41.5" vertical that never translated into the contact finishing his measurables suggest it should. The first thing you notice is the shooting gravity — defenders chase him off the line across the full half-court, because at any spot inside the three-point arc he can rise and release cleanly. The second thing you notice is what happens on the other end: opposing offenses hunt him through screens, dribble handoffs, and pin-downs on virtually every possession.

**Offensive profile.** The shooting is real and multi-zone. He pours in 3s at 40%+ on catch-and-shoot looks across massive volume, holds pull-up efficiency at a league-average mid-30s clip, connects from mid-range when he chooses to, and converts 89–90% at the free throw line with a 95%+ clutch rate. The handle supports meaningful on-ball creation — 70th-percentile pick-and-roll and isolation efficiency with functional ball security across primary usage. Floater touch has lifted into the 50s in his most recent season, and his finishing at the rim converts at a strong 63% weighted rate. Where the offensive profile cracks is at the point of contact: 0.14 free-throw rate is well below his scoring peers, a 5% dunk rate leaves 36 inches of vertical on the table, and drive PPP hovers at 0.74 because he elects finesse over absorbing defenders. He is a functional but uninspired secondary passer — vision for the open teammate exists, but the bias is scorer-first and adjusted assist percentage lives in the low teens even at primary-creator usage. Shot selection is the persistent drag — scouts repeat the phrase "contested pull-up threes" across multiple seasons, and it maps to a 40% contested-attempt rate that keeps true-shooting at 56.8% rather than the 59%+ his mechanics would otherwise support.

**Defensive profile.** This is where the profile falls apart. He has been one of the worst defenders on his team in each of his eight NBA seasons. Opposing offenses run him through pin-downs, curls, flare screens, and dribble-handoffs as a matter of course — his rotations lag, his screen navigation breaks down, and his contest volume runs well below starter norms. His DFGPOE sits at +2.4%, meaning opponents shoot 2.4 percentage points better than expected when he is the primary defender. His 0.2% block rate in 2024–25 was the lowest in the NBA among players logging 2,000+ minutes. The mechanism is a three-factor stack: below-guard-baseline strength at his frame, inconsistent lateral effort through screens, and engagement lapses the league has flagged across multiple franchises and coaching staffs. Rim protection is a structural zero at his size. Team defense drops measurably with him on the floor (-2.5 points per 100 possessions, 28th percentile), and he has shown no meaningful defensive trajectory in 8 years.

**Character and trajectory.** Character reads positive on the offensive development side — Lillard described him as already in the gym for extra work as a rookie, Mazzulla publicly endorsed his work ethic in Boston's contract-year setup, and he accepted the mid-season role reduction (32.7 to 24.9 MPG) without visible friction. Turnover rate has improved across the evaluation window. Offensively, the motor is functional. But the defensive-effort lag is the counterbalancing signal, and it has not moved despite three coaching staffs and a contract-year incentive. Competitive character reads neutral — no documented negative flags, modest positive clutch signal (45% clutch 3PT, 95% clutch FT in small samples), but no standout big-moment reputation. Trajectory overall is flat: the offensive skill peaked around 2022–23, and the defensive profile has never improved.

**Bottom line.** Simons is a genuine Microwave Scorer whose elite shooting volume makes him a valuable offensive piece on the right team, but whose defensive drag and missing contact-finishing pillar cap him well below the star-scorer tier his shooting suggests on its face. He plays like a shooting-heavy Jordan Clarkson with more handle — a starting-caliber 17–21 PPG guard when healthy, a playoff-lineup question mark in every series against a team that can attack him on defense. At 26 with eight seasons in, the picture is what it is — further improvement is unlikely to come from new skill development, only from environment.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.

The OVR is not calculated from the attribute ratings or skill areas. It is assigned by comparing the player against a library of anchor players whose ratings have been calibrated and locked in.

That is why an OVR can sit higher than any individual skill area. The OVR measures the whole player against the league; the attribute ratings and skill areas show where specific strengths and weaknesses fall within position.
