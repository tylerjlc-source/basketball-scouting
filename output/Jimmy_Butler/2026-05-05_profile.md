## 3. Scouting Narrative

Late-career Butler is a high-IQ creator-scorer-passer wing whose value comes from efficiency, ball control, and basketball intelligence rather than the athletic separation of his prime. He plays at 6'7.75" with a 1:1-ish wingspan and a wing-strong frame at ~230 pounds — the body that always made him a contact player still works, but the explosion that made him a multi-position perimeter stopper is gone. The player you watch in 2025-26 Golden State is recognizably Butler — same drive-and-foul-draw scoring identity, same elbow-orchestrator instinct, same competitive body language — but carrying him is now a different blend: efficiency over volume, IQ defense over individual lockdown, secondary playmaking over alpha creation.

Offensively, the engine is still the foul line. Butler converts at the rim at 69.7% on a heavy 42.7% rim share, gets to the line at .635 FTR — a rate that has led the league in high-usage-plus-FTR combination since 2019-20 above prime Shaq territory — and finishes with 85% touch when he gets there. The pick-and-roll ball-handler signal is genuinely elite (95th percentile PPP), and the ball security at primary-handler volume is one of the league's quieter superpowers (TOV per 100 touches 2.3, A:TO 3.45). Where the offensive game has changed is on the perimeter: the mid-range that was once a signature zone has eroded to 34.1%, the pull-up 3 sits at 32.5% — below the threshold where it threatens defenses — and the 3-point volume has never expanded into the catch-and-shoot infrastructure GSW gives him. He scores 20 a night in 2025-26 on 51.9% shooting because he goes to spots that don't require those declining tools — the rim, the line, the elbow facing-up against a mismatch — and because Steve Kerr has redesigned his role around exactly this profile, dumping him at the elbow or baseline to isolate, then watching him score-or-kick with 6.1 assists a night and a stretch-five-whisperer connection to Horford and Post.

Defensively, the scouting story is more honest than the team-level numbers suggest. The team-defense signal with Butler on the floor is real — Warriors ranked first in the league in DRtg from his arrival through the end of 2024-25, and Butler-Draymond paired as the second- and sixth-lowest individual DRtg of any 30-MPG player after the trade deadline. But the individual closest-defender data tells the other half: opponents shoot +2.4% above expected against him over the two-season weighted window, and the language has shifted from "lockdown wing" to "surrendered blow-by drives a little more often than was comfortable." Butler at 36 is a help defender, not a stopper. He sees rotations early, he times his digs, his steals and deflections track at competent rates, and his physical strength still lets him hold position in switches against bigger players — but if you put him on Anthony Edwards or Jalen Williams in a primary on-ball role, the matchup will probably go the offense's way. Rim protection was never his game and remains structurally limited by the wingspan; post defense is functional rather than weaponized. The Defensive Specialist version of Butler ended somewhere in the early 2020s.

The character file is the most-discussed and most-debated part of the profile. The competitive resume is genuinely top-tier: 2020 Bubble Finals where he averaged 26/9.8/8.3 across six games and posted a 40-point triple-double without a 3-point attempt in Game 3; 2023 ECF MVP run leading an 8-seeded Heat team to the Finals while averaging 31.9 points in the playoffs and dropping 56 in a single game; 2025 first-round vs Houston where he carried a Curry-less Game 4 with 23 of 27 points in the second half to validate the Warriors' deadline trade. "Playoff Jimmy" is one of the most-cited basketball-character labels in the league, and it earns its place. But the negative file is equally documented and equally specific. Three Heat suspensions in January 2025, walked out of practice, "no joy" comments, Pat Riley calling him "combative in meetings with management and ownership" — that's not nothing, even if the Warriors environment has held the character forces in check since the trade. The 2025 second round vs Minnesota with Curry sidelined is the cleaner basketball signal of the same fault: 31 total points on 20 attempts across two elimination losses, "no excuse for how I played" self-assessment, NBA.com framing it as "didn't live up to the Playoff Jimmy moniker." Butler can carry — but as a complement, not as the actual #1. And the November 2025 public dispute with Steve Kerr over Warriors defense suggests the friction follows him into new environments, just at lower intensity.

The bottom line: a high-floor, capped-ceiling distributed profile. Seventeen sub-domains break 7.0 across all eight domains; nothing is below 5.0; the foul-draw and free-throw and ball-security peaks (#2=9.4, #7=9.1, #10=9.0) are genuinely rare, and the pick-and-roll creation skill is elite. But only those three sub-domains break 8.5 — there is no second-tier weapon that lifts the profile above its distributed shape, and the age-decline + ACL injury arc means whatever next-tier upside exists is forward-narrowing rather than forward-expanding. The composite reflects an efficient, complete, post-peak wing creator producing All-Star-line numbers at age 36, before a major injury removed him from the next playoff cycle and reshaped the projection horizon.
