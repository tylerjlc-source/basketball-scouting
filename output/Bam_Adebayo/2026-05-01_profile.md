## Section 3 — Scouting Narrative

Adebayo is the modern franchise center built around dual identities — a primary offensive hub running Miami's half-court offense from the elbow and high post, and the league's most credible 1-through-5 perimeter switch defender at his size. The first thing you notice is what he does for his size: 6'9.75" with a 7'2.75" wingspan, 5.2% body fat at draft weight, and the lateral quickness and recovery explosion to mirror wings off the dribble at center size. He is undersized for the position by traditional measurement, and he plays bigger than that almost everywhere except where it matters most.

Offensively, he is a hub before he is a scorer. He passes like a top-tier playmaking big — the role is structurally similar to what Marc Gasol ran in Memphis or Jokić runs in Denver, with Miami's entire dribble-handoff offense routed through him at the high post and the team scoring at near-Jokić-tier rates when he touches the ball. He scores 18-22 points per game across multiple zones: a 96th-percentile-among-bigs mid-range diet that he converts at functional rates, an above-average drive game (6+ drives per game at 46% from the field), and a 3-point shot that has gone from a vestigial threat at 4% of his attempts to a 19% share with stretches of 40%+ shooting. He gets to the line on roughly a third of his field goal attempts and finished one regular-season game with the all-time NBA records for free throws made (36) and attempted (43). The shooting is the area where the gap between trajectory and floor is widest — segment numbers are bright, full-window aggregates are ordinary, and the projection should reflect both.

Defensively, the picture splits cleanly into two halves of the floor. On the perimeter he is elite — multi-source convergence on "best switching big in the league," leads the NBA in pick-and-roll switches at 64 points per 100 possessions allowed, and isolation-defense efficiency in the 71st-to-92nd percentile across the evaluation window. He is the player most NBA defensive coordinators would build a switching scheme around. At the rim he is the opposite — opponents converted 70.6% of contested attempts at the basket against him in 2024-25, the worst rate of any qualifying center, and his block rate of 0.7 per game is modest for the position. Miami's 2025-26 partial mitigation of pairing him with Kel'el Ware as the rim partner is the active-roster solution, but the structural deficit is real, fires the position non-negotiable cap, and is the single most consequential drag on his ceiling.

Character is unambiguous. The pre-draft Heat workout where he challenged Pat Riley and Erik Spoelstra has been retold by every relevant participant; Spoelstra has called him "a caretaker for this culture," and Udonis Haslem has called him "literally our hardest worker." He was the heart and soul of Miami's 2023 #8-seed Finals run, averaging 21.8 points and 12.4 rebounds, with multi-source language casting him as the team's spine on both ends. He has been an All-Star in five of six seasons and the All-Defensive First Team selection in 2023-24. Trajectory at age 28 reads laterally — his 3-point extension and late-window scoring stretches in 2024-25 and 2025-26 push offensive ceiling forward, while his All-Defensive miss in 2024-25 and the persistent rim-protection deficit cap defensive ceiling. He is largely the player he is going to be.

The bottom line: Adebayo is a clear All-Around Big in the Jokić/AD confirmed-fit cohort whose offensive role and defensive footprint earn that placement, but whose rim protection separates him meaningfully from the tier above his current band. The case for him pushing higher than this composite rests on the Ware partnership reducing his rim exposure and the 3-point extension continuing to convert on volume; the case for him sliding rests on accelerating decline of the lateral mobility that anchors his switch identity. As of May 2026, he sits at the upper edge of perennial All-Star territory with a clearly defined ceiling above and a clearly defined floor below.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.

The OVR is not calculated from the attribute ratings or skill areas. It is assigned by comparing the player against a library of anchor players whose ratings have been calibrated and locked in.

That is why an OVR can sit higher than any individual skill area. The OVR measures the whole player against the league; the attribute ratings and skill areas show where specific strengths and weaknesses fall within position.
