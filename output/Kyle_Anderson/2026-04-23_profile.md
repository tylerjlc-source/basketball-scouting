## Section 3 — Scouting Narrative

Anderson is the living template for the Secondary Playmaker archetype — a 6'9" point-forward whose identity is built on craft, length, and basketball IQ rather than athletic tools, and who has made a 12-year NBA career out of producing value faster than burst-limited wings are supposed to. "Slow Mo" has been his nickname since 8th-grade AAU, and it has aged into the single most accurate descriptor of his style: deliberate pace, fakes and spin moves rather than first-step separation, length and angles rather than speed and verticality. He catches, surveys, and delivers — with the kind of half-court pass-accuracy coaches describe in "pinpoint" terms — on every other possession he's in.

The offensive profile is narrow by design and excellent within its lane. He converts at the rim at a 65.5% clip across the two-season weighted window — elite for any wing archetype — with a dunk rate of 9.5% that quietly suggests the vertical is better than the nickname implies. Floaters make up 53.9% of his total FGAs, he hits them at 45.3%, and his non-dunk rim finishing at 63.4% reflects soft touch and creative angle-use at the cup. Outside the paint, the shooting falls off: catch-and-shoot 3PT at 33.3% weighted (cleaner at 37.0% prior season, cratered to 14.3% on a cosmic small sample this year), 71.8% free throws. The shooting is functional but not weapon-grade — and the Secondary Playmaker archetype is specifically designed around not needing it to be. What the archetype does need is clean decisions with the ball, and Anderson delivers at an elite rate: A:TO of 3.51 is top-of-archetype, TOV per 100 touches at 2.3 is genuinely elite, and assist points created of 6.9 on only 13.9% usage signals the per-minute production rate that has kept him in rotations for over a decade.

Defensively, the 7'3" wingspan does all the heavy lifting that the lateral quickness can't — and the lateral quickness genuinely can't. The persistent draft-era critique ("extremely upright stance, reaching for steals is the most effective strategy") holds through today. What the length produces is real: DFGPOE sits at a neutral +0.1%, meaning opponents shoot exactly as expected against him rather than exploiting him; deflections at 2.1/g and help blocks at 0.6/g outperform wing baseline; help-defense rating is above average. He is not a stopper, but the archetype isn't asking him to be one. He is the kind of defender whose presence doesn't break a defense even when he can't make it better — which at 6'9" with 7'3" arms is meaningful role currency.

The character file is, unusually for a multi-team journeyman, uniformly clean. Twelve NBA seasons with zero documented motor critiques, zero attitude flags, nine playoff seasons across five franchises, and multi-source "toughness, leadership, fan favorite" framing that extends across every team stop. The 2024 Timberwolves WCF run gave him a real contributing rotation role at age 30 — evidence that when the team context fits his skillset, he still plays winning basketball. The 2025-26 Utah-to-Memphis-to-Minnesota journey is role-driven rather than performance-driven, with his latest Wolves signing specifically positioning him for the stretch run and playoffs. R13 playoff track record is neutral on both axes: career TS delta -0.8 is within statistical noise, and last three runs mix one contributing role (2023-24) with two role-driven dormant appearances (2024-25, 2025-26).

The bottom line is a Swiss-army-knife bench Secondary Playmaker whose per-minute production, clean character, and length-driven defensive viability make him a repeatable rotation piece for the remainder of his career. Ceiling going forward is a key piece on a contender — a return to the 2024 WCF form at starter minutes. Floor is continued waiver cycles if team contexts don't align, but his floor is durably high: no 12-year NBA vet with zero negative signals and a 3.5 A:TO falls out of the league absent injury. Age 32 is the inflection point where his physical margin is already at its floor; the craft that carried him here is the same craft that carries him the rest of the way.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.

The OVR is not calculated from the attribute ratings or skill areas. It is assigned by comparing the player against a library of anchor players whose ratings have been calibrated and locked in.

That is why an OVR can sit higher than any individual skill area. The OVR measures the whole player against the league; the attribute ratings and skill areas show where specific strengths and weaknesses fall within position.
