## Section 3 — Scouting Narrative

A 33-year-old All-Around Big whose two-way ceiling — when on the floor — still places him among the league's top dozen players. Davis is built on a 6'10" / 7'5.5"-wingspan / 9'0"-reach frame that has carried elite mobility into his thirteenth NBA season; the combination of size, length, and preserved guard-skill instincts produces a profile no other modern big quite replicates. The defining tension in his profile is no longer skill — it is availability.

Offensively, the package is unusually broad for his archetype. Davis lives at the rim where he converts at a near-74% clip on huge volume, draws fouls at the highest rate in the league among high-volume drivers, and finishes through contact with both hands. The face-up game and floater volume — 369 attempts in the 4-to-14-foot zone over the eval window, more than a third of his total field goals — anchor a mid-range and pull-up package that registers as anomalous craft for the position. He initiates Delay and Horns sets as the trigger man at 28.5% usage and reads the secondary action competently, though his half-court tendencies still skew toward shooting from the post rather than passing out, and his catch-and-shoot three remains a soft spot — early-season hot stretches in 2024-25 did not survive a full sample, with the season landing at 28% from deep on meaningful volume. He is not a true spacer; defenses can leave him beyond the arc. Everything inside it is a problem.

Defensively, the case is almost the inverse — virtually no soft spots, with the rim, the post, and the help line all anchored at All-Defensive First Team levels. Davis's post-up suppression in 2023-24 led the league among high-volume post defenders, and his points-per-direct-play numbers as a help defender ranked first in the NBA. He switches onto wings credibly, contests at the rim with timing rather than fouling, and rebounds the defensive glass at a rate that anchors team possessions. The team-level impact has not always matched the individual measurements — Lakers DRtg was middle-of-the-pack in 2023-24 — but at the player level the four defensive sub-domains all measure elite.

Character is the layered question. The playoff record is a real signal: Davis was the best player on the floor for stretches of the 2020 title run, hit a Game 2 dagger in the 2023 Western Conference Finals, and put up 27.8 / 15.6 in the 2024 first round while playing one-armed through a shoulder injury. The career playoff-versus-regular-season delta is +1.5 TS% across six runs — a strong-rise signal by R13 standards. Against that record sits a sustained, multi-source critique that has followed him from New Orleans through Los Angeles to Dallas: deferential half-court tendencies, "soft" framing in moments where the team's best option fades to a screener role, and motion-heavy schemes designed to keep him out of static post catches. Both readings are true. The competitive fire shows up at the highest stakes; the day-to-day half-court ownership does not always match it.

Trajectory is the load-bearing concern. The 2023-24 season — 76 games, All-NBA Second Team, All-Defense First Team, fourth in DPOY voting — was a genuine peak-tier campaign. The 2024-25 split between Lakers and Mavericks held most of the production but began softening at the edges (paint percentage hit an eight-year low at 60%, FG% slid four points). The 2025-26 fragment is the first credible production-decline season layered onto a worsening availability trend: 20 games, bilateral Achilles tendinopathy, recurring calf strain, hand ligament damage that ended his season, and a second mid-season trade to a non-playoff team. The composite reflects the healthy-baseline read from the eval window. The forward projection cannot ignore what the most recent fragment is showing.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.

The OVR is not calculated from the attribute ratings or skill areas. It is assigned by comparing the player against a library of anchor players whose ratings have been calibrated and locked in.

That is why an OVR can sit higher than any individual skill area. The OVR measures the whole player against the league; the attribute ratings and skill areas show where specific strengths and weaknesses fall within position.
