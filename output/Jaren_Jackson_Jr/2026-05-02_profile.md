## Section 3 — Scouting narrative

A switchable defensive big whose frame and tools say "five" but whose feet, hands, and shooting touch say "modern four." The first thing you notice is the length-and-mobility combination — 7'5.25" wingspan attached to small-forward foot speed and a 40" vertical, on a lean 240-pound frame that runs the floor more than it absorbs contact. The defensive identity is the value driver: 2022-23 DPOY at age 23, multi-year league leader in blocks, and an isolation defender who shifts seamlessly between hedging, switching, trapping, and dropping. Everything else is layered onto that.

Offensively the profile is built around floor spacing and touch. The catch-and-shoot three is a real weapon — 38.6% on 437 attempts across the evaluation window, with credible range to both corners and above the break. Free throw stroke is genuinely elite for a big at 79% career; the floater is the league's most-used signature look (448 attempts at 51% inside 14 feet); the rim conversion holds up at 63%. What's not there is creation. The pull-up jumper is unreliable, the handle is functional but not shifty in traffic, the post game has been quietly abandoned in favor of face-up drives, and the playmaking is secondary at best — assist conversion sits below 50% and the assist-to-turnover ratio runs negative at 26% usage. He scores in volume because the shot diet is healthy and the offense flows through his spacing, not because he creates advantages off the dribble.

Defensively the ceiling is genuine but the structural gaps are persistent. On-ball pressure, help rotations, lateral switchability, and rim deterrence are all All-Defensive level — opponents shoot below 43% against his contests overall. The post defense and rebounding are the chronic limitations. The lean frame yields ground against stronger bigs, and the rebounding numbers have plateaued at well below big-position-anchor rates for seven straight seasons — fewer than one box-out per game, defensive rebounding chance percentage in the bottom third of starting bigs. The fouls compound the issue: 3.6 per game and a career-high 16% of minutes in foul trouble in 2025-26. The defensive identity is scheme-versatile and tool-driven; what's missing is the physical anchor work that converts elite contests into closed possessions.

Character signals are above-average and durable. He took on the vocal locker-room leadership role through the post-Bane Memphis era, willingly accepted the spokesperson role, and competed on USA Basketball commitments through team-level adversity. The trajectory question is the live one. Pre-2025-26 production climbed steadily — 16 PPG to 22 PPG across four seasons, second All-Star selection in 2024-25, healthy 74-game season anchored by All-Star caliber two-way play. Then came the offseason turf toe surgery, the late season start, the mid-season trade to Utah, and a benign-but-aggressive PVNS growth removed from the left knee in February — season-ending. The 2025-26 sample is short and fragmented; his production with Memphis dropped to 19.4 PPG and several percentile bands collapsed, which The Ringer's "Least Improved" framing captured. Whether 2025-26 is a one-year disruption with a clean return waiting on the other side or the first signal of a flatter forward arc is the central uncertainty.

Bottom line: a recent-vintage All-Star Switchable Big whose defensive identity, spacing, and touch carry an All-Star-caliber profile, with three persistent primary value-driver shortfalls (post defense, defensive rebounding, strength) that have capped his ceiling for years and will not yield without a frame change that, at age 26 with a lean profile, is no longer an open development pathway. The composite reflects current state weighted between the 2024-25 All-Star peak and the 2025-26 disruption; the projection waits for proof-of-return from the knee.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.
