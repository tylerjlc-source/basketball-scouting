## Section 3 — Scouting Narrative

Ja Morant is a 6'2", 174-lb Offensive Engine — a high-usage primary creator whose value lives in the half-court ball-screen game and in transition, where his handle, vision, and remaining burst still produce advantage. He played in 50 games in 2024-25 — the anchor sample for this evaluation, selected as his most-recent pre-injury season after the 2023-24 shoulder surgery. The first thing scouts notice now is what's missing: pre-shoulder Ja was a signature above-rim guard whose dunk rate halved by 2024-25, replaced by a more skill-heavy game built around the floater, the pull-up mid-range, and free-throw conversion.

Offensively, the package is dense at the top. The handle is elite — change of pace and shoulder/hip manipulation that creates separation against most NBA on-ball defenders. The touch and feel are equally elite — the "Ja Floater" reputation is supported by 44.4% on 315 floater attempts, paired with 61.0% non-dunk rim conversion and an 82.4% free-throw stroke. Court vision is the third elite peak — 12.5 potential assists per game and a recurring scout description as a "genius-level passer." The mid-range game has improved (47.8% on a meaningful sample), and his isolation efficiency jumped to the 68th percentile. Where the profile thins: the perimeter shot (33.6% catch-and-shoot, 28.6% pull-up 3) is below the position standard, and the cognitive layer beneath the vision drags. He has the genius-level passing intellect described above, paired with a career-worst 16.9% bad-pass turnover rate — the seeing and the doing diverge by three full points on the rubric. Hero-ball flares returned in late-game possessions in 2024-25, and shot selection sits at average despite improved efficiency.

Defensively, the profile is at the survival floor. On-ball pressure and help defense both score 5.0 — the line below which teams scheme around the player. The film evidence is consistent across multiple sources: the lateral tools to defend at point of attack exist, but on-court application is selective. Ja gambles for steals rather than sliding feet, dies on screens off-ball, and concedes deep post catches to bigger wings. His defensive rating regressed from -3.3 DFGPOE in 2022-23 to -0.2 in 2024-25 — opponents shoot near expected against him now. Defensive rebounding for a 6'2" guard is a real positive (10.1% DREB%, willing crashes from the backcourt), but it's the only defense-adjacent positive in the file. Rim protection and post defense are structural zeros — appropriate by archetype, not held against him.

The character file is non-trivial and bidirectional. The competitive fire is real and well-sourced — Murray State chip-on-shoulder origin, trash-talk reputation, refuses to withdraw under adversity, sharp clutch-scoring uptick in 2024-25 (46.3% FG, +1.0 +/- in close games). The negatives are equally documented: two suspensions in 2023 (8 games March, 25 games starting 2023-24) for displaying handguns on Instagram Live, plus several smaller incidents. Public profile has been notably quieter since December 2023 — no further publicized incidents — but the suspensions sit in the file. Trajectory is the primary concern: 79 games total since the start of 2023-24, currently shut down for the remainder of 2025-26 with a left elbow UCL sprain, ruled out of the upcoming playoff cycle. The athletic decline appears persistent rather than temporary — multi-source comp pattern from league evaluators places his arc alongside Derrick Rose and John Wall, "smaller guards who relied heavily on spectacular athleticism that was sapped by injuries."

The bottom line: Ja sits in upper Tier 7 (8.22) on the strength of an offensive package that still includes three elite peaks (handle, touch, vision) and broad three-level scoring competence, capped by defensive collapse, cognitive concerns at the decision-making layer, the off-court file, and active-injury dormancy that closes the upside path through the playoffs. The path back to Tier 6 runs through clean elbow recovery and a convergent playoff-rise signal in 2026-27 — both gateable conditions, neither currently met.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.

The OVR is not calculated from the attribute ratings or skill areas. It is assigned by comparing the player against a library of anchor players whose ratings have been calibrated and locked in.

That is why an OVR can sit higher than any individual skill area. The OVR measures the whole player against the league; the attribute ratings and skill areas show where specific strengths and weaknesses fall within position.
