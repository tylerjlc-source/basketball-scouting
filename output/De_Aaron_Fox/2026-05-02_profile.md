## Section 3 — Scouting Narrative

The first thing you notice with De'Aaron Fox is the speed. End-to-end transition burst that bends defenses backward, a first step that pulls help defenders out of their stance, and an off-ball motor of 4.69 mph that registers near the top of the league for guards. At 28, the burst is undiminished — and now, in his first full season with San Antonio, it is being deployed in service of a more complete and more selective game than the one that defined him in Sacramento. The wiry frame remains the trade-off — there is a structural cap on contact absorption that no offseason has resolved — but the speed has always been the answer to that limitation, and it still is.

Offensively, Fox is a pick-and-roll engine before anything else. The PnR ball-handler PPP sits at the 81st percentile, drives convert at 54.8%, and the floater (52.5% on 350 attempts) is a load-bearing piece of his shot diet that tilts his rim conversion rate (67.6%) above guard expectation despite a modest 7% dunk rate. The playmaking layer — vision, decision, execution — has matured into a genuine secondary skill, with potential assists at 11.5 per game and a series-clinching nine-assist performance against Portland in his first playoff series win. Where the profile narrows is the off-the-bounce three: 29.3% on pull-ups across two seasons is a real liability, and it caps the comparison set among Offensive Engine peers — Fox is closer in shape to Shai Gilgeous-Alexander's mid-range-and-burst creator than to Curry or Lillard. The catch-and-shoot has been the late-career addition: 35.2% weighted, 37.6% in 2025-26 — a career-best mark and the reason San Antonio can run lineups where Castle and Harper handle.

Defensively, the picture is more complicated. The tools are real — 8.0 lateral quickness, 2.9 deflections per game, 1.3 steals — but the Sacramento years documented a steady decline in engagement and a 13th-percentile pick-and-roll defense rating that no scouting language softened. The 2025-26 defensive rating improvement from 114.9 to 108.9 is partially scheme — Wembanyama at the rim absorbs much of what Fox cannot — and partially a culture-fit gain that the Spurs locker room appears to have unlocked. Fox is functional on the perimeter, capable in switches against most guards, and a real liability against bigger backcourt scorers who can lean on the strength gap. The defensive ceiling is where the Offensive Engine archetype meets its honest limit; Fox is a creator first and a defender second, and the composite reflects that order.

Character and trajectory tell a split story. The competitive evidence is genuine: inaugural Clutch Player of the Year, a historic 27.4-PPG playoff debut against Golden State (192 total points, second-most ever in a playoff debut behind LeBron), 62 games played in 2024-25 on a damaged extensor tendon in his shooting hand, and a 2025-26 playoff breakthrough with two All-Star caliber closing games against Portland. The counter-evidence is also data: a 5-of-19 Game 7 collapse in 2023, the Sacramento "sourpuss" framing, and a defensive coast year that was visible to anyone watching. Fox in San Antonio appears to be a different competitive presence — embracing a reduced volume role (career-low 14.7 FGA), mentoring Castle and Harper, and signing a 4-year max extension that signals franchise commitment rather than star-chasing. Whether the change is durable or context-dependent is the open question on this profile.

The bottom line: Fox is a mid-prime All-Star caliber guard whose value has restructured rather than declined. He is no longer the high-usage Slasher of his early Sacramento years; he is a polished Offensive Engine with elite burst, a real playmaking layer, an improving catch-and-shoot, a structural off-dribble three weakness, and a defense that needs scheme support to function. The Tier 6 placement reflects a complete profile that does not break through to the Mitchell/Brunson/Murray peer set above it, while still sitting clearly above the Morant/Garland tier below.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.

The OVR is not calculated from the attribute ratings or skill areas. It is assigned by comparing the player against a library of anchor players whose ratings have been calibrated and locked in.

That is why an OVR can sit higher than any individual skill area. The OVR measures the whole player against the league; the attribute ratings and skill areas show where specific strengths and weaknesses fall within position.
