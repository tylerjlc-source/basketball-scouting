## 3. Scouting Narrative

Maxey is a primary-handler lead guard whose entire game is built on speed and shooting. He plays with the ball nearly every possession — 28.9% usage, 93.2 touches per game, 7.3 minutes of possession time — and the first thing every defender feels is the first step. Top-end speed is the second wave; defenders can't sit on the gather. The shooting is genuine: catch-and-shoot is elite, the free throw line is automatic, and the threat of the dribble-up three in transition pulls help defenders into rotations they'd rather not make. He is not an above-rim athlete and never has been — the rim finishing he generates comes from craft, body control, and a multi-option decision tree at the cup, not from explosion.

The offensive game spans three levels with a clear hierarchy. Catch-and-shoot is the headline weapon, with current-season volume and percentage that broke the franchise's all-time three-point record. The mid-range stepback is a recently consolidated piece — defenders now have to honor a pull-up that two years ago was readable. The half-court pull-up three is the one zone still developing into the rest of the package; the transition pull-up works at an elite rate, but the contested half-court version converts at a more pedestrian clip. As a primary creator he attacks the rim 14 times a game and finishes at a strong 62% rim FG%, but his foul-drawing rate is functional rather than weaponized — the body type just doesn't generate the contact rate the elite contact-finishers in his peer group produce. Playmaking is functional rather than elite; vision and decision-making are both above the position threshold but capped by a documented "leaves plays on the table" pattern that surfaces both in the analytical record and in his head coach's framing of his ongoing development.

Defensively the trajectory is the headline story. The career baseline at point-of-attack was modest — 0.7 steals per game across his first four seasons — and 2025-26 produced a leap to 1.9 steals and 3.2 deflections per game with the head coach explicitly endorsing the improvement ("he's gotten way better at getting into the basketball"). The DFGPOE number is essentially neutral at +0.2%, which is the right read: the leap is in defensive playmaking and activity, not in lockdown one-on-one ability. The 6'2" frame structurally caps post defense and rim protection, and the gambling-prone profile means decisive drivers and pull-up shooters can still punish him. He'll never be a one-on-one stopper. He has become a meaningful disruptor, and at point guard that's enough.

Character is uniformly positive across the research record, with no negative signals from any source surveyed. The work ethic is documented and specific — he trains with Drew Hanlen (who also trains Embiid and Tatum), and the offseason work targets are stated and visible the following season ("I worked on my physicality this summer; I can't wait to show the world that"). The leadership emergence in 2025-26 is real: the demeanor change is multi-source (his own framing in SI, the standard-setting language, Embiid's "willing to learn humble," Lowry's external validation). He started both 2025 and 2026 All-Star Games, finished second in East All-Star voting in 2026, and authored the most 30-point/zero-turnover playoff games by a point guard in NBA history during the current Celtics series. Trajectory is unambiguously ascending; this is a leap year, and it has been verified across SI, PhillyVoice, Liberty Ballers, NBA.com, Boston Globe, and the Inquirer.

The bottom line: Maxey is a perennial-All-Star primary handler whose realistic ceiling sits in the Brunson/Mitchell/Booker neighborhood — All-NBA-second-team type at peak, never an MVP. Speed and shooting are the value drivers and they're both verified at peer-group-elite level. The ceiling is bounded by the half-court pull-up gap, the conservative-passing pattern that limits the lead-creator path, and the structural physical limits of the 6'2" frame on the defensive end. He is different from peer lead guards Brunson and Murray by virtue of speed and CAS volume; different from Curry and SGA by the half-court self-creation depth and scoring flexibility. The leap-year consolidation supports a small upward dial from prior evaluation; further movement requires either a half-court pull-up jump or a sustained playoff resume that goes beyond individual moments.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.

The OVR is not calculated from the attribute ratings or skill areas. It is assigned by comparing the player against a library of anchor players whose ratings have been calibrated and locked in.

That is why an OVR can sit higher than any individual skill area. The OVR measures the whole player against the league; the attribute ratings and skill areas show where specific strengths and weaknesses fall within position.
