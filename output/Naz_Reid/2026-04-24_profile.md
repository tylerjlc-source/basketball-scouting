## Section 3 — Scouting Narrative

Reid is a floor-spacing big who took the undrafted-to-Sixth-Man-of-the-Year path and now sits on a five-year, $125 million extension with the Timberwolves. At 6'10" with a 7'3 wingspan and quick feet for his size, he is the modern stretch-5 prototype: shoots it at real big-volume from distance, moves well enough to survive defensive switches, and pairs cleanly next to Rudy Gobert as a PF or steps into the 5-spot when Gobert sits. He is not an elite athlete, not a dominant rebounder, and not an offensive hub — but there are no obvious holes in the profile, which is unusual at his tier.

Offensively the 3-point shot is the headline: 37.8% on catch-and-shoot attempts on roughly four big-volume 3PA per game, with a healthy 40.3% from the corner and genuine off-dribble capability (34.3% on pull-up threes weighted, elevated to 41.2% in the prior healthy season). He finishes around the rim at 67.9% — better than most stretch bigs because his drive package is real at four attempts per game — and has enough post touch (hook shot, footwork out of both shoulders) to punish mismatches when the matchup calls for it. The handle is functional for his size; he can run pick-and-roll as a ball-handler in selected actions and operates as a secondary DHO hub rather than a primary creator. Weaknesses are foul-drawing (FTR 0.147 is thin for a big who gets into the paint), middling mid-range (34.1% weighted, confounded by a late-2025-26 shoulder injury that dragged his shot), and secondary-level playmaking (A:TO 1.47 — he converts his reads cleanly but the volume is not Vučević-level hub work). Touch and free-throw shooting are solid, not elite.

Defensively Reid is the rare stretch big with genuine switchability. Multiple sources, including head coach Chris Finch, describe him as a switch-one-through-five piece within the Wolves' scheme. Deflections (1.7/g) and blocks (1.0/g) are above the stretch-big baseline, and his on-ball DFGPOE (−1.5%) is respectable. The caveat is real: Gobert anchors the defense, and the numbers thin out in non-Gobert minutes. Rim protection (6.2) clears the C non-negotiable gate but leans on the Gobert-paired context; post defense (5.8) sits on a tiny 0.7-poss/g sample because Gobert absorbs post matchups. Reid is a functional team defender with switch capability — he is not an independent interior anchor.

Character and trajectory are a clean positive with one specific caveat. The undrafted-to-6MOTY-to-$125M path is a work-ethic profile most scouts would endorse; Finch has described him as a "fourth-quarter hero" in specific playoff moments (2024 Round 1 Game 4 vs Denver, 2025 Round 1 vs Lakers where he shot 9-for-11 from the field and 6-for-6 from three in fourth quarters). The caveat is regular-season clutch: across 57 games of last-five-minutes sample he shoots 26.8% from the field and 14.0% from three — a real statistical pattern of non-playoff-crunch-time shrinkage that the coach-trust framing doesn't fully capture. A late-season shoulder issue (left side January 2026, right side March 2026) dragged his shooting through the final 18 regular-season games and a small-sample early-playoff collapse (3 GP, 44.2% TS). R13 career-matched delta is +1.0 TS%, classifying as Moderate Rise given mixed-positive qualitative.

The bottom line: Reid is a clean-profile Stretch Big whose best comparison peer is Bobby Portis — same archetype, same rotation role, same 6MOTY credential, similar TS/USG/REB signature. The library had him at 7.65; re-evaluation places him at 7.75 to reflect the zero-liability distribution, the market validation, and the playoff rise signal. There is no cap binding, and the age curve still has room — if the Gobert-anchored role structure changes, Reid has the skill profile to step into a starter role. The ceiling is a one-time All-Star, not franchise player.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.

The OVR is not calculated from the attribute ratings or skill areas. It is assigned by comparing the player against a library of anchor players whose ratings have been calibrated and locked in.

That is why an OVR can sit higher than any individual skill area. The OVR measures the whole player against the league; the attribute ratings and skill areas show where specific strengths and weaknesses fall within position.
