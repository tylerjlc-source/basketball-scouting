## Section 3 — Scouting Narrative

Pascal Siakam at 31 is a Modern Four whose archetype — size-skill hybrid operating inside and out — still fits cleanly nine years after his rookie year. His 2025-26 on the Pacers is a study in individual excellence decoupled from team success: 24.0 points per game (career high), 6.6 rebounds, 3.8 assists, 57.7% true shooting weighted across the two-year window, while Indiana sits 15-40 at the All-Star break after Tyrese Haliburton's Achilles injury removed the team's offensive fulcrum. Siakam earned his fourth career All-Star selection — a second consecutive nod — on the strength of per-game production that reads as carrying-a-bad-team rather than co-starring on a good one.

Offensively the profile holds up and even expanded in volume. Rim conversion sits at 70.3% on 335 attempts — elite efficiency at the basket with a 68.9% non-dunk rim rate showing the touch that defines a craft finisher rather than a vertical athlete. The mid-range dropped to 36.1% (from 38.3% prior), which is the softest zone in the profile. Catch-and-shoot 3PT at 37.3% on real volume (305 attempts weighted) is credible, pull-up 3 is surprisingly strong at 38.2%, and the floater — 46.9% on 413 attempts making up 35.5% of his total FGA — is a signature weapon. Self-creation is solid (ISO PPP 0.971, 67th percentile) but not elite; he's more spot-operating than primary creator. Playmaking is the archetype's soft layer: 3.6 assists per game on a 1.94 A:TO ratio, adjusted assist rate 11.3%. Good enough for a wing #2 or #3 option, light for a team's primary.

Defensively the 2025-26 data shows regression from his Toronto peak. Overall opponent FG% of 47.9% and DFGPOE at +0.2% (neutral-slightly-negative — he's allowing basically what's expected of an average defender) move him well below his 2019 All-NBA 2nd Team level. ISO defense PPP 1.034 is below-average. His 2024-25 numbers were notably better (DRtg 108.9 vs 2025-26 117.2), suggesting the slide is recent rather than structural, but on film he is no longer a primary matchup shutdown. He is still an active contestant — 6.0 contested shots per game with his 7'3" wingspan — and rebounds with full engagement (OREB% 4.6%, DREB% 15.2% both strong for a wing). The defense grades now as "functional for a star, not a weapon."

Character and motor remain his identity credentials. The Haliburton-out 2025-26 year — carrying a losing team without quitting, career-high scoring on volume, no locker-room friction surfacing — is exactly the competitive-character read that propelled him through the 2019 championship run and his 2023-24 and 2024-25 Indiana deep playoff cycles. The 2019 Finals is far outside the current eval window, but the recency of the 2024-25 ECF and the 2025-26 All-Star selection confirm the reputation is live, not historical.

Bottom line: the drift-sweep hypothesis for Siakam was R13 Stage 2 rise candidacy on the 2024-25 ECF + 2019 championship credential stack. Under S99-F01 strict gate interpretation (captured this session), the gate does not fire — his career matched TS% delta is neutral at -2.6, not a statistical rise signal to converge with the qualitative. The credential-driven placement at 8.70 reflects sustained two-year All-Star cadence and playoff contribution, partially offset by defensive regression and bad-team context in 2025-26. Net drift from 8.66 is small (+0.04) — this is a confirmation more than a correction.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.

The OVR is not calculated from the attribute ratings or skill areas. It is assigned by comparing the player against a library of anchor players whose ratings have been calibrated and locked in.

That is why an OVR can sit higher than any individual skill area. The OVR measures the whole player against the league; the attribute ratings and skill areas show where specific strengths and weaknesses fall within position.
