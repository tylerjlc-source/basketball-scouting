## Section 3 — Scouting Narrative

Shai Gilgeous-Alexander is the reigning MVP and Finals MVP, a 6'6" lead guard whose 2024-25 championship season with Oklahoma City stands as one of the signature single-season performances of the past decade. He repeated the production into 2025-26 — 31.1 points on 55.3% shooting, 6.6 assists, 4.3 rebounds, 65.4% true shooting weighted across both seasons — and earned the 2025-26 Clutch Player of the Year (150+ clutch points league-leading) plus a third straight MVP-finalist spot. The profile is a primary creator at the absolute top of the league, with defense that is genuinely functional and character that held a championship together.

Offensively he operates at four elite layers. Self-creation is the foundation: ISO PPP 1.138 (91st percentile) and pick-and-roll ball-handler PPP 1.161 (95th percentile) put him among the most efficient shot-creators the tracking era has measured. Finishing at the rim is clinical — 70.5% weighted rim conversion, 68.3% on non-dunk attempts, 88.7% from the line at elite volume (44% FTR, drawing contact almost at a rate of 1 foul per 2 shots). Shot-making from every zone is mature: 53.0% mid-range on 352 FGA volume is the signature weapon, pull-up 3PT at 36.9% is legitimately dangerous off the dribble, catch-and-shoot 3PT 42.7% is elite, and the floater (53.0% on 529 FGA) rounds out a scoring profile that demands defensive respect everywhere. Playmaking is above-average rather than elite — 20.0% adjusted assist rate, 6.5 APG, 2.85 A:TO — the ceiling of what a primary offensive engine needs while not quite reaching pure point guard vision.

Defensively he plays to his tools honestly. DFGPOE of -3.6% (opponent FG% 43.0% as primary defender) and 1.5 steals per game indicate he takes primary-defender assignments seriously, though post and rim-protection duties fall outside the archetype by design. For a primary offensive engine, "functional on defense" is already ahead of the Curry/Dame baseline — he is not a defensive liability. Lateral quickness is above-average and the length differential lets him contest closely without fouling at high rate (2.1 PF/g).

Character is where the profile locks in at era-defining level. Clutch Player of the Year 2025-26 with 49-game clutch sample showing 49.9% FG and +2.6 on-court plus-minus. Championship pressure produced 23-game playoff run at 29.9 PPG and 57.4% TS last season, closing the Finals as Finals MVP. Competitive reputation through the 2024-25 run — measured in interviews and peer testimony — is "relentless professional" across multiple independent sources. Work ethic is the baseline of the franchise's identity now.

Bottom line: this is a legitimate Tier 2 player at current level. Prior library placement of 9.46 (Tier 3 middle) predates the MVP + Finals MVP + championship triple-credential from 2024-25 and the continued MVP-finalist / Clutch POY trajectory in 2025-26. The Finals MVP credential is the textbook Stage 2 Defining trigger per R13; even by the conservative reading that requires strict TS%/qualitative convergence, the anchor-comparison case alone pulls him across the Tier 2 floor (9.50+) based on where he sits relative to Jokic (9.58) at current level.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.

The OVR is not calculated from the attribute ratings or skill areas. It is assigned by comparing the player against a library of anchor players whose ratings have been calibrated and locked in.

That is why an OVR can sit higher than any individual skill area. The OVR measures the whole player against the league; the attribute ratings and skill areas show where specific strengths and weaknesses fall within position.
