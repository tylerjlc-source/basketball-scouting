## Section 3 — Scouting Narrative

Karl-Anthony Towns is a 6'11" / 7'3" shooting big — the canonical Stretch Big the modern NBA was built to accommodate. The first thing you notice is the catch-and-shoot stroke from a frame this size: quick release, barely-leaves-the-floor mechanics, line-drive accuracy on real volume. He has built out a 271-pound interior body to go with the perimeter touch, which is the unicorn combination that makes him valuable. Few players in NBA history have shot from this size with this consistency at the line and from three.

Offensively he is a multi-area scorer anchored by elite shooting. The catch-and-shoot 3 is the value driver — career mid-30s percentages with stretches of 40%+ that pull centers thirty feet from the rim and reshape his team's spacing. He shoots 84% from the free-throw line, which is a separation-from-his-position skill: Hack-a strategies do not exist against him. Inside the arc he finishes at 64% at the rim on functional volume, drives at over a point per attempt, and operates a real post game with hooks, fades, and turnaround jumpers. His handle is good enough to attack closeouts and make plays from the high post — he averages 33+ passes per game with growing facilitator usage, and notched two triple-doubles in the 2026 first round. The drag is on the off-the-dribble pull-up, where his pull-up 3 has historically been low-percentage, and on the mid-range, where the actual conversion (34%) trails the reputation. Late double-teams remain a known failure mode — scouting reports across two seasons describe him losing the ball to deflected passes and offensive fouls.

Defense is the team-managed liability. He is heavy-footed laterally and gets hunted in pick-and-roll — Indiana put him in switch coverages on Haliburton in the 2024-25 ECF and got open driving lanes by his staying back. His rim-protection numbers in 2024-25 were among the worst posted by a starting center — opposing FG% at the rim spiked when he was the primary defender, and the on/off team defensive rating gap was real. The 2025-26 season showed meaningful improvement (best D-LEBRON of his career) under a simplified Mike Brown role, but the multi-year pattern still anchors. He clears the C non-negotiables (rim protection at the threshold, defensive rebounding well above, strength elite), which keeps him on the floor — but the defensive band is the band that caps his ceiling. He does anchor the defensive glass at an elite rate (28% DREB%, 9+ DREB/G), and he competes physically on rebounds even if not on shot contests.

Character and motor are mixed signals. He is durable in the recent window — 75 and 72 games the past two seasons, heavy minutes under Thibodeau without complaint. He took accountability publicly when Charles Barkley confronted him about foul trouble in the 2024-25 ECF. But the criticism has been multi-source and persistent: Shaquille O'Neal called him soft, Brian Windhorst reported a Mike Brown rift early in 2025-26, and the foul rate (3+ per game in nine of ten seasons) is structural, not situational. The 2025-26 first round versus Atlanta showed the upside — two triple-doubles, franchise-record 140-89 elimination win, dominant TS% — but the broader playoff record across six seasons trends as a moderate efficiency dip from his regular-season baseline. He is high-variance: capable of takeover quarters and disappearing through fouls in the same series.

Bottom line: a top-30 NBA player whose primary value is positional unicorn shooting at center size, paired with elite rebounding, real interior scoring, and a defensive profile his team accepts as the cost of doing business. The Stretch Big archetype fits because shooting is the identity — but the post game, rebounding, and emerging playmaking make Versatile Scoring Big and All-Around Big the close runners-up. Tier 6 placement reflects the All-Star ceiling capped by defensive band and high-variance playoff record.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.

The OVR is not calculated from the attribute ratings or skill areas. It is assigned by comparing the player against a library of anchor players whose ratings have been calibrated and locked in.

That is why an OVR can sit higher than any individual skill area. The OVR measures the whole player against the league; the attribute ratings and skill areas show where specific strengths and weaknesses fall within position.
