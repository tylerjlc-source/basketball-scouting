## Section 3 — Scouting Narrative

Fifteen years into his career, Vučević is a skilled, declining big whose value has always come from the neck up. He is a 6'11" / 260 lb traditional center with soft hands, a veteran's feel, and a hub-passing instinct that is rare for his position. He is not an athlete. He has never been above the rim, and at 35 the mobility and explosion that were already modest are now structural limits. What he does — and still does well — is process the game from the elbows, make passes that keep offense moving, and convert the shots his craft produces.

Offensively he operates from three zones. The mid-range and floater zone is where most of his shots live — a 37.8% slice of his attempts come from 4–14 feet, and he converts them at 53.6% on a signature turnaround hook and a variety of touch finishes from either shoulder. The 3-point line is a real weapon: 38.4% on catch-and-shoot over the evaluation window, 40.2% in 2024-25, on real big-volume attempts. The post is where his craft shows up most clearly — footwork, both-shoulder counters, and passing out of post touches when doubles come. As a hub, he ranks near the top of all centers in secondary assists, runs dribble-handoff actions as a primary offensive initiator, and sets the kind of screens that opposing coaches game-plan around. The gap is contact: at 0.127 FTR he almost never draws fouls, and the finesse identity that makes everything else work also puts a hard ceiling on how many free points he generates.

Defensively he is a liability the team accepts. He can only play drop coverage. He cannot switch, cannot hedge aggressively, and cannot contest at the rim at an NBA-starter level — 0.6 blocks per game is well below any functional center bar, and opponents convert 56% at the rim when he is the closest defender. Post defense is the one dimension where small-sample numbers look acceptable, but the volume is too thin to claim a strength. Help rotations are cognitive rather than athletic — he knows where to be, but the recovery to contest after he gets there is gone. The defensive rebounding is the exception that anchors the floor: 21.8% DREB rate and consistent 10-board production keep the defensive profile from collapsing entirely.

Character and trajectory read cleanly but downward. Multi-source career framing is professional, durable (five consecutive 70-plus-GP seasons pre-2025-26), and respected; more recent Bulls-era coverage noted up-and-down effort during losing stretches, which the trade to Boston and Mazzulla's "he just wants to win" framing has partially rebalanced. The 2024-25 career-year numbers (23.4 / 11.7 / 3.8 at 48/40/84) are almost certainly the peak that has passed. Current production is lower, usage is lower in the bench role, the 3-point percentage has slipped, and a minor ring-finger surgery in March 2026 cost him 14 games. No convergent playoff-rise or playoff-shrink narrative across his five qualifying runs — he is what he is in the regular season and in the playoffs.

The bottom line: Vučević is a rare archetype fit — confirmed Versatile Scoring Big alongside Cousins and Aldridge — whose skill floor is genuinely high and whose defensive floor is genuinely low. The composite sits in Tier 8 because the C-gate non-negotiable cap on rim protection binds the ceiling, but the 14 strengths spread across six domains give him more to offer than most role players at that composite. What remains is a veteran-rotation arc: a stretch / hub / post contributor who needs to be protected at the defensive end by scheme and by minutes management.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.

The OVR is not calculated from the attribute ratings or skill areas. It is assigned by comparing the player against a library of anchor players whose ratings have been calibrated and locked in.

That is why an OVR can sit higher than any individual skill area. The OVR measures the whole player against the league; the attribute ratings and skill areas show where specific strengths and weaknesses fall within position.
