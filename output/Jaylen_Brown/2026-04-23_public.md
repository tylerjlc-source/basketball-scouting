# Jaylen Brown — Public Profile

## Identity

Jaylen Brown is a Score-First Wing in his 10th NBA season, a 7-time All-Star with a 2024 Finals MVP and championship to his name. At 6'6", 223 lbs, with a 6'11.75" wingspan, he combines elite wing strength with the length to defend across multiple positions. With Jayson Tatum out for most of 2025-26 recovering from injury, Brown has carried Boston's offensive load at a career-high level. He averaged 29.3 points per game at the All-Star break, up from 22.2 the prior year, and earned All-Star starter honors for the second straight year.

## Strength

Brown is a driver-first scorer. He gets to the rim 16.5 times per game, converting 52.8% from inside the arc on those drives, and finishes 68.3% at the basket on legitimate volume. His 13% dunk rate reflects real above-rim finishing for a wing his size. Off the bounce he is comfortable creating his own shot from mid-range (41.0% on 284 attempts) and the pull-up zone, and his playmaking has improved alongside the scoring expansion: 17.3% adjusted assist rate and 4.9 assists per game. Defensively he remains a functional wing stopper. He holds opponents 2.6% below their expected field-goal percentage, contests 4.5 shots a game, and his combination of strength and lateral quickness lets him switch from point guards to power forwards.

## Weakness

The perimeter shooting is the softest part of the profile. His catch-and-shoot 3-point conversion has dropped to 34.1%, well off the 38-39% peaks he held during the 2022-24 title-contending years, and his pull-up 3 sits at 32.9%. Ball security is a real cost of the higher usage. He turned the ball over 220 times in 71 games, with an assist-to-turnover ratio of 1.52 that is weak for a player operating as a primary handler. Defensively he is functional but not at an All-Defensive level. The team defense around him has slipped, and his individual ceiling on that end has settled at very-good rather than elite.

## Projection-verdict

Brown is a perennial All-Star whose level sits below the league's MVP candidates and above the back end of All-Star rotations. The ceiling is a first All-NBA selection and another deep playoff run, in the territory of Kawhi Leonard at his peak, if Tatum returns healthy and Boston's roster regenerates around the pair. The floor is an age-decline arc, where his shooting recovery never comes and his defensive role contracts. What he is, year over year, is a winning Score-First Wing with championship credentials and the physical tools to keep producing into his 30s. What he is not, and is unlikely to become, is the lead engine of an offense or an All-Defensive selection.
