## Section 3 — Scouting Narrative

Jaylen Brown at 29 is operating in a different context from his 2024 Finals MVP peak. With Jayson Tatum recovering from injury through most of 2025-26, Brown has carried Boston's offensive load at career-high volume — 29.3 PPG at the All-Star break (up from 22.2 PPG the prior season), seventh consecutive All-Star selection, starter honors for a second straight year. The profile is classic Score-First Wing: volume three-level scoring with 7'0" wingspan enabling real defensive functionality, playmaking that is adequate rather than elite, and a competitive reputation credentialed by the 2024 championship run.

Offensively Brown is a driver-first athlete. 16.5 drives per game at 52.8% FG converts elite drive volume into real production; rim conversion sits at 68.3% on 330 FGA weighted; dunk rate 13% reflects legitimate above-rim finishing. The perimeter shooting is the soft spot of the profile — catch-and-shoot 3PT has dropped to 34.1% (from the 38-39% peaks of his 2022-24 title-contending years), pull-up 3 sits at 32.9%, mid-range 41.0% is functional not elite. True shooting 56.6% weighted at 32% usage is a clean "score-first but not Curry-efficient" profile. Playmaking has improved with the scoring expansion — 17.3% adjusted assist rate, 4.9 APG — but A:TO ratio of 1.52 and TOV/100 touches at 5.0 flag the cost of higher volume; he turns the ball over more when decisions speed up.

Defensively he remains a functional wing stopper, though not at All-Defensive level. DFGPOE at -2.6% and 4.5 contested shots per game indicate genuine contest quality. Lateral quickness and strength combination lets him switch across 1-4; post defense at 1.044 PPP is competent. Team defense on-court (DRtg 112.5) has slipped from Celtics-peak numbers, consistent with Tatum's absence reducing overall lineup quality. Brown's individual defense is holding; the system around him is weaker.

Character and motor are anchored by the 2024 championship run and Finals MVP performance, though that credential is now outside the two-season evaluation window (2023-24 playoff cycle vs the current 2024-25 / 2025-26 window). Within recency: 2024-25 saw Celtics fall in 2nd round (11 playoff games, slight TS% drag) and 2025-26 is ongoing in the 1st round vs Philadelphia. The 29.3 PPG scoring surge is the narrative of the season — taking ownership of the team through injury adversity is a real competitive-character signal.

Bottom line: the drift-sweep hypothesis was R13 Stage 2 rise candidacy on the 2024 FMVP + 2024-25 playoff run. Under S99-F01 strict interpretation, the FMVP was already priced into the prior 8.79 anchor placement via anchor comparison — firing Stage 2 on top would double-count the same credential. The actual drift is the 2025-26 scoring-surge + continued All-Star starter trajectory, driving a modest upward correction from 8.79 to 8.83. FMVP credential remains the ceiling anchor; lack of new All-NBA in the eval window is the drag.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.

The OVR is not calculated from the attribute ratings or skill areas. It is assigned by comparing the player against a library of anchor players whose ratings have been calibrated and locked in.

That is why an OVR can sit higher than any individual skill area. The OVR measures the whole player against the league; the attribute ratings and skill areas show where specific strengths and weaknesses fall within position.
