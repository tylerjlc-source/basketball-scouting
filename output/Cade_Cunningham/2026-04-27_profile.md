## Section 3 — Scouting Narrative

A wing-sized lead guard whose value is the playmaking, not the scoring. At 6'6" with a 7'0" wingspan and 220 pounds of sturdy frame, Cunningham looks across the floor over defenders other primary guards have to maneuver around — and he uses that vantage to read coverages a half-beat earlier. The first thing visible is the size; the second is that he runs the entire offense from a controlled tempo, sees the second and third pass before the defense rotates, and plays with the unhurried patience of a primary creator who knows the play is coming back to him.

The offensive engine is built around pick-and-roll mastery and self-creation craft, not athleticism. He manipulates ball-screen coverages, throws cross-court skip passes with pinpoint accuracy, and gets to his spots through a hesitation handle and a long stride rather than a top-end first step. The signature shot is a step-back self-created jumper from the mid-range and pull-up zones — the floater off the four-to-fourteen-foot range is a primary touch shot at high volume. Free throws are reliable. The mid-range is opportunistic — taken when defenses give it, converted at the rate that makes them keep giving. The shooting profile has one real soft spot: the catch-and-shoot three-point percentage dipped this season from a strong prior-year peak, on real volume. The mechanics are intact (the eighty-four-percent career free-throw rate confirms the touch), but the current sample is below the position's expected standard. At the rim, he converts above sixty percent on heavy attempts — improving every year despite the limited vertical — by leveraging size, touch, and a body strong enough to absorb contact at the gather.

Defensively, he plays like an oversized wing helper rather than a true point-of-attack stopper. Against guards he is functional, not feared — the lateral quickness is below the position's average and the foul rate ticks up as he uses his hands to compensate. But the size, length, and timing show up as real positives in help: he chases shooters around screens, contests well in ball-screen coverage, and the team's defensive rating is meaningfully better with him on the floor. He blocks shots in transition, secures defensive boards at wing-sized rates for a primary guard, and absorbs switches against wings adequately. The defensive ceiling is genuine team-defender capability with structural caps on rim protection and on-ball stopping that no amount of effort breaks through.

Character is the upward modifier on the profile. The nickname is Motor, the demeanor is even-keeled, and he was voted first in the East by his fellow players for the 2026 All-Star starter — the strongest available peer-respect signal. The 2026 playoff sample has shown the maturation directly: a thirty-nine-point Game 1 effort in defeat, a clutch closing run from down seventeen in Game 3, and a series-shaping leadership presence on the top-seeded team in the Eastern Conference. The prior playoff struggle in 2024-25 against length is documented and real — the career playoff true-shooting delta sits below the regular-season baseline by a moderate margin — but the active sample is course-correcting that signal in real time. The trajectory is up across nearly every measurable indicator: assist-to-turnover, rim conversion, mid-range, free-throw rate, and team defensive impact have all improved year over year.

The bottom line is a clear All-NBA-caliber primary creator whose ceiling is set by athletic limitations and whose floor is held up by character and basketball IQ. He is a scaled-down Luka in archetype shape — same physical mismatch profile, same vision-first creation engine, lower peaks across most individual skills. He projects as a perennial All-Star with All-NBA First Team upside if the playoff résumé continues to convert. The structural caps on burst and lateral quickness mean he will not climb into the Tier 3 elite without sustained playoff dominance and a reversion of the catch-and-shoot to the prior peak.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.

The OVR is not calculated from the attribute ratings or skill areas. It is assigned by comparing the player against a library of anchor players whose ratings have been calibrated and locked in.

That is why an OVR can sit higher than any individual skill area. The OVR measures the whole player against the league; the attribute ratings and skill areas show where specific strengths and weaknesses fall within position.
