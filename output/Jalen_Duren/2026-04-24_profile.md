## Section 3 — Scouting Narrative

Duren is the modern Energy Big with the physical profile of a Detroit interior monster — 6'11", 250 pounds, a 7'5.25" wingspan, and the kind of vertical explosion that tape rooms describe as the quickest second jump in his draft class. The first thing the eye finds is the contest: he high-points blocks at peak rather than swiping at them, finishes lobs through the rim rather than off it, and rebounds in traffic with a leaping pattern that lets him fire a second jump before bigger defenders have come back down. He is not a stretch big. He is not a switch big. He is the rim-running, rebound-anchoring, paint-pressuring identity center, and in his fourth season he has graduated from athlete-with-promise to first-team All-Star anchoring a top-three NBA defense.

Offensively the value driver is interior conversion at All-Star volume — 19.5 points on 65 percent shooting, 73 percent at the rim on roughly 500 attempts, and a free-throw rate near .500 that puts him at the line as often as most lead guards. The 2025-26 step forward is the self-creation that has been layered onto the catch-and-finish identity: rim attempts per 100 possessions jumped roughly 35 percent year over year, drives are up to nearly three a game, and the handle has tightened enough that isolation possessions are producing in the eighty-second percentile and pick-and-roll ball-handler reps are landing in the ninetieth. The mid-post additions are real but still developing — counters exist, the touch is genuine (50 percent on 184 floaters, 60 percent on non-dunk rim attempts), but the post is not yet a hub. Off the ball is where the offense lives: cut possessions producing 1.39 points per chance on heavy volume, transition reps in the elite tier across both seasons, and a rolling-and-sealing pattern that consistently arrives on time. The shooting absence is structural and total — zero three-point attempts across the two-season window. The clutch free throw rate (66 percent) is the one mechanical concern under pressure, and the assist-to-turnover ratio (1.25) and 60 percent assist-conversion rate cap his playmaking at functional rather than elite. The hub-creation flashes during Cade Cunningham's mid-season absence (3.2 assists per game over a six-game stretch at 22 points and 65 percent) are the trajectory signal: developing, not arrived.

Defensively the fourth-year leap is the defining story. Individual defensive rating dropped from 114 to 108, the Pistons rose to top-three team defense with Duren as the primary big anchor, and rim deterrence holds opponents to roughly 55 percent at the basket — top-of-league territory. The block volume (0.9 per game) understates the real impact: more than 75 percent of his blocks come away from his direct assignment, signaling help-side anticipation and verticality more than chasing reach-ins. Post defense produces 0.69 points per possession across two seasons of consistent ninetieth-percentile equivalent play, and isolation defense is similarly stingy on a thin sample. The ceiling on the defensive profile is lateral. He does not move his feet well in space, plays drop coverage too high more often than the scheme would prefer, and gets exploited by stretch fives and quicker pick-and-roll handlers who can attack the gap. Foul rate (3.0 per game) is the discipline tell. Switchability onto the perimeter is not part of the role and never will be — but he does not need it. The defensive identity is interior anchor, post defender, and weak-side rim deterrent, and at all three he plays at a clear above-average level.

Character and trajectory are both positives. Coach quotes reference his sameness — same approach, same attitude, same work — and his offseason regimen with Cade Cunningham (Italy, Texas, scheduled training camp prep) reads as a structured veteran-mentorship pattern, not a publicity tour. The work shows up on the floor: trimmed body fat, expanded mid-post repertoire, tighter handle, improved free-throw mechanics (67 percent rookie year through 75 percent current). The 2026 All-Star reserve selection at age 22 is the credential that confirms the leap. The 2026 first-round Game 1 against Orlando produced a passive eight-point, four-attempt performance that surfaces the recurring concern about pressure-decision quality — but the Game 2 bounceback (11 points, 9 rebounds, 4 assists in a series-leveling win) reads as on-the-job adjustment from a young All-Star, not a character signal. The injury history is clean (90 percent availability across the two-season window) and the trajectory is genuinely ascending, not plateau.

The bottom line: Duren is the league's clearest current example of a top-end Energy Big with All-Around skill emerging underneath. The base case is sustained All-Star production as a finishing-rebounding-anchor center with growing playmaking responsibility. The ceiling case is evolution into an All-Around Big in the Bam-lite mold — defensive anchor with offensive hub responsibility — though the spacing structural zero (zero three-point attempts) and the lateral cap make the All-Around tier a ceiling, not the median. The floor case is plateau into a high-end Energy Big like prime Andre Drummond — multi-All-Star, multi-rebounding-champion territory. Either path produces real NBA value at the position; the trajectory says the median outcome is closer to the upper end.
