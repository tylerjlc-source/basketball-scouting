## Section 3 — Scouting Narrative

Cooper Flagg is a 19-year-old 6'7.75" two-way wing whose rookie season ended with the ROY trophy and a 26-56 team record. The headline trait is competitive identity — a relentless motor, vocal leadership on a young Mavericks roster missing Kyrie Irving and Anthony Davis for most of the year, and a willingness to absorb the entire offensive burden when Dallas's roster construction collapsed around him. He led all NBA rookies in drives per game, was the second player since 1974 to lead his team in points, rebounds, assists, and steals across a full season, and posted a 51-point game (the first teen in NBA history to crack 50) and a 45-point game inside the season's final two weeks. The frame is wing-sized rather than power-forward-sized, the wingspan is positionally functional rather than positionally dominant, and the athletic profile is balanced and skill-leveraged rather than a wow-tier physical separator.

Offensively, Flagg's value runs through driving volume and elite playmaking rather than through any reliable jump shot. He attacks the rim at All-Star-level frequency, finishes through length with both hands, and converts at the basket above the wing baseline. The floater is the second offensive engine — high volume (38.4% of total field goal attempts) at a respectable conversion rate, generating in-between offense when defenses shrink the paint. Both the playmaking signature and the touch make the second story of this offense: 290 total assists led the rookie class, the read progression is described as quarterback-style, and skip passes against rotations are a real weapon. The load-bearing weakness is shooting. Catch-and-shoot percentages cratered through the early and middle of the year (25% on 64 attempts through mid-January) before a late-season uptick (43.6% from December 21 onward) suggested mechanical functionality with a hesitation problem; the full-season aggregate landed at 28.2% on 244 attempts. The Mavericks ranked 28th in offense in part because Flagg's spacing did not threaten anyone, and the heavy floater volume reflects the shot diet of a primary creator who could not access the most-efficient shot types — clean catch-and-shoot threes — at scale.

Defensively, the profile is help-driven rather than on-ball-dominant. Flagg's weakside swat radius, passing-lane disruption, and rotational IQ produce real two-way value — 0.9 blocks, 1.2 steals, 2.2 deflections, and 6.8 contested shots per game on a roster that was never built for defensive identity. The on-ball matchup language ("mirrors the ball well," "can defend 1 through 5") is real but cap-bound at the wing level by adequate-not-elite lateral quickness; against the league's top primary creators, the lateral structure shows up. Rebounding contributes meaningfully — 6.7 boards per game with a defensive-rebound rate that ranks in the wing top quartile — driven by motor and instinct more than by frame leverage at this stage. Post defense is not yet a notable assignment area at his current weight.

The character, work ethic, and competitive trajectory are the highest-confidence elements of the entire profile. Multi-source convergence on competitiveness language is unusual — West GMs, opposing scouts, peer All-NBA players, and his personal trainer all use the same framing without prompting. Through a season that included home crowd booing, a mid-season GM firing, an Anthony Davis salary-dump trade, and the worst-in-league offensive system, Flagg never disengaged. Late-season production climbed materially (career-high outputs, ROY winner by narrow margin over Knueppel) — the trajectory points up, and the ceiling is gated by skill development rather than by character-or-effort risk.

The bottom line is that this is a high-floor primary-creator wing with All-NBA-tier upside if the catch-and-shoot three and the handle compound into archetype-typical levels. The shooting cluster is the load-bearing variable, and the projection band is wide because the realistic outcomes range from "valuable primary defender / playmaker on a contender" to "two-way All-Star wing in the Banchero–Tatum mold." Shooting development is the path between those outcomes.
