## Section 3 — Scouting Narrative

Jalen Williams is a 6'5" two-way guard with the wingspan of a small-ball four — a 7'2" spread makes him one of the longest players in recent history relative to his height. He plays as the secondary creator and primary defensive stopper for a championship Oklahoma City roster, and the combination defines him: his floor is what he takes away on defense, his ceiling is what he adds as a co-star scorer behind Shai Gilgeous-Alexander. The first thing a viewer notices is the reach — at the rim contesting, in passing lanes, at the point of attack closing space faster than his feet should allow.

Offensively, he operates as a pick-and-roll ball handler whose read against hedging coverages is essentially automatic — roller, shooter, or refused pullup — and whose self-creation lives in two places: at the rim, where he converts 66% on solid volume, and in the mid-range, where his healthy 2024-25 pull-up work was a legitimate weapon. The pull-up three is the soft spot; it sits in the high 20s and drags his off-dribble efficiency into the "solid" range when the broader profile earns "above average." Catch-and-shoot production (38%) is credible. Touch shows up everywhere — floaters, cutting finishes, non-dunk rim work — and his ball security for a primary ball handler (drive turnover rate under 6%) is a real asset. In the half-court, he creates advantages without requiring the ball; his cut efficiency sits in the 80th-percentile range of play types.

Defensively, he is a first-team-caliber stopper with a second-team credential. During the 2024-25 Western Conference Finals he took the bulk of the Anthony Edwards and Julius Randle assignments, and his DFGPOE (-2.6%) and contested-shot volume (3.6 per game) back the credential beyond the All-Defensive Team vote. The 7'2" wingspan is the enabler — he can stay with quicker guards laterally and still contest shots they think are open because his reach rewrites contest distance. He switches comfortably onto wings, holds up briefly in post switches against 4s, and his length generates help-side blocks and deflections at rates unusual for a guard. Defensive rebounding sits at 12.7%, elite for a 6'5" player, an honest byproduct of length and engagement.

Character is the sub-domain that carries the profile upward. His 2024-25 Finals run is a single-season credential that few NBA players produce in full careers: 40 points on 14-for-25 in Game 5 to take a 3-2 series lead, three straight Finals games with 25+ points and 5+ rebounds (company nobody under 25 had joined since Shaquille O'Neal in 1995), and a 20/4/4/2 Game 7 performance that closed OKC's first championship. "Really gutsy" is the recurring scouting language — the team-behind-him quality that coaches name when they describe a player who rises in the moments when the margin narrows. The motor matches it: championship-level engagement across both ends, pro-level work ethic surfacing in every rehab recovery story, no character flags to manage.

Bottom line: this is a legitimate Tier 5 star pulled into Tier 7 by library drift. The 2024-25 credential stack — All-NBA Third Team, All-Defensive Second Team, All-Star reserve, NBA champion, Finals Game 5 hero — places him among Jrue Holiday, Jamal Murray, and Pascal Siakam in the championship-co-star-with-elite-defense neighborhood. The 2025-26 injury year (49 games missed) is a trajectory flag but not a ceiling cap; acute, not chronic. At 25, the runway is long and the floor is firmly established.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.

The OVR is not calculated from the attribute ratings or skill areas. It is assigned by comparing the player against a library of anchor players whose ratings have been calibrated and locked in.

That is why an OVR can sit higher than any individual skill area. The OVR measures the whole player against the league; the attribute ratings and skill areas show where specific strengths and weaknesses fall within position.
