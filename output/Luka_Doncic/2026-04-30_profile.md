## Section 3 — Scouting Narrative

**Identity.** A 6'7" 244-pound primary creator who runs offense like a point guard and bullies the post like a wing. The first thing you notice is the deceleration — he doesn't beat defenders by exploding past them, he beats them by suddenly being slower than them, then suddenly faster again. Eccentric-force testing has him in the 92nd-93rd percentile of NBA athletes for precisely this trait, which is why nothing about his game looks fast and everything about his game generates separation. He is the canonical Jumbo Playmaker — playmaking at size, with a physical mismatch as the product. He fits next to LeBron and Magic on a list of three confirmed Jumbo Playmaker fits.

**Offensive profile.** This is a top-five offensive engine in the league. He gets to the rim 14.5 times a game and converts 58% on those drives, lives at the line at 11.8 free throws a game leading the NBA, and shoots 36% on a step-back three taken with the same release-point variation that makes the shot impossible to scout. The handle is a top-10 active guard handle and the touch is a 51% floater on 400 attempts a year — the volume and efficiency on the floater alone is generational. The vision is genuinely elite: he creates 13.5 potential assists a night, processes the floor like a point guard, and threads lobs and skips that only a handful of passers in the league can complete. He bullies smaller guards in the post with one-foot fadeaways and uses post entries as a half-court weapon, which most guards can't do. The honest gap is that the shot diet runs hot — he routinely takes wild one-legged threes early in the shot clock because his coaching staff has given him the green light, and his teammates have to talk him off the hero-shot pattern in late-clock situations. The efficiency holds anyway (60.4% TS on 36.8% usage is MVP-tier) because his shot-making compensates for the selection critique. Ball security is the soft spot — 4 turnovers a game with documented vulnerability under perimeter pressure, including a single game with nine live-ball giveaways against the Suns. Off-ball movement is structurally absent. He is a primary creator and the team designs movement actions for everyone else.

**Defensive profile.** This is the gap. He is below-average laterally, doesn't have first-step burst, and the multi-source critique — "lack of lateral quickness, poor foot positioning, inconsistent effort on closeouts" — is durable across both the Mavericks and Lakers eras. The 2025 first-round elimination game against Minnesota featured nine blow-bys on sixteen defended drives, the worst single-game number by any player in the entire 2025 postseason. The 2025-26 conditioning reset has produced real on-ball containment improvement — Lakers data shows him as the team's lowest points-per-possession allowed in switch — but the Lakers still finished 20th in defensive rating. Active hands are a genuine asset (3.2 deflections, 1.7 steals a game) and the size profile makes him a non-liability on switches against wings. Defensive rebounding is the single defensive elite trait — 19.6% DREB rate is forward-tier, generationally rare for a guard, and feeds his grab-and-go transition initiation. He is not a rim protector and never will be. The archetype excuses interior defense; the perimeter defense itself is bottom-tier and a real composite drag.

**Character and trajectory.** The character read is bifurcated and recent. Through 2024-25, the dominant narrative was conditioning concerns and lower-body durability — fourth calf strain in eighteen months by Christmas 2024, which Nico Harrison cited as the trade-driving factor along with "off-court habits" in his pitch to Mavericks ownership. Through 2025-26, the narrative is a documented offseason reset with multi-source coverage of the diet and training discipline, an ascending efficiency season with a career-best TS%, and an MVP-finalist-tier production line cut short by a Grade 2 hamstring strain on April 2 that ended the regular season at 64 games — one short of the awards-eligibility threshold. He is currently in Madrid receiving stem-cell treatment and will miss the first round of the 2026 playoffs. The motor narrative is the most stable critique across both eras — selective effort in transition defense, ref engagement that occasionally costs the team possessions, a tech foul pattern that has run 15-plus per season since 2021. The competitive character is real but flagged: two career game-winners in playoff games, clutch FT% 84.4%, the 2024 Finals run with Game 1 (30/10) and Game 2 (32/11/11) signature performances. Lower-leg durability — calf 2023-2024, hamstring 2025-2026 — is a long-term floor concern that one offseason of discipline does not yet retire.

**Bottom line.** Tier 4 middle. He is between Hali (cleaner cognitive, dormant Achilles) at the top of the band and Cade (same archetype, less elite ceiling) at the bottom. The 16-strength profile is the densest in active Tier 4 with seven sub-domains at 9.0-plus, but the eight-sub-domain defensive cluster at 5.0-5.5 plus the absent MVP credential plus the durability cluster prevent Tier 3 entry where Curry sits. The path to Tier 3 runs through sustained durability across multiple offseasons and a deep playoff run with the Lakers that ratifies the 2024 Finals signal as a multi-cycle pattern rather than a one-off.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.

The OVR is not calculated from the attribute ratings or skill areas. It is assigned by comparing the player against a library of anchor players whose ratings have been calibrated and locked in.

That is why an OVR can sit higher than any individual skill area. The OVR measures the whole player against the league; the attribute ratings and skill areas show where specific strengths and weaknesses fall within position.
