## 3. Scouting Narrative

Avdija is a 6'8" wing creator who graduated to primary ball-handler in his sixth NBA season and rewarded the trust with the most efficient scoring + playmaking line of his career — 24.2 points, 6.9 rebounds, 6.7 assists, on 60% true shooting and 26% usage. He earned his first All-Star selection, finished third in Most Improved Player voting, and led the league in assists generated off the drive. The first thing the eye picks up is the size-skill mismatch: a wing-sized primary playing point forward, throwing left-handed alley-oops one possession and cleaning up his own offensive rebound the next.

The offensive engine is downhill drive volume with foul-pressure secondary. He drives 16 times a game, converts 53% inside the arc on those drives, and gets to the line 9.2 times — only Doncic averages more this season. He pairs that drive volume with above-average catch-and-shoot 3PT (36% on real volume), reliable mid-range pull-up at 49%, and a touch package of floaters and runners that has improved markedly each season. The gap is the off-dribble 3, where he sits at 28% on pull-ups across both eval-window seasons — the structural shooting limitation that defines the within-archetype ceiling. San Antonio's first-round series exposed a related limitation: when defenses force him left, the right-side handle-and-finish package loses its bite, and the foul-drawing flood that defines his regular-season offense slows. The TOV volume is the other counter-signal — he ranks third in the NBA in turnovers per 100 possessions, an artifact of high-pressure pick-and-roll usage rather than careless ball security in space.

Defensively he is functional rather than dominant. He handles wing-to-power-forward assignments adequately, contests at expected rates, and his strength shows up when wings try to drive through him. Defenders shoot 1.3 percentage points above expectation against him at the rim and on the perimeter; he is a true perimeter defender in the colloquial sense, not a stopper in the schematic sense. The defensive rebounding (17.6% DREB%) is genuinely above wing average and supports lineup flexibility — Portland can play him at the four without conceding the glass.

Character and trajectory are the through-line of this profile. He has improved every year since the Washington years, and his interim coach (Tiago Splitter) named him the team's vocal leader through a year of organizational turbulence. The playoff series against the Spurs ended in a five-game loss but he sustained 22 PPG and was the only Portland player whose production held; he framed the elimination as "this experience was needed." The durability flag is real: a recurring lower-back issue cost him 10 games across multiple episodes in 2025-26, with similar soft-tissue patterns in 2024-25, and the cumulative two-year picture warrants attention even though he played 66 games and was healthy by playoffs.

The bottom-line read: Avdija is a quality starter operating slightly above the band ceiling, with a credible runway into perennial-All-Star territory if the off-dribble shooting gap narrows. The 18-strength, zero-liability profile shape is genuinely rare in the band — he has no exploitable hole that defenses can attack possession after possession on either end. What gates the projection is durability and a single self-creation skill; closing both moves him into Pascal Siakam / Bam Adebayo-adjacent territory. The downside is that the back recurrence accelerates into chronic load management, in which case he stabilizes as a 21/6/6 high-IQ wing starter rather than ascending.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.

The OVR is not calculated from the attribute ratings or skill areas. It is assigned by comparing the player against a library of anchor players whose ratings have been calibrated and locked in.

That is why an OVR can sit higher than any individual skill area. The OVR measures the whole player against the league; the attribute ratings and skill areas show where specific strengths and weaknesses fall within position.
