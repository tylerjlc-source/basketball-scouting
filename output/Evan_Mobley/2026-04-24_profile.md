## Section 3 — Scouting Narrative

Mobley is the modern Switchable Big in its purest current form — a 7-foot defender who guards guards, forwards, and centers at 800-plus possessions each over a single season, then anchors a top-three team defense from the paint when those matchups end. The first thing you notice is length matched to lateral mobility that should not coexist at this size: a 7'4" wingspan and 9'0" standing reach attached to hip and foot speed that stays with perimeter creators through multiple dribble moves, without the fouling pattern that usually accompanies a big trying to defend on the ball. He is the reigning Defensive Player of the Year at age 23, anchor of a 64-18 #1-seed defense, and carrying that identity cleanly into the next contract window.

Offensively the profile is functional-across-the-board rather than primary-identity. He led the NBA in total dunks in 2025-26 (207 in 65 games) and converts at the rim at 75% on real volume — a true interior finisher, not a spacing-first big forced to finish when the play breaks down. Around the rim he has genuine touch: a high-volume floater game over 200 attempts per season and improving conversion (46% current vs 38% prior), non-dunk rim at 54%, and an elbow-distributor passing package that multiple outlets have described as "clairvoyant" — reads develop before they close, deliveries arrive where the receiver can catch and play. What he does not do is score self-creation at All-Star volume: pull-up 3PT is low-percentage, his catch-and-shoot 3PT regressed hard in 2025-26 (30.4% after a 38.7% prior season), and his FT% slipped to 60.6% this year on concerning footing. The assertiveness-fade discourse — documented for multiple seasons, surfaced publicly by Donovan Mitchell ("demand it; if I don't pass you the ball, get on my ass"), and acknowledged by Mobley himself — is the single clearest developmental line separating his current All-NBA Second Team ceiling from a genuine top-10 offensive-defensive star.

Defense is where the profile earns its composite. He contests 10+ shots per game, was second in NBA FG% allowed at the rim in 2024-25, was the only player league-wide averaging ≥1.5 blocks on ≤2 fouls, and co-held the sole distinction with Jaren Jackson Jr. of defending guards, wings, and bigs at 800+ possessions each. The on-ball coverage is a 9-plus sub-domain outright — he handles switches onto guards without scheme help and still protects the rim on the back end. Help defense reads develop early; post defense holds on thin sample at the 93rd percentile. The only defensive gap is rim-protection volume: block rate stays near 1.7 rather than 2.5+, because the framework prioritizes his perimeter switch responsibilities over paint roaming. It's an archetype design choice, not a deficiency.

Character sourcing is positive and multi-source. Head coach Kenny Atkinson on Mobley's work ethic: "he works his ass off quietly, he competes quietly" — and on his ambition: "he wants to be one of the greats." Assistant coach Andrew Olson on the developmental arc: "he realized it needed to go another level. And he's taken it to that next level." The competitive fire is real but quiet, a framing that has dogged him since USC when coaches tried to coax him into more visible leadership. The playoff track record has flipped sharply upward in the last two cycles: 69.9% TS% on 17.1 PPG in the 2024-25 run, and a current 67.1% TS% / 19.0 PPG on 77% field-goal shooting through the first two games of the 2025-26 opening series. The 2023-24 Boston series was the only meaningful downward playoff signal, and it is receding further with each productive run.

Bottom line: Mobley is the clearest current-era successor to the Bam Adebayo archetype template — defensive-first 1-5 coverage with a Jokić-derivative passing feel bolted on — with more lateral quickness than Bam and less post-scoring gravity, a cleaner 3PT ceiling than Jaren Jackson Jr. (when healthy) with less rim-protection volume and more switch versatility. The floor is a perennial DPOY-candidate interior anchor. The ceiling depends on the jump shot stabilizing and the scoring assertiveness catching up to the skill that's already there. Both are real paths at 24.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.

The OVR is not calculated from the attribute ratings or skill areas. It is assigned by comparing the player against a library of anchor players whose ratings have been calibrated and locked in.

That is why an OVR can sit higher than any individual skill area. The OVR measures the whole player against the league; the attribute ratings and skill areas show where specific strengths and weaknesses fall within position.
