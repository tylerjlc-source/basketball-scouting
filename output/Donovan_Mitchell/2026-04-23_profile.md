## Section 3 — Scouting Narrative

Donovan Mitchell at 29 has ascended to unquestioned franchise-anchor status — 2024-25 All-NBA First Team, seventh consecutive All-Star selection (2025-26), and the offensive engine of a Cleveland team that held the best regular-season record in the Eastern Conference last season. His compact 6'1"/215 frame sits structurally between pure point guard and wing, but his profile — elite three-level scoring, moderate playmaking, proven playoff volume — is Offensive Engine at every dimension that matters to the archetype.

Offensively the profile is deep and efficient for a primary creator. Catch-and-shoot 3PT at 40.7% on 623 attempts weighted is elite volume-plus-conversion; pull-up 3 at 33.9% is solid; mid-range 46.4% (49.4% in 2025-26) is a real weapon; FT% 85.0% career-consistent. True shooting 59.8% at 30.6% usage says he picks his shots responsibly even at volume. Self-creation is strong — pick-and-roll ball-handler PPP 1.014 (85th percentile) and ISO PPP 0.985 (72nd percentile) — though he sits below the Curry/Dame/SGA elite tier at that specific sub-domain. Playmaking has quietly expanded in Cleveland: 5.4 APG on 17.8% adjusted assist rate, potential assists 10.3/g, secondary assists 0.9/g. He is not a pure point but he reads the court well enough to orchestrate lineups around him. Touch shows up in floater volume (53.4% on 334 FGA) and at the line.

Defensively he is functional rather than a weapon. DFGPOE -0.2% overall (neutral), STL 1.4/g, deflections 2.7/g with 215-pound compact frame enabling switchability and handsy disruption. Lateral quickness is good; rim protection is position-appropriate floor. His 2024-25 and 2025-26 team defensive numbers (DRtg 110.9 on) are middle-of-the-pack — reflective of Cleveland's scheme-level defense more than individual impact. He competes, he contests, he doesn't quit on possessions, but he is not the Utah-era steal-happy defender he was at peak. For an Offensive Engine, "functional" is par.

Character and motor are durable. The 2025-26 first-round exit against New York featured a 38-point playoff debut that set the franchise record for scoring in a playoff debut, and a 28/7/5 Game 5 performance in elimination. Career playoff PPG delta of +3.3 vs regular season is a concrete statistical rise signal across 9 playoff seasons — he shows up when the volume matters. The recurring criticism is playoff stage: Cleveland has not yet broken through to the Conference Finals during his tenure. That is a team-context gap, not a motor or character one — his individual playoff output continues to rise.

Bottom line: the drift-sweep hypothesis was R13 Stage 2 rise candidacy on Mitchell's deep Cleveland playoff runs. Under S99-F01 strict gate interpretation, the picture is more nuanced — TS% delta -1.1 career is neutral (normal playoff drag), PPG +3.3 rise is real, but the qualitative signal is "prolific scorer whose team doesn't win" rather than the championship-Defining rise that R13 Stage 2 is designed to credit. No Stage 2 modifier fires. The drift correction from 8.86 to 8.92 is credential-driven — the 2024-25 All-NBA First Team selection is his first career First Team credential and pulls him cleanly from upper Tier 5 into Tier 4 floor alongside Kawhi and Booker.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.

The OVR is not calculated from the attribute ratings or skill areas. It is assigned by comparing the player against a library of anchor players whose ratings have been calibrated and locked in.

That is why an OVR can sit higher than any individual skill area. The OVR measures the whole player against the league; the attribute ratings and skill areas show where specific strengths and weaknesses fall within position.
