## Section 3 — Scouting Narrative

Durant is the consummate All-Around Wing — three-level scoring at 7'5" wingspan with elite touch and a complete shot diet, anchored by IQ and switchable defense across multiple positions. The first thing you notice is the size-skill combination: a 6'10" forward who shoots like a guard, with a release point no defender can contest cleanly. At 37 the body has been permanently modified by the 2019 Achilles tear (right calf atrophied; he removed step-back threes and side-step jumpers from his game), but the production has barely flinched — 78 games at 26 PPG on 64% TS, 27% USG, designated closer for a Houston team that was last in the league in clutch efficiency before adding him.

Offense is the densest part of the file: six sub-domains 9.0+ across the shooting and ball-skills domains. The catch-and-shoot three is generationally elite at 43.2% on 419 weighted attempts, with corner shooting at 50%. The pull-up three is improving year-over-year (32.4% prior → 40.1% current) and the mid-range remains a primary weapon at 49.7% on 366 FGA, 40% of his shot diet. Free throws at 86.2% with clutch FT 86.8% bracket every late-game decision rule. The ball-skill cluster is just as dense — ISO 1.076 PPP at 84th percentile, PnR ball-handler 0.937 PPP, drives 9.4 per game at 57.7% FG (anomalously high through-contact finishing for a 37-year-old post-Achilles wing), and floater touch at 54.8% on 515 FGA. Playmaking is co-star quality — reliable secondary creation, not anticipatory primary distribution. The negative-balance anchors on offense are offensive rebounding (1.3% OREB%, role-driven by perimeter scorer assignment) and an off-screen package that decayed materially in the move from Phoenix to Houston (74th → 44th percentile, scheme-or-decline ambiguous).

Defense is functional, not anchor-tier. Durant switches across positions 2–4 on the strength of length and IQ — 88th percentile in BBall Index defensive position versatility — but at 37, post-Achilles, he is no longer a high-deflection or playmaking defender. STL 0.8/g, deflections 1.5/g, charges drawn 0.01 — passive-contain switcher, not a disruptor. Closest-defender DFGPOE is −5.2% on 12.2 D_FGA per game, materially above wing baseline; ISO defense reads elite on a small sample. Houston's team defense with Durant on the floor has been mid-pack (DEF Rating 114.4), so this is not an anchor situation; he satisfies the "defends multiple positions" floor for an All-Around Wing without driving the team's defensive identity. Secondary rim protection at 1.0 BLK/g sits at the strict #23 strength cap.

Work ethic is multi-source elite — film review on his own shot mechanics weekly, defensive film before practice, and sustained craft refinement at a level that produced 78 games at age 37, anomalously good for the post-Achilles cohort. Wembanyama's 2026 profile self-quotes name Durant alongside Gobert as the work-ethic standard he's chasing. Competitive character is similarly heavy: 2× Finals MVP, 2× Champion, 1× MVP, 4× scoring titles, 11× All-NBA, 14× All-Star. The negative balance is post-Achilles playoff record (1 series win in 7 cycles) and a moderate career playoff TS% shrink (−3.2 across 14 series, 171 GP), held below the 9.0 ceiling not above. Trajectory is post-peak: skill production has held flat or improved (shooting and playmaking holding or improving), athleticism gradually declining off the 2018-19 Golden State peak. The active 2026 dormancy — bruised right knee + sprained left ankle, missed Games 1/3/4 vs Lakers, Houston down 1-3 — fires the R13 Stage 1 shrink modifier on the projection ceiling.

The current rubric reads as the heaviest career resume in the active Tier 3 cluster paired with the densest offensive cluster in the anchor library — both true. The defense and athleticism floors at 7.0–7.3, plus active-injury dormancy, plus post-Achilles structural modification, plus post-peak trajectory, plus R13 statistical shrink, combine to keep Durant below the active healthy peers at the same tier. Composite 9.26 reflects the lower-third of Tier 3, top edge — clearly elite franchise player, clearly below the current-cycle peak of the all-time-tier active comparators.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.

The OVR is not calculated from the attribute ratings or skill areas. It is assigned by comparing the player against a library of anchor players whose ratings have been calibrated and locked in.

That is why an OVR can sit higher than any individual skill area. The OVR measures the whole player against the league; the attribute ratings and skill areas show where specific strengths and weaknesses fall within position.
