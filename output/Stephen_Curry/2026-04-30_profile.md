## Section 3 — Scouting Narrative

Stephen Curry is the realized ceiling of the perimeter shot creator — a 6'2", 185-pound point guard whose career has rewritten what offensive value looks like at the position. The first thing you notice is the gravity: defenders chase him through screens before the ball arrives, off-ball pressure dictating where the offense breathes. At 38 with two recent injuries layered (hamstring strain 2024-25 playoffs, right patellofemoral pain syndrome 2025-26 with "new normal" framing in his own language), Curry is no longer ascending — but the per-possession output is still elite, dropping 26.6 points per game on 30.9 minutes, the third-most ever for a player 37 or older.

Offensively, Curry is the NBA all-time leader in free throw percentage at 91.2% career and one of a small handful of players ever to convert 39%-plus on 300-plus made 3PT in six different seasons. The current evaluation window pulls 40.4% catch-and-shoot 3PT on 604 attempts, 38.5% pull-up 3PT on 277 ball-handler possessions, and 50.5% on a low-volume mid-range diet. The shotmaking package is a record-setter — 98th percentile shotmaking efficiency on 14th percentile shot quality, the gap between the two reflecting both creation burden and outlier-skill conversion. The off-ball movement is the dimension no peer has matched: Cut 1.493 PPP, Off-Screen 1.145 PPP, off-speed 4.82 mph, with universal recognition as the best movement shooter of all time. The handle stays creative-elite at 38, feeding isolation, pick-and-roll ball-handler, and dribble-handoff weaponry. Playmaking is solid rather than Hall-of-Fame — court vision and passing execution work as secondary creator alongside Draymond Green, with elevated turnovers under primary-creator burden (TOV per 100 touches 4.3, A:TO 1.85).

Defensively, Curry is the structural weakness in the profile. He is 6'2" with a 6'4" wingspan and 185 pounds — career-long matchup target on every playoff series, exposed again in the 2025 first-round Houston series with Dillon Brooks and Fred VanVleet leading sustained physical pressure. DFGPOE sits mildly negative at +1.4%, with 1.1 steals per game and 2.4 deflections showing active hands but limited stop power. Help defense reads better than on-ball — positive analytical signal on rotations and weak-side reads — but team defensive rating 114.0 reflects mediocre team support. Rim protection is structurally zero. The athleticism floor — balanced burst (always craft-over-explosion), middling lateral, slim-frame strength — caps the defensive ceiling. He survives via team scheme and competitive effort, not lockdown ability.

Character and trajectory are the universal-positive cluster of the profile. Steve Kerr's "metronome" framing of the daily routine — training room, weight room, court — is multi-source corroborated. 2023-24 Clutch Player of the Year, leading the NBA in total clutch points that season. 2024 Olympic gold medal with a 36-point/9-three semifinal against Serbia and a 24-point gold-medal final consisting entirely of 3-pointers, including four in the final three minutes — the kind of singular big-stage performance that anchors the competitive-character ceiling. Four championships, two MVPs (one unanimous), one Finals MVP (2022, won without a co-superstar). The trajectory is declining on availability — 43 games played in 2025-26 versus 70 in 2024-25 — but per-game rate output is up, with 4.4 made threes per game as healthy as ever. The honest read: peak rate output retained, durability is the binding constraint, retirement window approaching but not imminent.

Curry sits at the floor of Tier 3 because the structural defensive weakness and athleticism floor are the deepest in the band, offset by record-holding shooting, best-ever off-ball gravity, and a heavy compound resume comparable to any Tier 3 active anchor. The profile is the most extreme-skew of any Tier 3 anchor — narrow but historically deep peaks, structural defensive dependence on team scheme. The R13 playoff signal is moderate rise on qualitative (4 rings + Finals MVP + Olympic gold + Clutch POY) paired with neutral statistical (career playoff TS% delta -2.3, normal star drag); Stage 2 gate-fails on convergence-axis divergence per the strict-interpretation precedent. Path D legacy 9.20 confirmed — credential accumulation since legacy (Olympic gold + 12th All-Star) and durability inflection (patellofemoral pain syndrome + age-38 reset) approximately cancel.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.

The OVR is not calculated from the attribute ratings or skill areas. It is assigned by comparing the player against a library of anchor players whose ratings have been calibrated and locked in.

That is why an OVR can sit higher than any individual skill area. The OVR measures the whole player against the league; the attribute ratings and skill areas show where specific strengths and weaknesses fall within position.
