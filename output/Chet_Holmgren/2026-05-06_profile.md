## Section 3 — Scouting Narrative

A 7'1" defensive anchor with skill-big offense layered on top — the modern Switchable Big at the center of a championship roster. The first thing you notice is the length operating in space: switchable to guards, vertical at the rim, mobile in transition, with a guard-style handle that puts the ball on the floor more credibly than any peer at his size. The frame is the perpetual scout-talk concern — 7'1", listed 208 lbs on a wiry build, with two structural bone injuries (Lisfranc, iliac wing fracture) costing roughly half of his three-year availability. Year 4 is the cleanest healthy sample anyone has had on him, and the ascent inside it is real.

Offensively the package is wider than the role currently demands. Catch-and-shoot threes hit at a steady 36–37% on real volume, free throws drop at 78%+ (elite for the position), and the mid-range — historically a small share of his diet — converted at 50% in 2025-26 on modest attempts. Around the rim he finishes 75%+ on layups and dunks with finger-roll touch from both hands. The off-the-bounce game is the ceiling-mover: Loud City film breakdown documented a corrected mechanical flaw this season — feet within shoulder frame, hip-height dribbles, attack stance balanced either direction — and he is generating unassisted looks against bigger defenders for the first time. The handoff PPP at 1.19 is elite for a big. The gaps are documented: Finals 3PT collapsed to ~16% across the 2024-25 series on persistent volume, off-dribble pull-up 3PT dropped to 28% in 2025-26, and the post-up game runs into a strength wall against bulkier defenders. Playmaking is functional rather than elite — high-post passing instincts and short-roll reads exist, but career-low assist volume in 2025-26 confirms this is a downstream-of-SGA role, not a hub.

Defense is the franchise asset. At-rim opponent FG% of 46.4% career sits third all-time behind Wembanyama and Gobert; in 2025-26 he allowed 39.9% as the contesting defender, the best mark among 42 players with 250+ contests. The verticality is technically clean — arms high, body straight, second-jump quick. He contested 93% of opponent shot attempts as the closest defender over the last two seasons, second in the league. Help defense is anticipatory rather than reactive; team defensive rebounding rises by nearly nine per 100 possessions when he is on the floor. The switchability is real and championship-validated — the Game 7 reel against Indiana caught him rotating, switching, holding Siakam to 5-of-13 with 2 assists, and setting the all-time Finals Game 7 block record. The structural drag is in the post: against bulkier post-up bigs (Valanciunas, Gafford, Lively, the Jokic concern) he is leveraged out of position, and aerial-displacement studies during contested rim attempts measured the highest dislodge rate in their sample. Wemby head-to-head matchups are documented as soft (11/7/1 across three games). Lateral quickness against shifty guards holds with a cushion-leave technique rather than mirror-quick stay-in-front coverage.

The motor is the highest-conviction trait in the entire profile. Daigneault calls his work ethic a "bottomless pit"; SGA, post-championship, said "the worst version of Chet today is the worst we'll get." Durant, naming All-Star teammates, singled out the mindset. Presti described instincts that "drive winning" without crossing into agenda. The trajectory is unambiguously up: first All-Star (2026), career-high points and field-goal percentage, mechanical handle correction, Hartenstein-corroborated strength gain, and 2026 Round 2 Game 1 producing a playoff-career-best 24/12/3 against the Lakers. The competitive-character read is split — career playoff TS% sits 4.7 points below regular season across 38 games and three runs, the Finals 3PT volume despite 16% efficiency was a documented decision-pressure flaw, and pre-2025 analyst skepticism asked whether he would assert himself in the postseason. The Game 7 closeout (record blocks, 18/8/5) reframes that question without erasing it. Defense never shrinks in the playoffs; offense moderate-shrinks.

The bottom-line read: an upper-Tier-6 Switchable Big with rim-protection peak that touches Tier 5 territory, held below a star tier by two structural drags (post defense vs heavy bigs, frame strength) and by playoff offensive shrinkage that is real even after the championship counter-evidence. The realistic ceiling is Mobley/AD-adjacent — elite defensive anchor with skill-big offense who can headline a championship core when the rest of the roster fits. The downside is durability: 51% career availability is the number that bounds this projection more than any skill gap. Year 4 is the inflection year. He'll either consolidate into Tier 5 over the next 24–36 months or hold around 8.48 with availability and post-D limits keeping the ceiling honest.
