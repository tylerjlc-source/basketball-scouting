## Section 3 — Scouting Narrative

Haliburton is a Pure Point Guard in the Chris Paul / Steve Nash lineage — a 6'5" floor general whose value is delivered through passing, decision-making, and shot-making rather than self-creation volume. The first thing you notice is the contrast: the body is lanky and unhurried, the footspeed is unspectacular, and yet the offense moves at his tempo. He punishes defenses by reading them, not by overpowering them. The shot is unorthodox — low set point, fast wrist snap, one-motion release — and it falls. The handle is efficient rather than creative. The motor is steady rather than relentless. Everything about the profile is engineered around one principle: he wants the ball to find the right player at the right moment, including when that player is himself.

Offensively, this is one of the most efficient high-leverage profiles in the league. He is a 42.7%-on-volume catch-and-shoot 3PT shooter on a four-consecutive-40%-seasons streak — the fourth-longest career-start streak in NBA history. Mid-range is automatic at 50.9% on real volume; the floater (54.6% on 208 attempts) is among the best touch profiles among any active guard. Pull-up shooting is functional rather than weapon-tier (36.3% from three off the dribble is solid, not elite). The handle is the same pattern: PnR ball-handler at the 94th percentile in points-per-possession, but ISO creation against set defenses lives in the 66th percentile. He does not break opponents down one-on-one; he conducts the game past them. Where the shooting and passing meet — drive-and-kick, pick-and-roll reads, off-screen reception (1.40 PPP, 96th percentile) — the offense is genuinely elite. Where the rim is the destination, it gets less elite: he attempts a low share of his shots at the basket (15.6%), draws fouls at a strikingly low rate for a primary creator (FTR 0.219), and the contact-finishing dimension is a real weakness, not a stylistic preference. Drive points-per-possession (0.513) is below average. He is converting the rim looks he chooses to take at 70%, but he's choosing carefully.

The playmaking is the franchise piece. Vision is top-of-league (top 5–10 active guards), execution is right behind it, and ball security is elite — A:TO 5.26, 1.9 turnovers per game on 92.4 touches. The 2024-25 season produced a 32-point / 15-assist / 12-rebound / 4-steal / zero-turnover line in the Eastern Conference Finals — the first such combination in NBA playoff history. He sees the play one beat early, delivers it on time and on target, and doesn't lose possessions through his handle even when forced to live-dribble against pressure. The lone counterexample sits in plain view: 8 turnovers across the first two games of the 2025 NBA Finals against Oklahoma City's point-of-attack defense. Against the most disciplined defensive pressure in the league, the handle wavered. It is the only multi-game decision-and-execution dropoff on his record, and it bookends the elite résumé honestly.

Defensively, this is the structural ceiling. He is a documented point-of-attack target — Cleveland in the 2025 second round and Oklahoma City in the 2025 Finals both built scouting reports around hunting him in screening actions, regardless of his nominal assignment. Defenders shoot 3.9 percentage points above their expected accuracy when he is the closest defender; isolation defense lives in the bottom decile. The compensation is real: 2.6 deflections per game, 1.3 steals, the rangy 6'7.5" wingspan plus genuine anticipatory IQ. He lives in passing lanes. But the help-defense competence does not offset the on-ball liability — coaches will continue to attack him until he proves otherwise. The interior defense numbers (rim protection, post defense) are not part of the role and are excluded from the profile by archetype design.

The character read is unambiguously positive. Carlisle calls him "a basketball savant" and "a second iteration of Reggie Miller" and trusted him with the keys to the Pacers' offense at age 22. The 2025 playoffs produced five career game-tying or go-ahead buckets in the final five seconds — only LeBron James has more in the play-by-play era. The "most overrated" anonymous-poll vote from April 2025 reads like a relic against the Finals run that followed. The bottom line is that the profile is a clean Tier 4 placement at healthy baseline, but the Achilles ruptured in Game 7 of the 2025 Finals is the central forward-looking question. Pre-injury athleticism was already modest by guard standards; perimeter players coming off Achilles tears tend to lose explosiveness — Klay and Wall (both guards) returned diminished, Cousins (a big) similarly so — and KD is the standalone exception, fully recovered as a 7-foot wing at age 30. Haliburton has limited margin to absorb that loss. The composite holds at 9.11 because R13 dormancy treats the active recovery as a hold-pending-proof-of-return, not a deflation. The projection block carries the downside.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.

The OVR is not calculated from the attribute ratings or skill areas. It is assigned by comparing the player against a library of anchor players whose ratings have been calibrated and locked in.

That is why an OVR can sit higher than any individual skill area. The OVR measures the whole player against the league; the attribute ratings and skill areas show where specific strengths and weaknesses fall within position.
