## Section 3 — Scouting Narrative

Sabonis is the modern archetype of the scoring, playmaking, rebounding big man whose defensive limitations define where the ceiling lives. He anchors a possession — touches on 90% of halfcourt sets — not through usage but through gravity: the elbow pass, the high-post DHO, the short roll, the offensive-rebound reset. He's a 3x NBA rebounding champion and approaching a 4th consecutive title in a club of Wilt, Rodman, Moses, Garnett, and Drummond. What separates him from that company is the passing: 60+ career triple-doubles, including a "perfect" triple-double in 2024-25 (first since 1977-78), and elite potential-assist volume (8.1/game weighted) that reads less like a big man and more like a hub point-forward in a 7-foot frame.

Offensively the profile is textbook Versatile Scoring Big with one outlier signature — his efficiency is anomalous for the archetype. 2024-25 produced 20.8 / 14.2 / 6.0 on 61.6% TS — a line that VSB prototypes historically don't hit at peak (Cousins at his best was 25/13/5 on 58% TS; Vucevic's All-Star breakout was 21/12/4 on 57% TS). Sabonis scores from five spots: rim on high volume (55% of shots, 68% conversion), floater range with elite touch (50.6% on 124 FGA — unusual volume for a big), mid-range elbow pull-ups (45% prior, career-trending up), post with full counters (hooks, fades, turnarounds), and a genuine 3PT weapon when healthy (41% on real volume in 2024-25). He is not a self-creator — ISO PPP in the 32nd percentile, PnR BH in the 8th, dribbles-per-touch of 1.15 — but the catch-play facilitation-and-scoring hub is a legitimate offensive identity. The free-throw line lives at 74.6% career, a functional-above-big-starter baseline that clutch-situation drops (67.5%) slightly tarnish.

Defense is where the profile collapses. Rim protection is near-fringe for the position — 6th percentile block rate among bigs, 68.9% opponent FG% at the rim ("worse than backup centers" per BR analysis), multi-source consensus as "one of the worst shot blockers at center in the NBA." The structural driver is the wingspan: 6'11" on a 6'10" frame is a minus-plus differential unusual for a starting C, and no amount of effort closes that gap. He shows up physically — 3.4 PF/g is high volume for contests — but deterrence doesn't follow. The 2023 Warriors first-round series was the textbook case: Golden State sagged Looney into the paint, dared Sabonis to shoot, and the Kings' whole offensive hub disrupted. Elsewhere on defense, he's a below-average switch defender, a functional-at-best post defender (thin sample but 2024-25 held up at 0.835 PPP), and a help-defender who makes the obvious rotation but doesn't anticipate plays. Team DRtg runs 114+ with him on the floor in healthy seasons. This is the definition of "defense the team accepts" from the VSB template.

Character sourcing is consistently positive. Coach language: "extremely aggressive, plays with motor, always going hard." Assistant coach Andrew Olson describes Sabonis as "obsessive about improving himself." He's iron-man-durable historically (70+ GP across 2023-24 and 2024-25) until the 2025-26 meniscus injury broke the streak. 2024-25 produced his career-best season at age 28 — but no All-Star selection despite the numbers, a Western Conference depth casualty. Playoff performance is the one real character shadow: historical-only status (last playoff 2022-23, 3-year gap with no intervening activity) plus documented strong statistical shrink (career −6.0 TS% delta vs regular season; 2018-19 vs BOS at −14.6, 2022-23 vs GSW at −15.1) plus Sabonis's own 2023 admission ("I lacked confidence in my shot") produces a multi-run, multi-source shrink pattern.

Bottom line: The C non-negotiable on rim protection is the decisive profile feature. At PF, where he clears all three gate thresholds, Sabonis is a Tier 6 player on credential stack (3x All-Star, 2x All-NBA, 3x rebounding titles, elite 2024-25 hub-offense production) — a genuine team star. At C, where he actually plays, the gate cap holds him at upper Tier 7 — a quality starter and role-defining contributor whose offensive hub identity cannot fully compensate for structural defensive liability. The system's position-gate mechanism activates exactly as designed: credentials don't override structural position incompatibility. Post-meniscus return at 30 carries real uncertainty; projection reflects that. The ceiling going forward is below his current level because his peak year has already happened.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.

The OVR is not calculated from the attribute ratings or skill areas. It is assigned by comparing the player against a library of anchor players whose ratings have been calibrated and locked in.

That is why an OVR can sit higher than any individual skill area. The OVR measures the whole player against the league; the attribute ratings and skill areas show where specific strengths and weaknesses fall within position.
