## Section 3 — Scouting Narrative

Giannis Antetokounmpo is the modern franchise Big rendered at maximum athletic scale — a 6'11", 242-pound primary creator with a 7'4" wingspan, a 40-inch max vertical, and a point guard's handle attached to a power forward's frame. The first thing you notice is the open court: in transition he covers ground in three strides that take other bigs five, finishes above the rim against any contest, and creates advantages with raw downhill momentum that no defender at his size can mirror. The second thing you notice is that the perimeter game has not arrived. He is a non-shooter at 31, defenses sag to make him beat them with a jumper that comes and goes, and the free throw line — where mechanical retooling has not stopped a multi-year decline — is the structural soft spot in an otherwise overwhelming offensive picture.

The offensive engine runs on rim pressure, transition dominance, and elite primary creation usage. Inside the arc, he is among the most efficient finishers in the league — converts at the basket at top-five rates, dunks on a third of his rim attempts, and gets to the line on roughly six tenths of a free throw per field goal attempt. The pick-and-roll ball-handler game is genuinely rare for a player his size (top-tier per-possession efficiency at high volume), and the isolation game produces above-average output even on tiny perimeter sample. From the post he is a credible secondary scorer (elite per-possession when used) but not a hub. The shooting profile is the structural ceiling: catch-and-shoot is sub-30% on tiny volume, pull-up threes are a non-tool, mid-range produced at a functional rate in 2024-25 healthy and collapsed in the 2025-26 fragment, and the free throw rate is a real intentional-foul-strategy concern late in close games. The mid-range improvement narrative attached to the early 2025-26 hot stretch did not survive the full sample.

Defensively he is the prototype free-safety Big — roams the paint, switches credibly across all five positions, and discourages attacks at the rim through length and timing rather than swat volume. On-ball pressure shows up in opponent shooting suppression on the order of four points below expected, and the historical rim-FG% allowed sits at the elite end of the league. Help defense is the dominant identity: he is one of the rare players in the league whose primary and help-defense responsibilities run simultaneously, and the 2019-20 DPOY is supported by ongoing multi-source elite-help language even if the 2025-26 Bucks team defense is mid-pack rather than anchor-tier. Defensive rebounding holds at top-25-40 big level by length and positioning, not grit. The 7'4" wingspan and 12'2" reach mean he competes structurally on the glass even without dedicated boxing-out volume.

The character profile is unambiguous and probably the single biggest carrier of the composite. Multi-source peer testimony converges on a defining-identity work ethic — first in the gym, last out, post-game weights every game, opponents at FIBA EuroBasket 2025 describing him as "trying to destroy everybody." The competitive resume is the rarer signal: a 2021 Finals MVP delivered through a 50-point series-clinching Game 6 with 17-of-19 free throws under championship pressure, a 2024-25 first-round series in which he averaged 33 points and posted a historic 30/20/13/2/2 line in a Game 5 OT loss, and a triple-crown of two regular-season MVPs plus DPOY plus Finals MVP plus a championship — a stack only Jordan and Hakeem share. The career playoff TS% delta is mildly negative on aggregate (career matched -3.4 vs regular season), pulled down by one-and-done dips against Boston in 2021-22 and Miami in 2022-23, but the qualitative side carries forward an elite "rises to the moment" data point that cannot be erased.

The trajectory question is the closest thing to a real concern. The 2025-26 season was a 36-game injury-fragment ended by an accumulated multi-injury cluster (hamstring, knee, calf, adductor) that left him on the floor for six of the team's final 37 games and the Bucks at 29-42 outside the play-in. Across the last six seasons he has missed at least 15 games in five of them. The skill-trajectory has been plateau-elite for seven years; the physical ceiling remains intact when on the floor; the forward projection is bounded primarily by health volatility rather than skill regression. He is firmly in late-prime territory at age 31 with rising injury risk and a non-shooting profile that becomes more exposed as the rest of the league accumulates floor-spacing.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.

The OVR is not calculated from the attribute ratings or skill areas. It is assigned by comparing the player against a library of anchor players whose ratings have been calibrated and locked in.

That is why an OVR can sit higher than any individual skill area. The OVR measures the whole player against the league; the attribute ratings and skill areas show where specific strengths and weaknesses fall within position.
