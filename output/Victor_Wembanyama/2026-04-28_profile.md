## Section 3 — Scouting narrative

The first thing you notice is the body — there is no analog. The second thing you notice is what a 22-year-old is doing with it: anchoring a top-three defense as the unanimous Defensive Player of the Year, finishing as an MVP finalist, and operating at 31% usage on 61% true shooting while averaging 25 points, 11.5 rebounds, 3.1 assists, and 3.4 blocks. He is one of two players in fifty years with two unanimous individual awards (Rookie of the Year 2024, DPOY 2026). The archetype is All-Around Big — wing skills layered onto a big baseline, with rebounding and rim presence non-optional. The natural lineage is the Anthony Davis / Nikola Jokić / Bam Adebayo template, with a defensive identity weighted heavier than any of them and a playmaking dimension that has only recently emerged.

Offensively, he is a multi-level scorer who lives at the rim through verticality — 75% on rim attempts, 47% dunk rate, transition possessions converting at 76th percentile efficiency on high frequency for size — and stretches the floor on real volume from three (37% on 371 attempts, weighted across two seasons). The mid-range game is genuine: 40% on 168 attempts, with a floater (44% from 4-14 feet, 195 attempts) that gives him a trail-finish option no traditional big has. The pull-up jumper is the offensive gap, sitting under 30% on real volume, and his isolation creation lags league average even at high usage. Where the profile broke new ground in 2025-26 is the playmaking — six potential assists per game, growing inverted pick-and-roll responsibility, and the height to deliver passes over any defender. Vision and decision-making both register All-NBA caliber, supported by 31% usage with only 2.4 turnovers per game (improved from 3.2 the prior season).

Defense is where the league-defining work happens. The Spurs' defensive rating with Wembanyama on the floor was 103.2 in 2025-26; without him 113.4 — the largest on/off swing in the league. Opponents shoot 8.7 percent worse with him as the closest defender, and the deterrent effect cascades downstream: 5.5 percent fewer opposing shots come at the rim when he plays, because the rest of the offense routes around him. He led the league in blocks per game on starter minutes. The mechanism is verticality and length, not strength. The 245-pound frame is light for the position, smaller players are documented to rip the ball from his grasp, and he gives ground physically when teams target him directly. He wins with reach and reaction. On the perimeter, his cushion-and-react approach functions against non-primary creators in switch situations, but he is not a true one-on-one perimeter container against lead guards.

Character is the multiplier. Three independent positive signals: Pop calls him "one of the most mature 19-year-olds I've ever been around" with character that "is incredible," his development coach describes someone who "doesn't want to skip steps," and Wembanyama himself is publicly critical of NBA peers' work ethic, naming Durant and Gobert as the standards he's chasing. He spent his DVT recovery training with Shaolin monks in China — for mental focus and body positioning, by his own framing. The trajectory is straight up: unanimous Rookie of the Year in 2024, a DPOY-caliber 2024-25 season cut short by a deep vein thrombosis in his right shoulder at 46 games played, and the unanimous DPOY plus MVP-finalist 2025-26 just completed.

Bottom line: the DVT is the swing factor on long-term projection. A 7'5" body anchoring top-of-league defense and elite playmaking has no precedent for longevity assessment, and recurrent thrombosis is a real medical risk. The strength deficit is the structural ceiling on every cross-reference cap that fired in this evaluation; verticality and length compensate empirically right now, but if the gap stays open while opponents adjust, the questions surface in postseason matchups. The composite assignment moved up from 9.14 to 9.34 (revised S118 from initial 9.30 — anchor-cluster recalibration) on the strength of unanimous DPOY, MVP finalist, archetype migration from Switchable Big to All-Around Big, and a full-season healthy 64-game sample. Tier 3 placement reflects current-level production clearly above Durant (9.26), Giannis (9.30), and Curry (9.20); career credentials still trail the three peer anchors but ascending trajectory + unanimous DPOY 2025-26 + MVP finalist current-cycle awards drive the upper-edge placement. The 2026 first-round playoff sample (35-point franchise-record debut, 27/11/7 post-concussion bounce-back in Game 4) is directionally positive but below the R13 two-series sample minimum.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.

The OVR is not calculated from the attribute ratings or skill areas. It is assigned by comparing the player against a library of anchor players whose ratings have been calibrated and locked in.

That is why an OVR can sit higher than any individual skill area. The OVR measures the whole player against the league; the attribute ratings and skill areas show where specific strengths and weaknesses fall within position.
