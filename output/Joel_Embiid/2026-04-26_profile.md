## Section 3 — Scouting Narrative

A 7-foot, 280-pound All-Around Big whose healthy-state profile is one of the most complete in the sport — primary post hub, face-up scorer, foul-drawing engine, interior defensive anchor, and developing offensive initiator. The first thing the eye lands on is the combination of length and frame: a 9'5.5" standing reach paired with strength that has never been a question mark, even as the rest of the athletic profile has changed. The frame and the skill ceiling are the constants. Everything else — the explosion, the mobility, the availability — has become contingent on a knee timeline that has now bracketed three calendar years.

Offensively, the post is the foundation. Drop step, jump hook, fade, and a face-up game that has expanded under Nick Nurse into elbow pull-ups, kickouts, and high-post initiation. Free throws come at elite rates — career FTR around 0.50, 83% career conversion that climbs to 85%+ in recent seasons — and the contact finish is a genuine weapon, not a byproduct. The mid-range pull-up out of triple-threat is signature; the catch-and-shoot 3PT is functional rather than primary, sitting in the low-to-mid 30s as a baseline with the 2022-23 MVP-year 39.8% standing as the outlier. The playmaking development is real: passes per game and potential assists have continued to rise even as availability has dropped, and the half-court hub function is intact when he's on the floor. Where the offense has changed is on the drive — Drive FG% has fallen from 57.8% to 46.0%, Drive PPP from 1.016 to 0.786 across the eval window, signaling that the burst into contact at the rim now produces different outcomes than it did at peak.

Defensively, the profile splits in two. The interior anchor remains live: NBA.com's post-return film study showed opponents shooting 21.9% worse than average inside six feet against him, and 41.4% at the rim with him as the only obstacle — the best defensive mark for any center in that stretch. Length, verticality, and standing reach do not require explosion to function. The post defense is similarly intact, supported by elite strength. But the perimeter mobility has cracked. Multi-source 2025-26 reporting — independent of the box score — describes him as "dizzy and cement-footed" in pick-and-roll coverage, "feet too slow to contest off screens," and isolation defense as "problematic." The ISO Defense PPP percentile has fallen from 82nd to 39th. He still helps inside scheme; he no longer covers ground in space. Switch-heavy modern defenses cannot use him at the second-side action.

Character is the most complicated part of the read. The MVP credential is real; so is the playoff record that runs alongside it. Career playoff TS% is down 3.6 points from regular season, multiple elimination-game low-water marks have been documented (15 points in a Game 7 elimination during the MVP year stands out), and league-wide qualitative coverage has converged on "the only MVP without a Conference Finals appearance" framing. Conditioning concerns are similarly multi-source: a 50-pound weight gain during one rehab cycle, blown-off conditioning drills earlier in his career, a public challenge from Doc Rivers about whether out-of-shape MVPs exist. The counter-evidence is concrete and worth weighing — a 35 PPG, 60.9% TS first-round series against the Knicks in 2024 played through Bell's Palsy and a torn meniscus is genuine competitive grit, not a manufactured narrative. The pattern is selective rather than absent: at the highest stakes in winnable contexts he has produced, but the elimination shrink and the maintenance gaps are documented across enough independent sources that they are not noise.

The trajectory is the operative reality. Two left-knee surgeries in fourteen months. A 2024-25 season of nineteen games. A 2025-26 of thirty-eight. An appendectomy on April 9, 2026 that took him out of Games 1, 2, and 3 of the current first-round series against Boston, with Game 4 doubtful. The R12 anchor on the 2022-23 MVP year captures who he is when healthy — a top-15 active big across most domains, top-5 at his archetype's primary value drivers — but the active-injury dormancy is what is true today. The Tier 6 placement, the POT below composite, the Min that drops into G-League territory if attrition compounds, and the Max that still touches All-Star ceiling if he returns to form: all of those are reflections of a player whose ceiling and floor are now meaningfully separated by health alone.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.

The OVR is not calculated from the attribute ratings or skill areas. It is assigned by comparing the player against a library of anchor players whose ratings have been calibrated and locked in.

That is why an OVR can sit higher than any individual skill area. The OVR measures the whole player against the league; the attribute ratings and skill areas show where specific strengths and weaknesses fall within position.
