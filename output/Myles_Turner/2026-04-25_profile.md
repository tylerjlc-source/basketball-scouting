## Section 3 — Scouting Narrative

Eleven seasons in, Myles Turner is the canonical stretch-big-with-rim-protection — the player every team imagines when sketching a modern five who can shoot threes and cover the paint. The first thing you notice is the length: 7'4" wingspan and a 9'4" standing reach that does the bulk of his defensive work without needing elite explosion or lateral mobility. His tools are almost entirely vertical and positional rather than athletic. Now in his first season with Milwaukee after a decade in Indiana, he replaced Brook Lopez beside Giannis Antetokounmpo with a four-year deal that asked him to be Lopez 2.0 — pop-and-protect.

Offensively the identity is floor spacing, full stop. He shoots 39%-plus from three on real volume, and does it primarily as a pick-and-pop big out of high screens — a role the Bucks designed around him from day one. The corner three is a weapon (45-plus percent), and his catch-and-shoot mechanics translate from any spot above the break. Off the dribble he is much more limited — pull-up threes drop into the low thirties, and he does not break defenders down to create his own shot. The mid-range is functional but low-volume; teams concede it. He does not bully into the paint, does not draw fouls at a big-five rate, and rarely posts up despite being capable when he gets there. Inside the arc, he finishes what the offense creates: rolls, cuts, transition, dump-offs. Eighty-eight percent of his rim attempts are assisted. That number tells the offensive story — he is a connector and a shooter, not a primary scorer.

Defensively the rim is the value driver. He has been a top-five rim protector by points-saved-at-the-basket metrics, holds opponents under 50% in the post, and reads pick-and-roll coverage well enough to play drop or jump-out depending on scheme. Pacers fans saw him go 5-for-1 blocks in Game 3 of the NBA Finals — the most in a Finals game in three years and the first 5-block / 5-steal pair with a teammate since Iverson and Mutombo in 2001. The defense is not athletic in the explosion sense; it is built on length, timing, anticipation, and IQ. The cost is lateral mobility — he has never been a real switch defender, teams hunt him on the perimeter, and there is genuine evidence that he has lost a step at age 29. Rebounding is the chronic limitation. His total rebound percentage sits below the big-anchor baseline on both glasses, and even with the spacing-cost framing, the gap is real. On the defensive board specifically, he clears the position non-negotiable threshold by half a point — meaningful margin, no margin to spare.

Off the court, the read is durable and positive. Loyal, hard-working, mature since college; eleven years on one franchise before age-29 free agency; Pacers franchise blocks leader; no off-court flags across a long career. Clutch shot-making is genuinely elite — 59% FG and 58% from three in late-game situations across the evaluation window. The competitive character is solid rather than transcendent — his Finals offense was uneven (37.7% FG, 21.4% from three across seven games) even as his defense produced signature moments — and the 2025-26 season has been framed across multiple Bucks-side outlets as disappointing, with concerns about decline and Doc Rivers usage. The trajectory is plateaued bordering on mild decline: his career-best 3PT-shooting year (2024-25 in Indiana) immediately preceded a step-back in usage, role, and impact this season.

Bottom line: Turner is a quality NBA starter whose dual identity — high-volume floor spacing plus elite-tier interior defense — is genuinely rare. The composite reflects what he produces, not what scouts once projected. He never made an All-Star or All-Defensive team, the rebounding has always been below par for the position, and at 29 his physical floor is starting to show. But there are very few centers in the league who make floors and rim coverage simultaneously, and his profile fits the role-defining-contributor band cleanly. The forward ceiling is bounded by age and physical decline; the floor is supported by skill durability, length, and a long track record of availability.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.

The OVR is not calculated from the attribute ratings or skill areas. It is assigned by comparing the player against a library of anchor players whose ratings have been calibrated and locked in.

That is why an OVR can sit higher than any individual skill area. The OVR measures the whole player against the league; the attribute ratings and skill areas show where specific strengths and weaknesses fall within position.
