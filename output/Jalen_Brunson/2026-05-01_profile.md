## 3. Scouting narrative

Brunson is an undersized, strength-anchored primary-creator point guard who has built a top-tier offensive engine without elite athleticism. The first thing you notice is what's missing — no burst, no above-rim finishes, lateral foot speed merely adequate for the position — and how completely it doesn't matter on the offensive end. He has built every counter a 6'1" guard needs to win against length: stop-on-a-dime two-foot stops in the lane, fundamental pivots into spin-back fades and baby hooks, an extreme floater volume that compensates for the lack of vertical pop, and a mid-range pull-up game that converts at a rate most NBA wings don't reach. The body usage is post-player footwork running through a guard's frame.

The offense is the product. He runs 16 drives a game and converts 53.5% from them, mostly via floaters (37% of his total attempts come from the 4-14 foot zone, and they go in at 48.5%). His pick-and-roll ball-handler efficiency sits in the 87th percentile on 591 possessions across the evaluation window — high volume, top-tier production. Catch-and-shoot 3PT lands at 41.1% on 475 attempts. The handle is elite without wasted dribbles. Ball security stays strong (2.4 turnovers per game on 29.3% usage; 2.88 assist-to-turnover) despite the heavy primary-handler load. The 2025-26 evolution toward fewer drives and more spot-ups — free-throw rate dropped from 0.373 to 0.285, pull-up 3 volume up — is a deliberate role refinement, not regression. Net true shooting held at 59%. Where the offensive profile finds its ceiling is at burst — the lack of a reliable separation move puts a structural cap on his pull-up 3 (35% on a workable but not elite sample) and limits late-clock isolation against elite point-of-attack defenders.

Defensively he is a real liability. Estimated Plus-Minus had him as the worst defender in the 2025 NBA Playoffs, and the targeting evidence is consistent: opponents shot 59% when he was the primary defender in the Pacers second-round series, and Atlanta built their entire 2026 first-round game-plan around hunting his matchup. His on-ball pressure grades below the survivability line — opponents shoot above expected against him, contested-shot rate is modest, and his structural lateral cap (6'4" wingspan, average foot speed) does not improve. Help-defense effort is genuine — he led the 2025 postseason in charges drawn — but the IQ-driven help is not there to compensate for the on-ball gap. He provides essentially zero rim deterrence (0.1 blocks/game). The Knicks' team scheme actively shields him as much as scheme can; deep playoff series will continue to expose the matchup.

Character is the multiplier on the entire profile. He won the 2024-25 Kia NBA Clutch Player of the Year award, leading the league in clutch scoring at 51.5% from the field. His coaches converge on the same language across four programs (Wright, Carlisle, Thibodeau, Brown): work ethic compared to Patrick Ewing, "born scorer and leader," the rare player who doesn't need to say "follow me" because his preparation does it for him. Zero negative signals in the public record. The 2024 Eastern Conference Finals run was as the lead, not the second option. The pattern under pressure is rise — game-volume up, recognition up, multi-source convergence on "best when his best is needed."

The bottom line: a near-perfect offensive primary creator with defensive softness that would disqualify many archetypes but is absorbed by the All-Star-tier offensive output. The career ceiling is one more peak season — a deeper playoff run could earn All-NBA First Team recognition — but structural defense and athletic limits keep him out of the Tier 4 conversation. He IS the player at this point: the trajectory from here is sustaining current peak with managed minutes and team scheme protection, not adding new dimensions.

## How to read these ratings

A complete basketball player is built from 26 attributes — the specific skills, physical tools, and intangibles that determine ability on the court. Every player is rated on every attribute.

- **Attribute ratings (1–10)** grade each individual attribute — like finishing at the rim, off-ball defense, or shot selection — against other players at the same position. A 9 in shooting for a guard means elite among guards.
- **Skill area ratings (1–10)** organize the 26 attributes into broader areas like Shooting, Defense, and Playmaking, giving a faster read of a player's profile without the full detail.
- **Overall player grade (OVR, 1–10)** grades a player's complete basketball ability against the entire basketball universe. A 10.00 would be a theoretically perfect player; around 6.79 marks the floor for NBA-caliber play. Position, height, weight, and archetype do not change where a player lands — every player is measured on the same scale.

The OVR is not calculated from the attribute ratings or skill areas. It is assigned by comparing the player against a library of anchor players whose ratings have been calibrated and locked in.

That is why an OVR can sit higher than any individual skill area. The OVR measures the whole player against the league; the attribute ratings and skill areas show where specific strengths and weaknesses fall within position.
