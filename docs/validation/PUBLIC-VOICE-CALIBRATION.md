## §1 — Zach Lowe (PRIMARY voice exemplar)

**Outlet:** The Ringer (formerly ESPN, Grantland). League-wide pro NBA columnist; "Ten Things" weekly.

### Excerpt 1 — "Bam Adebayo is the fiercest, best NBA player you don't know" (ESPN, 2020-02-13)

> TOWARD THE END of Bam Adebayo's freshman year at Northside High School in tiny Pinetown in eastern North Carolina, two teachers, a coach, and a mother gathered in the coach's classroom to discuss how they would wrap their arms around the mother's son — a raw basketball prodigy already bordering on 6-foot-8.

> If there was something that was hard to project — something evaluators missed — it was Adebayo's ability to channel that almost violent competitive rage into healthy directions: toward self-actualization and winning, never greed.

> Adebayo's addiction to defense — to breaking his opponent's will — shined in pre-draft workouts.

[Source — ESPN](https://www.espn.com/nba/story/_/id/28678382/bam-adebayo-fiercest-best-nba-player-know)

### Excerpt 2 — "Khris Middleton is a rare kind of second star" (ESPN, 2019-02-21)

> But peak Middleton is a snug complement for Antetokounmpo: an ace shooter who can space the floor, score late in the shot clock, and defend multiple positions.

> Midway through his rookie season, teammates and coaches noticed how he used leaners, step-backs, and other tricks of footwork to get it off whenever he wanted.

> He is not as good as the typical second banana on a title team. The Bucks hope to compensate with depth and fit.

[Source — ESPN](https://www.espn.com/nba/story/_/id/26041744/khris-middleton-rare-kind-second-star)

### Excerpt 3 — "Ten things I like and don't like, including pass king Nikola Jokic" (ESPN, 2018-03)

> Jokic has a way of making the simple revelatory. That pass is hiding in plain sight for any rim-running big man, but you rarely see it.

> Jokic is wrapping perhaps the best passing season of all time for a big man. He is on pace to be the fourth human 6-foot-10 or taller … to average six assists per game.

> Jokic has assisted on 29 percent of Denver baskets while on the floor, a higher share than Chamberlain or Garnett ever produced.

[Source — ESPN](https://www.espn.com/nba/story/_/id/23044990/zach-lowe-10-things-like-including-nikola-jokic-dimes-nba)

### Voice notes

- Alternates scene (the classroom, the coach's office) with dense analytical clauses that braid stat with eye-test in a single sentence.
- **Em-dashes are characteristic of Lowe's published prose.** They let him insert the qualifier or counter-evidence inside the same sentence as the claim. Per S174-F02, the system's published-output target ([PUBLIC-LANGUAGE-GUIDE.md](../PUBLIC-LANGUAGE-GUIDE.md) §5.4 Mitchell sample) does NOT use em dashes; em-dash density was tested in Mitchell v4-rev2 and produced stilted prose. Treat Lowe's em-dash usage as upstream stylistic context, not as a rule for system output.
- Stats are not lined up in parallel. They are dropped where they sharpen a verbal point ("a higher share than Chamberlain or Garnett ever produced").
- Judgment is direct but never bald. Always a "rare kind of," "snug complement," "addiction to" putting texture on the verdict.
- Willing to negative-balance in plain English: "He is not as good as the typical second banana on a title team."
- **Cannot be reproduced by Claude at sentence level.** S174 v1–v4 attempted Lowe-imitation; all four iterations were rejected. The §10 ranking has been revised accordingly.

## §2 — Jonathan Tjarks (SECONDARY register; conversational pace)

**Outlet:** The Ringer. Pro NBA features, prospect coverage. (1987–2022.)

### Excerpt — "The Mavericks Are Done Coming Up Short" (The Ringer, 2020-12-01)

> Everything is bigger in Texas, including Luka Doncic's teammates.

> Doncic is so big that he can always create angles for himself off the dribble, and he has such excellent touch around the basket that he needs only a sliver of space to score.

> The philosophical adjustment the Mavs have made this season is emphasizing defensive versatility around Doncic instead of 3-point shooting.

> No guard will ever be asked to do too much on offense next to Doncic, arguably the most ball-dominant player in the league.

> He shot better from 2-point range last season (57.4 percent) than both LeBron (56.4) and Davis (54.6).

[Source — The Ringer](https://www.theringer.com/nba/2020/12/1/21754661/dallas-mavericks-luka-doncic-josh-richardson)

### Voice notes

- Short, declarative, conversational. Almost the opposite of Lowe's clausal density.
- Reads like a smart fan talking to another smart fan: "Everything is bigger in Texas."
- Stat citations are sparse and always inside an argument, never in clusters.
- Tactical argument builds paragraph by paragraph at the speed of speech rather than packed into one heavy sentence.
- **Use this register when Lowe is too dense** — vet retrospectives, declarative judgments where you want the reader to feel the conclusion settle.

## §3 — Howard Beck (FEATURE/MAGAZINE register; sources do the analytical work)

**Outlet:** The Ringer (formerly Sports Illustrated, NYT, Bleacher Report). Longform NBA features.

### Excerpt — "The Dallas Mavericks Risked It All—and It Almost Worked" (The Ringer, 2024-06-18)

> Luka Doncic, drenched in sweat and despair, his hair matted, his face flush, his neck wrapped in a towel, settled in for his final postgame interview of the 2023-24 season about an hour before midnight on Monday.

> A thrilling, and wholly surprising, run to the NBA Finals had ended with a wholly lopsided 106-88 defeat in Game 5, and a 4-1 loss in the series. Doncic had been dominant at times, but not often enough.

> "There's so much luck involved," Cuban said, "that if you're waiting on luck, you have to be very, very, very patient. And we knew we had a generational talent in Luka, and I just didn't see a path for us just to be patient."

[Source — The Ringer](https://www.theringer.com/nba-finals/2024/6/18/24180855/luka-doncic-dallas-mavericks-mark-cuban-nba-finals-2024)

### Voice notes

- Most traditionally journalistic of the set: physical detail in the lede ("drenched in sweat and despair, his hair matted"), then narrative scaffolding, then the principal on record.
- Sentences are long but unfussy; rhythm is feature-magazine.
- **Beck stages judgment through sources rather than rendering it himself** — useful contrast point. Where Lowe and Tjarks deliver verdicts in their own voice, Beck builds the case through the principals.
- Less directly applicable to scout-publish (the system delivers its own judgment); useful for opening-paragraph imagery patterns.

## §4 — J. Kyle Mann (PROSPECT scouting register)

**Outlet:** The Ringer. NBA Draft Show, Big Board scouting reports.

### Excerpt — Chet Holmgren scouting report (The Ringer, 2022-05-17)

> Holmgren is very fluid and mobile for his size, and he processes the game quickly, but physically, he ain't twitchy; he'll never make a living off of his 0-to-60 burst from a resting position.

> He's a calculating player with schematic versatility and the ability to switch, hedge hard, and quickly recover; as a result he has an opportunity to be a floor-raising defensive player who can affect the offense.

> His handle is fluid and comfortable for a player his size.

> Chet loves to get to his fadeaway in that 10- to 15-foot range.

> [Mann projects Holmgren as] a critically important secondary star who spends his career being adored by hoop nerds and overlooked by a chunk of the basketball community because his production might not be eye-popping.

[Source — The Ringer](https://www.theringer.com/2022/05/17/nba-draft/chet-holmgren-nba-draft)

### Voice notes

- Closest sample to a pure scouting register.
- **Semicolons chain qualifier-then-projection** — "is X, but he ain't Y; he'll never Z." The scouting move: claim, immediate counter-claim, projection.
- Vernacular drops ("ain't twitchy") cut against the formality.
- Strengths and weaknesses are not separated into bins; they interlace inside the same sentence.
- **Tells you what archetype the player will become**, not just what skills he has. Verdict-as-archetype-projection at close.
- Reserved for prospects (post-launch scope); useful pattern reference for the pro corpus too.

## §5 — Sam Vecenie (STRUCTURAL template — kept; voice register is Mann/Lowe blend)

**Outlet:** The Athletic / Game Theory Substack. NBA Draft + pro NBA. Annual Draft Guide (paywalled).

### Excerpt — Darius Acuff free preview (Game Theory Substack, 2026 cycle)

> Arkansas guard Darius Acuff has been one of the more polarizing prospects in the country this season despite being among its most productive. On the plus side, he's exceptionally polished and smart offensively, making consistently great decisions and playing a much more unselfish brand of basketball than what we saw at lower levels.

[Source — Game Theory Substack](https://gametheorysamv.substack.com/p/potential-arkansas-lottery-pick-darius-129)

### Excerpt — Vecenie on Khaman Maluach (podcast, quoted via On3, 2025)

> The numbers say what they are. Like, his numbers are absolutely insane and, like, the efficiency numbers, the block numbers? You can look at, like, the box plus-minus numbers.

> He struggles to catch the ball, which we knew was an issue coming into the year. I don't think his offensive feel is particularly great yet.

> Guys with this toolset? It doesn't fail. You know what I mean? It just does not. It does not fail. He is a seven-foot-one, seven-foot-two dude with a seven-foot-six wingspan who's a real athlete… There are flaws. And you're just like, okay, what do we do with these flaws now too?

[Source — On3](https://www.on3.com/pro/news/sam-vecenie-weighs-pros-cons-of-khaman-maluach-as-prospect-in-nba-draft/)

### Voice notes

- Acuff opener shows the written register: polarization frame, then plus/minus structure.
- Maluach quotes are spoken (podcast disfluency), but reveal the underlying analytical structure: numbers first → catch flaws → toolset-can-anchor-around-flaws verdict.
- **Vecenie's 4-paragraph structure (Identity / Strength / Weakness / Verdict) is the right consistency anchor.** The rigidity issue at S174 came from over-strict application — short stat-list sentences inside each paragraph rather than Lowe-style braided clauses.
- Full Draft Guide text paywalled; sample size limited.

## §6 — Jonathan Givony (TV-graphics scouting register; minimum-prose contrast)

**Outlet:** ESPN. NBA Draft. DraftExpress founder.

### Excerpt — 2025 NBA Draft Big Board (ESPN)

> Flagg was the best player in college basketball last season despite starting the season as a 17-year-old.

> Playing through injuries and illness, Harper carried an undermanned Rutgers team with his scoring prowess and competitiveness.

> After a revealing summer playing alongside NBA players for the Bahamas national team, Edgecombe had a strong season at Baylor.

> No player in this draft can score like Bailey can when he is firing on all cylinders.

[Source — ESPN](https://www.espn.com/nba/story/_/id/41662748/2025-nba-draft-big-board-rankings-top-100-prospects-players)

### Voice notes

- Compressed, brief — closer to TV-graphics scouting than long prose.
- One-sentence verdicts that combine context (age, injury, team quality) with a single dominant attribute.
- **Useful contrast: this is what minimum-prose scouting reads like.** Efficient and information-dense. Don't write like this for a full _public.md, but the structured fields (sub-domain rationale rows) can lean on this register where appropriate.

## §7 — John Hollinger (DEADPAN-ANALYTICAL register; numbers-guy persona)

**Outlet:** The Athletic. Former Grizzlies VP Basketball Operations. Inventor of PER.

### Excerpts

> Tanking was the word at All-Star Weekend, at least until the part where Anthony Edwards, Kawhi Leonard and Victor Wembanyama conspired to save it with their brilliant and, shall we say, surprisingly inspired play in the main event on Sunday night.

> Texas Tech guard Christian Anderson has been an acquired taste for NBA scouts because he doesn't blow you away with his physical tools. He's 6 feet 2, weighs maaaaaybe 170 pounds and isn't going to wow you with plays above the rim or ankle-breaking crossovers.

[Source — Hollinger article index](https://muckrack.com/john-hollinger/articles)

### Voice notes

- Wry, deadpan persona that pure analytics writing usually flattens — "weighs maaaaaybe 170 pounds," "shall we say, surprisingly inspired."
- Front-office numbers guy who has earned the right to be casual.
- Stats inform the sentence's spine but rarely sit naked on the page.
- Most Athletic work is paywalled; sample size limited.

## §8 — Patterns Across the Best of Them

1. **Judgment in own voice.** Lowe, Tjarks, Mann, Hollinger all render verdicts directly. Not "scouts say X" or "the data suggests Y." The system delivers its own conclusions on the page. Declarative — no hedge words ("might," "perhaps," "potentially" with no commitment). Willing to bound the upside in plain language ("will not become Embiid," "will not be a stretch five at any point in his career") rather than soften with optionality.
2. **Stats as load-bearing pieces of an argumentative sentence**, not bullet-list rows. "A higher share than Chamberlain or Garnett ever produced" is an argument; "29 percent assist rate" alone is a row.
3. **Strengths and weaknesses interlace inside the same clause**, not separated into pros/cons silos. Mann's "is X, but he ain't Y; he'll never Z" is the canonical shape.
4. **Semicolons and qualifiers carry texture in upstream exemplars.** "Rare kind of," "snug complement," "addiction to," "ain't twitchy" — the qualifier IS the judgment, not decoration. **Em-dashes are NOT inherited at the system level**: Lowe uses them as a workhorse but the [PUBLIC-LANGUAGE-GUIDE.md](../PUBLIC-LANGUAGE-GUIDE.md) §5.4 Mitchell sample uses zero. Match the §5.4 target, not the §1 exemplars.
5. **Almost always project an archetype or role at the close.** Tell the reader what the player will be, not only what the player can do. Mann: "a critically important secondary star adored by hoop nerds, overlooked by a chunk of the basketball community."
6. **Conversational pace where useful.** Tjarks at the conversational extreme, Lowe in the middle, Givony at the compressed extreme.

## §9 — Pitfalls to Avoid

- **Stat-rhyming.** Lining up parallel-structure stats in successive sentences ("He shoots 40%. He converts 56% on drives. His TS% is 59.8."). Reads robotic. Each stat needs to earn its sentence by being load-bearing for an argument.
- **Snapshot-stat clustering.** Three or more single-season percentages in one narrative paragraph reads as a stat dump, not scouting prose. The reader cannot retain the cluster; the sentence-level argument disappears under the numbers. Per [PUBLIC-LANGUAGE-GUIDE §5.1](../PUBLIC-LANGUAGE-GUIDE.md) (post-pivot 2026-05-10), target 0–2 stats per narrative paragraph and relocate excess single-season percentages to the sub-domain rationale row that scores that skill. Different failure mode from stat-rhyming above: stat-rhyming = parallel structure with any stats; snapshot-stat clustering = single-season stat density per paragraph, regardless of structure. The four-profile audit (Curry / Kawhi / JJJ / Jokić-draft) surfaced this pattern at 9–12 stats per Strength paragraph with 60–91% sourced from a single recent season.
- **Strict pros/cons silos.** "Strengths: A, B, C. Weaknesses: X, Y, Z." Real scouting prose interlaces.
- **Pure rubric-register verdicts.** "Tier 4 franchise piece." "All-NBA caliber." Reader sees the system, not the player. Strip rubric vocabulary at publish.
- **Series of context-context-attribute sentences.** Fine for big-board paragraphs (Givony register), flattens longform.
- **Lowe-imitation by Claude.** S174 v1–v4 attempted Lowe-density (em-dashes, clausal qualifiers, "rare kind of" texture phrases). All four iterations were rejected; v4-rev2 PASSed PUBLIC-RUBRIC and was still grade-6-fail per Tyler. The system cannot sustain Lowe's register at sentence level without producing AI-tells, contrived comparisons, and writerly clichés. Per S174-F02 the narrative is now Tyler-authored; this doc is reference-only for understanding what professional NBA writing looks like, not a prescription Claude follows.
- **Em-dash density.** Lowe uses them as a workhorse. Claude using them produces stilted prose. The system-output target (PUBLIC-LANGUAGE-GUIDE §5.4) uses zero em-dashes. Match the target, not the exemplar.
