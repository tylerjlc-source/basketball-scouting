## What the Game Actually Is

Basketball is a game of space and time. Offense creates both. Defense destroys both. Every action on the court is either making the floor bigger or making it smaller.

But space isn't just about where players stand. It's about **gravity** — the attention a player demands from the defense by existing on the court. A player with gravity warps the floor. Defenders shift toward them, and that shift opens lanes, windows, and shots for everyone else. A player without gravity shrinks the floor for their teammates, no matter how talented they are individually.

The deepest truth about basketball offense: a player's value is not what they score. It is the total effect of their actions on themselves and their teammates combined. Ben Taylor calls this Global Impact. A player who scores 25 but makes every teammate worse is less valuable than a player who scores 16 but makes every teammate better. The box score captures individual output. It misses the gravity, the spacing, the defensive attention, the screen that freed the shooter, the cut that pulled the help defender one step too far.

Defense is the mirror. The job is to take away time and space — pressure the ball, cut off driving lanes, force tough decisions before the offense is ready. Great team defense is five players connected by trust, rotating as a unit, closing windows before the offense can exploit them. Individual defense matters. Connected defense wins.

## What Makes a Player Valuable

Three questions filter NBA-level talent from everyone else:

**Can this player create an advantage?** Not just score — create. Force the defense to rotate. Collapse the help side and find the open man. Draw a second defender and make the right decision under pressure. Advantage creation is the scarcest skill in basketball. Players who can consistently create advantages against set defenses are rare and valuable.

**Can this player defend more than one type of player?** Versatility on defense is the modern currency. A wing who can guard both backcourt scorers and stretch bigs without constant scheme protection is worth more than a specialist at either. Switchability keeps a player on the court in the playoffs, when lineups shrink and matchups tighten.

**Does the skill hold up under pressure?** The shot that falls in practice needs to fall in the fourth quarter of a close game with tired legs and a hand in the face. Durability of execution — not just physical durability, but skill durability under fatigue and stakes — separates rotation players from closers.

Beyond these three: **playing hard is a skill.** Not a cliché. Scouts treat effort as a genuine differentiator. A player with average tools and relentless competitive character will outperform a more talented player who competes selectively. 78% of NBA general managers rate character as very important or extremely important in draft decisions. The talent field levels out in the NBA. What's left is who wants it more and who does the work.

## The Three Groups

Traditional positions (PG, SG, SF, PF, C) describe where a player lines up. They don't describe what a player does. The modern game runs on functional groups — clusters of players who share physical profiles and skill demands regardless of their listed position.

**Guards** handle the ball, create offense, and defend the perimeter. The spectrum runs from pure playmakers who organize the team to scoring guards who create for themselves. What binds them: they operate primarily on the perimeter, their defensive assignment is typically another guard or small wing, and their physical profile — height, wingspan, lateral quickness — determines which matchups they can take. A 6'6" guard who can switch onto wings blurs the line. A 6'1" guard who can only defend other small guards has a narrower path.

**Wings** are the bridge. They score from multiple areas, defend multiple positions, and often serve as the connective tissue between the backcourt and the frontcourt. The best wings are the most versatile players on the floor — they can guard up or down a size, create their own shot or play off the ball, and function in multiple lineup configurations. The modern NBA is built around wings. The scarcest commodity is a two-way wing who can create, shoot, and defend across positions.

**Bigs** protect the rim, rebound, and anchor the defense. The spectrum runs from traditional post anchors to stretch bigs who space the floor to switchable bigs who defend guards on the perimeter. What binds them: they operate primarily inside or at the elbows, their defensive identity starts with interior presence, and their physical profile — height, weight, standing reach — defines their role. A big who can pass from the post like a point guard (Jokić) or switch onto guards like a wing (Adebayo) expands what's possible. A big who can only catch lobs and protect the rim has a defined but narrower role.

The archetype within each group matters more than the listed position. A 6'8" player listed as SF who defends bigs and rebounds like a PF is functionally a wing with big tendencies. The group determines the peer comparison. The archetype determines the projection.

## How Scouting Actually Works

A scout answers one question: **who will this player be in the NBA — and when?**

That question runs through four inputs, each with different reliability:

**Biometrics** are the most reliable signal. Height, wingspan, standing reach, weight — these set the physical ceiling. They do not change. A 6'2" guard with a 6'4" wingspan has a structural limitation on defense that no amount of effort overcomes. A 7-footer with a 7'4" wingspan and 36" vertical has physical tools that create options no skill can replicate.

**Statistics** tell you what happened, but only in context. The same stat line means different things at different competition levels, in different systems, at different usage rates. The most important stats are often the ones that don't show up on the highlight reel — turnover rate under pressure, defensive shot suppression, off-ball movement that creates open looks for others.

**Scouting language** tells you how and why the numbers happened. Three independent sources converging on "tight handle, creative in traffic, gets to his spots" is a real signal. One source saying "elite prospect" in a recruiting context is noise. The quality of the source and the specificity of the language determine the weight.

**Character** is the variable that compounds everything else. It is the multiplier on all other inputs. Average tools with elite competitive character outperforms the physical ceiling. Elite tools with poor character underperforms every projection. Character is also the hardest input to measure — negative signals (quits, disengages, attitude problems across multiple programs) are more reliably predictive than positive ones.

The projection engine runs on two questions about every skill a player shows:

*Will this work against better competition?* A move that beats college defenders may not beat NBA defenders. A shot that falls against a closing hand may not fall against a contesting wingspan. Translation is not automatic.

*If it doesn't work now, can it be fixed?* Physical limitations (short arms, limited explosion) are structural — they don't change. Skill gaps (shooting mechanics, defensive footwork) are addressable — they can improve with coaching and work. The distinction between genetic ceiling and skill development is the most important judgment a scout makes.

## Governing Principles

**Projection is about trajectory, not current location.** A 19-year-old who improved every year matters more than a 19-year-old who arrived at their ceiling early. Rate of improvement predicts the destination better than present ability.

**The worst observation anchors the score.** Ceiling-framing language maps to the midpoint, not the ceiling. One viral highlight does not override three possessions of poor decisions. This is not pessimism. This is accuracy.

**Absence is data.** No documented post role at any level is itself the signal — the score is baseline. Zero off-screen possessions across two seasons is definitive evidence, not missing data. A scout who waits for positive evidence to appear will miss the negative evidence that is already there.

**The 5.0 line is the most important threshold.** Below 5.0 is a real liability — teams will scheme around it. Above 5.0 is at minimum survivable. Every sub-domain score crosses or doesn't cross this line, and that binary matters more than the difference between a 6.5 and a 7.0.

**Scouting is the beginning, not the end.** Players are not finished products at the point of evaluation. Environment, coaching, fit, teammates, and development infrastructure all shape outcomes after selection. The best evaluation in the world cannot account for what happens next. Humility about projection is not weakness — it is the mark of an honest system.
