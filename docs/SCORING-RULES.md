## R1 — Negative Balance Rule (mandatory)

The worst observation anchors the sub-domain score. Ceiling-framing language maps to the midpoint, not the ceiling. A player with one viral dunk and three turnovers in traffic does not score 9 on at-basket finishing.

## R3 — Competition Temper

Core principle: The NBA is the benchmark. All other competition levels receive a temper proportional to their distance from NBA quality.

**Stat type rules — apply before reading the table:**

| Stat category | Temper treatment |
|---|---|
| Shooting efficiency (3PT%, FG%, TS%, mid-range %, FTR) | Full temper per table |
| Turnover rate | Full temper — competition pressure increases at NBA level |
| Assist rate, rebound rate, block rate | Minimal temper — cap at 10–15% regardless of level |
| Steal rate | Not a primary signal — IQ/anticipation proxy only |
| Qualitative sub-domain observations | Qualitative temper per table |

## R8 — Cross-Reference Rule

Cross-references cap a sub-domain score downward only. They never lift a score upward. Maximum 2 cross-references per sub-domain. A cross-reference earns its place only if it represents a hard physical or cognitive ceiling.

**Confirmed cross-references:**
- #3 → #23 (strength is structural floor for post offense)
- #5 → #8 (handling is prerequisite for off-dribble creation)
- #5 → #24 (shot selection is cognitive ceiling on when to use off-dribble)
- #6 → #24 (shot selection is hard ceiling on mid-range value)
- #12 → #11 (court vision is cognitive ceiling on decision-making)
- #13 → #9 (touch/feel is structural ceiling on pass delivery)
- #15 → #22 (lateral quickness is physical ceiling on on-ball range)
- #15 → #23 (strength caps at point of attack for PF/C only; PG/SG/SF = directional)
- #17 → #21 (vertical is structural ceiling for rim protection range)
- #17 → #23 (strength holds position before block attempt)
- #18 → #23 (strength is primary physical constraint on post defense)
- #18 → #20 (def. rebounding caps completion of post defense — sequential, not structural)
- #20 → #23 (strength is structural floor for interior rebounding)
- #24 → #11 (court vision is hard cognitive ceiling on shot selection)

**Direct empirical measurement override (S96-F02 promoted Session 102):**

Cross-reference caps are designed to constrain extrapolated or projected scores where direct measurement is unavailable. For NBA-vet evaluations with complete statistical pipeline access, a sub-domain score anchored by direct empirical measurement that validates above the cross-reference cap is not subject to the cap.

**Conditions (all required):**
1. Complete stat pipeline present for the sub-domain (not extrapolation from qualitative signal)
2. Direct empirical measurement clearly validates a score above the cap value
3. Subject is an NBA player with sufficient sample size for the measurement (rule of thumb: ≥50 possessions/attempts for Synergy-based measures, full-season tracking data for rate-stat measures)

**Protocol:** Apply the direct empirical score. Document in the Cross-Reference Log: *"Cap considered, not applied — direct measurement validates above cap (S96-F02 override)."*

**Does not apply to:**
- Prospect evaluations where scores are inferred from qualitative signals (R11 regime governs)
- Non-NBA competition-level evaluations without complete statistical access
- Thin-sample direct measurements where the empirical result is not clearly above the cap — in these cases, the cap binds and the sample limitation is flagged

**Rationale:** Cross-reference caps embed a cognitive/physical ceiling argument (e.g., "shot selection cannot exceed court vision"). When observed on-court reality contradicts the ceiling argument on real measurement, the observation governs. Caps prevent over-crediting extrapolated skill; they do not override demonstrated performance.

## R9 — Injury Temper Rule

When athleticism suppression is multi-source documented as temporary and injury-related — not skill regression — the profile reflects the documented healthy baseline.

**Evidence standard (all four must be met):**
1. Injury must be explicitly named (hamstring, quad, ankle, etc.)
2. Multiple sources must confirm the decline was injury-caused
3. Pre-injury evidence must exist to anchor the healthy baseline
4. The decline must map to specific injury mechanisms ("first step and burst off all season")

**When all conditions are met:**
- Score #21, #22, #15 from documented healthy baseline
- Add structured note to Physical Profile: `Injury context: [description] — healthy-state profile applied per R9`
- Do NOT suppress athleticism to match injury-season output

**Contrast with true regression:** Gradual multi-season decline with no documented injury is a genuine development concern, not R9.

## R12 — Injury Evaluation Window Rule

When both evaluation-window seasons are compromised by injury, override the default two-season weighting (0.60 current / 0.40 prior) and anchor evaluation on the most recent anchor-eligible season per the eligibility floor below. Post-injury data serves as secondary reference only.

**Evidence standard (all three must be met):**
1. Injury must be explicitly named and multi-source documented (same standard as R9).
2. Both recent seasons show material injury impact — games missed, minutes reduction, or multi-source documentation of performance-affecting injury across the majority of the season.
3. An anchor-eligible prior season must exist within the player's career arc; if the anchor season is more than 3 years old, flag for aging concern review.

**Application:**
- Primary signal: most recent anchor-eligible season (100% weight).
- Secondary reference: post-injury seasons (directional only — trajectory and recovery read).
- Two-pass script execution (default for NBA vets, S110 Embiid precedent): run domain scripts on the R12 anchor season AND on the most recent fragment season via `--season-override`. Skill 2 anchors scores on R12 healthy data; sub-domains where fragment-season worst-observation shows decline get pulled down from anchor ceiling per R1 negative balance. Numerical re-weighting (e.g., anchor × 0.70 + fragment × 0.30) is a contract change to R12 — not permitted. Worst-observation rule does the work cleanly.
- R9 remains active: athleticism sub-domains (#21, #22, #15) score from healthy baseline with injury context flagged in Physical Profile.
- Research packet flag (mandatory): "Evaluation window: [anchor season] applied as primary per R12 — [season A] and [season B] compromised by [injury]."

**Single-season step-back (S120-F01 promoted Session 120):**

When the current season is 0-GP-compromised (entire-season miss — Achilles, ACL, suspension) but the prior season is healthy, R12 anchor mode is heavier than necessary. A clean two-season window IS available — just one position back. In this case, step the evaluation window back one position:

- new current season = previous prior (most recent healthy season at 0.60 weight)
- new prior season = the season before that (next-most-recent healthy season at 0.40 weight)

The default two-season weighting structure is preserved; only the position is shifted. R12 anchor mode (single-season anchor at 1.00) does not fire — that mode is reserved for the both-compromised case where no clean two-season window is available.

**Trigger conditions (all required):**
1. Current season classification = compromised (gp < FRAGMENT_GP_MIN — typically 0 GP entire-season miss)
2. Prior season classification = healthy (gp ≥ HEALTHY_GP_MIN; passes MPG band check)
3. Before-prior season exists and is anchor-eligible (healthy, or fragment with gp ≥ FRAGMENT_ANCHOR_FLOOR per S96 fragment exception)

If condition 3 fails (no anchor-eligible before-prior), fall through to R12 anchor mode using the prior season as the single-season anchor at 1.00 weight.

Output flag: eval_window.py reports DEFAULT mode with a "step-back ({reason})" note for transparency.

**Anchor selection (S96 — R12 fragment exception):**

When walking back to find an anchor season, the most-recent season meeting the anchor eligibility floor is selected — which may be a fragment season when more recent than any qualifying healthy season.

Anchor eligibility floor:
- Healthy seasons (≥58 GP, MPG check): always eligible
- Fragment seasons (40–57 GP): eligible WHEN the most-recent pre-injury sample (more recent than any qualifying healthy season)
- Fragment seasons (25–39 GP): not anchor-eligible — walk back continues
- Compromised seasons (<25 GP): not anchor-eligible

Rationale: A 50-GP fragment season immediately preceding an injury is a cleaner read of the player's current state than a 60-GP healthy season from two years prior. Sample size below 40 GP is too thin to anchor a 26-sub-domain evaluation.

Output flag: When fragment anchors, eval_window.py output mode reports R12_ANCHOR with a "fragment anchor (X GP)" note for transparency.

**When R12 does NOT apply:**
- One compromised season + one healthy season → default two-season weighting applies (R9 may still govern athleticism sub-domains).
- Gradual multi-season decline with no named injury → treat as genuine regression, not R12.

**Contrast with R9:** R9 handles athleticism suppression within a normal evaluation window. R12 handles the case where the normal evaluation window itself is structurally unusable.

## R13 — Playoff Data Treatment

Playoff performance is distinct signal from regular-season performance — harder competition within the NBA, scouted gameplans, elevated stakes. It is the ultimate ceiling test. Playoff data enters the system through three channels with graduated consequences depending on evidence strength and recency.

**Sample minimums:**
- NBA playoff: ≥2 separate playoff series across different years
- NCAA tournament (prospects): ≥4 career tournament games (Elite Eight depth)
- Below minimum: narrative mention only; no sub-domain or modifier consequences

**Three entry channels:**

**Channel 1 — Sub-domain qualitative stress-test.** Playoff rise/fall language feeds the rationale sentence and confidence tier for any sub-domain where evidence exists. Does NOT move the numeric sub-domain score — that stays anchored to regular-season data per the 60/40 window.

**Channel 2 — Sub-domain direct input.** Three sub-domains treat playoff data as primary signal:
- **#26 Competitive Character** — primary; joins clutch data as top-weight evidence
- **#25 Effort / Motor** — secondary; applies when rise/fall pattern is attributable to effort
- **#12 Decision-Making** — secondary; playoff decisions against elevated defensive schemes test the universal ceiling

**Channel 3 — Projection/composite modifiers.** Two-stage mechanic governed by evidence strength and recency.

**Stage 1 — POT modifier (always, when sample minimum met):**

**Statistical signal thresholds (career matched TS% delta):**
- **Strong rise:** TS% delta ≥ +1 (3+ pts above playoff baseline)
- **Moderate rise:** TS% delta −1 to +1 (matches or beats baseline)
- **Neutral:** TS% delta −3 to −1 (normal playoff efficiency drag)
- **Moderate shrink:** TS% delta −5 to −3 (well below baseline)
- **Strong shrink:** TS% delta ≤ −5 (3+ pts below baseline)

*Playoff baseline note: Star-level players show a ~−2 TS% efficiency drop in playoffs vs. regular season — validated against Murray, Harden, Irving career data (Session 95). Thresholds are baseline-relative, not absolute. Archetype-primary stats (e.g., DFGPOE for defensive specialists) substitute TS% at comparable magnitudes when better suited. Baseline was established from star-level guards; position-specific baselines may differ and should be monitored during wing/big validation.*

| Signal | POT modifier |
|---|---|
| Strong rise — statistical strong rise AND qualitative, ≥2 runs | **+0.40** |
| Moderate rise — statistical rise OR qualitative rise, ≥2 runs | **+0.20** |
| Neutral / absence / mixed | 0 |
| Moderate shrink — statistical shrink OR qualitative shrink, ≥2 runs | **−0.20** |
| Strong shrink — statistical strong shrink AND qualitative, ≥2 runs | **−0.40** |

See PROJECTION-OUTPUT-BLOCK.md for integration with the full POT modifier stack (all values composite-scale, 1.00–9.99, per S98 unification).

**Stage 2 — Composite modifier (gated):**

Both conditions required:
1. Strong validation — multi-source statistical AND qualitative convergence
2. Recency — playoff activity in at least one of the last two playoff cycles

When gate is passed:

| Tier | Magnitude | Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Defining | ±0.20 | Career-defining playoff signal. Finals MVP or equivalent. Rare — once-per-generation cases. |
| Strong | ±0.15 | Multi-run rise/shrink that meaningfully reshapes how the player is viewed league-wide. |
| Moderate | ±0.10 | Clear rise/shrink pattern across multiple series, not career-defining. |
| Directional | ±0.05 | Suggestion of rise/shrink. Single series or mixed evidence. Use sparingly. |

**When Stage 2 does NOT apply:**
- Below sample minimum → neither stage fires; narrative mention only.
- Historical-only evidence (last playoff ≥3 years ago, no intervening activity) → Stage 1 POT absorbs the track record; POT re-evaluated downward to reflect dormant ceiling. Composite unchanged.
- **Active-injury dormancy:** Player meets recency test but has a documented multi-source injury ruling them out of the next playoff cycle → treated as dormant for composite purposes. Stage 1 POT still absorbs career track record. Composite waits for proof-of-return.
- **Convergence-gate fail:** Stage 1 of the gate ("Strong validation — multi-source statistical AND qualitative convergence") fails when:
  - Either axis is neutral / absent — no directional signal on stat or qualitative side.
  - Either axis is mixed — stat or qualitative evidence points in both directions within the axis (rise in some series + shrink in others, or career delta shrink with recent course-correction).
  - Axes diverge — stat rise paired with qualitative shrink, or vice versa (the S99 case, also covered in the "Stage 2 application note" below).

  Gate-fail outcome: Stage 2 modifier is 0. Stage 1 POT modifier still fires per its own thresholds. Composite governed by Steps 1–5 alone.

**Double-count guard:** When Stage 2 composite modifier applies, Stage 1 POT modifier is reduced by one tier (strong ±2 → moderate ±1). Rationale: composite has already absorbed part of the demonstrated track record; POT reflects residual ceiling movement only.

**Stage 2 application note — double-count protection (S99 clarification):**

Stage 2 is additive on top of the composite produced by Tier Assignment Protocol Steps 1–5 (including awards cross-check and anchor comparison). Credentials that Step 4 awards cross-check already prices in (MVP, FMVP, All-NBA, DPOY) must not be double-credited via Stage 2 — Stage 2 fires only when playoff evidence shows signal *beyond* what the credentials captured.

Strict convergence interpretation:
- The statistical side of the gate must independently support the direction the qualitative side is pushing. Divergence (stat shrink with qualitative rise, or vice versa) fails the gate regardless of how prestigious the qualitative signal.
- Finals MVP alone does not fire Stage 2 if TS% career-matched delta indicates shrink — the Finals MVP is already credited via awards cross-check.
- Archetype-primary stat substitution (PPG/DFGPOE for TS%) is a substitution of signal, not a loosening of the gate. Substituted stats must still support the qualitative direction independently.

When the qualitative credential is outside R2 recency (e.g., a 2024 Finals MVP in a 2025-26 evaluation), the credential remains priced into the anchor-comparison placement — Stage 2 firing would still double-count.

**Contrast with related rules:**
- R3 handles cross-circuit competition level (NBA vs NCAA vs HS). R13 handles within-NBA (playoff vs regular season) intensity.
- R9 sets healthy-state baseline when injury suppresses athleticism. R13's active-injury dormancy clause governs when a recent-sample player is treated as dormant for composite purposes.
- R12 overrides the 2-season regular-season window when both years are compromised. R13 operates alongside the window, not as an override.

## R14 — Decimal Precision Discipline

Each row in the position scale defines a band of 5 valid scores (e.g., 8.5–8.9). The descriptor anchors the median of the band. The scorer dials within the band based on whether the player just meets the bar (band floor) or approaches the next-tier descriptor (band ceiling).

High and Medium confidence sub-domains (per R4) dial within the band using full .1 resolution. Low and Very-Low confidence sub-domains land at the band floor only (.0 or .5).

.0 and .5 are valid landing points when the player genuinely sits at the band floor. The defect is *unjustified* clustering — round numbers chosen because the analysis was not pushed far enough to dial within the band.

The same standard applies to composite placement at .00–.99 precision. Round only when the anchor-comparison evidence supports a clean placement.

## R15 — Single-Game Evidence Prohibited

Scouting reports do not cite single games as evidence. Sample size is too small to support scoring or narrative weight. Any scoring or narrative claim resting on a single-game observation must be replaced with season-long pattern evidence or removed.

**Narrow exception:** A clutch moment in a championship-deciding game (Finals Game 6 / Game 7 close-out, or a game directly clinching the title). Conference Finals games, first-round playoff games, and regular-season individual games never qualify.

**Application:**
- Skill 2 (scout-scoring): Sub-domain rationales never anchor on a single game. If only single-game evidence exists for a sub-domain, that's a confidence problem, not a justification.
- Skill 5 (scout-output): Section 3 narrative does not cite specific games unless they meet the championship-clutch exception.
- Skill 7 (scout-publish): Strips any single-game reference that survived from upstream, regardless of source.

**Retroactive policy:** Existing profiles are not swept. Single-game references in already-shipped narratives stay until the player's next re-eval, at which point R15 applies.

**Rationale:** A single-game observation is not statistically meaningful enough to drive scoring or shape the narrative. Cherry-picking a Game 1 dud or a 50-point regular-season explosion misrepresents the player. Season-long patterns are the only evidence base that respects sample-size discipline.
