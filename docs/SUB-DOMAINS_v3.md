# SUB-DOMAINS — Layer 1 Scouting Rubric Reference
**Load during every evaluation session. Keep active throughout scoring.**
**All 26 sub-domain definitions with observable dimensions, proxy signals, and negative balance anchors.**
**Content locked through Session 19. No editorial changes.**
**Scale reference: All sub-domain scores use the 1–10 scale defined in POSITION_SCALE_{GUARDS,WINGS,BIGS}_v1.md (peer-specific) and POSITION_SCALE_UNIVERSAL.md (#12, #24, #25, #26). Scores are expressed to one decimal place. Load and reference the relevant scale before assigning any score.**

**Sub-Domain scoring protocol rule:

MANDATORY PEER RANKING STEP — Required before assigning any sub-domain score.
Before matching a description, answer: "Among active players in this peer group, where does this player rank at this specific skill?"
The ranking must integrate both:

Quantitative signals — statistics, efficiency, volume, consistency over time
Qualitative signals — league-wide consensus, scouting reports, credible analyst and coach assessments

No single statistic determines the rank.
If the ranking is driven by one statistic alone, identify at least one additional signal before assigning the score.
Evaluation window rule:

Active player → rank current ability
Retired player → rank at career median
Injured player → rank based on last active season

The scale description is a confirmation check — not the input. The peer ranking is the input. If the rank and the description conflict, the rank wins.

## Domain 1: Finishing

### #1 — At-basket finishing
What it captures: Conversion at the basket — rim finishing, touch finishing, floater, fingerroll, off-hand, above-rim finishes. How a player scores when they get to the basket by any means.

Observable dimensions:
- Field goal percentage at the rim — primary quantitative signal; filter by attempts within 4 feet where available
- Left-hand conversion rate — off-hand finishing percentage separates players who can finish from players who can only finish from one side
- Floater and fingerroll frequency and conversion — does the player have a secondary touch finish for when the rim is protected?
- Above-rim finish rate — dunks and high-contact above-rim conversions as a share of total rim attempts
- Finish quality against shot blockers — conversion rate when a documented rim protector is the primary defender
- Reverse layup and backboard use — evidence of spatial awareness and touch at the rim when direct angles are closed off

Proxy signals (data-sparse / international):
- Points in the paint per game relative to usage — available from FIBA box scores and most domestic European databases
- Free throw percentage combined with high FTR — a reliable proxy for touch and soft hands at the point of contact
- Qualitative language: "soft hands around the rim," "creative finisher," "uses the glass" — directional; midpoint rule applies
- Age-relative rim efficiency: converting above-position-average at the rim at FIBA U18 level after competition temper is a meaningful signal

Negative Balance anchors:
- Sub-50% at the rim — below this threshold the player is losing possessions at the most efficient spot on the floor
- Single-hand dependency — inability to finish left or right anchors the score to the midpoint even if overall conversion looks adequate
- "Struggles against length," "gets his shot blocked at the rim" — documented inability to adjust to rim protection is a ceiling signal
- High foul-drawing rate with low free throw percentage — in contact situations but cannot convert
- Floater absence in a drive-heavy player — no secondary touch finish; anchors finishing range downward

---

### #2 — Contact finishing / foul drawing
What it captures: Converting through or with contact; intelligent use of body to absorb fouls and earn free throws. Offensive physicality at the point of attack.

Observable dimensions:
- Free throw rate (FTA per field goal attempt) — primary quantitative signal; normalise for role and usage
- Free throw percentage — drawing the foul means nothing if the player cannot convert
- And-one conversion rate — finishing through contact on made baskets
- Body lean and hip use on drives — does the player initiate contact deliberately, or does contact happen to them?
- Euro-step and gather-step sequencing — using footwork to create contact at the optimal moment

Proxy signals (data-sparse / international):
- FTA per game relative to teammates and positional peers — available from all major box score databases including FIBA
- High drive rate combined with high FG% in the paint — signals the player is converting at the rim through contact
- Qualitative language: "gets to the line," "draws contact," "uses his body well," "hard to stop without fouling" — directional; midpoint rule applies
- Age-relative FTR: drawing fouls at above-average rate at U18 Euro or ANGT level after competition temper is a real skill signal

Negative Balance anchors:
- Low FTR in a drive-heavy player — contact avoidance or failure to absorb; anchors the score regardless of finishing percentage
- Sub-65% free throw percentage — draws contact but cannot convert
- "Soft," "avoids contact," "goes around defenders rather than through them" — anchors this to the low end
- Foul-drawing that relies on shot-fake manipulation rather than physical contact-seeking — unreliable against NBA-calibre defenders
- Declining FTR as competition level increases — a skill that does not translate

### #3 — Post offense
What it captures: Footwork, counters, scoring from the post; post playmaking — collapsing the defense and finding cutters (Jokic, Draymond model). Post offense is not only scoring. A player who draws doubles and finds the open man is elite at this dimension.

Observable dimensions:
- Post touch frequency — how often does the player operate with their back to the basket as a designed action?
- Footwork variety — drop step, up-and-under, jump hook, and spin move each represent a distinct counter
- Catch radius and post entry success rate — does the player secure difficult passes in the post?
- Doubling response — when the defense sends a second defender, does the player find the open man or force a difficult shot?
- Post scoring efficiency — points per post-up possession
- Counter usage — does the player have a go-to move and a counter?

Proxy signals (data-sparse / international):
- Post touch frequency from FIBA or domestic European play-by-play
- Points per post-up possession from any available database
- Assist rate in half-court settings — high assist rate from a non-guard in half-court settings is a proxy for post playmaking capacity
- Physically strong player with a noted post game — documented strength profile combined with explicit post role language is directional; cross-reference #23
- Physical profile as lower bound: a player below 220 lbs at C/PF with no documented post role has a structural ceiling regardless of skill language

Negative Balance anchors:
- No documented post role — absence of designed post-up usage at any level scores at or near position baseline
- Single-move dependency — "only goes right," "no counter," "predictable footwork" anchors regardless of conversion rate
- Poor catch radius — cannot secure post entry passes against pressure defense
- Low assist rate from post situations — scoring-only post player is a significantly lower ceiling in the modern NBA
- Below-average strength score — post offense without physical capacity has a hard ceiling; cross-reference #23

Cross-references: #3 → #23

## Domain 2: Shooting

### #4 — Catch-and-shoot 3PT
What it captures: Spot-up shooting off the catch; footwork, release consistency, and range depth.

Observable dimensions:
- Catch-and-shoot 3PT percentage — primary quantitative signal; filter for off-catch attempts only
- Footwork on the catch — does the player set their feet cleanly before releasing?
- Release consistency — same release point, same arc, same timing on every attempt
- Shot preparation off the catch — does the player catch in rhythm with hands ready?
- Range depth — how far back does the player shoot at acceptable efficiency?
- Performance under contest — does shooting percentage hold up against a closing defender?

Proxy signals (data-sparse / international):
- 3PT percentage from any available source — even a single season is a primary signal after competition temper
- 3PT attempt rate — high volume demonstrates comfort and role
- Free throw percentage — strongest mechanical proxy when 3PT volume is low; 85%+ FT% signals the touch and mechanics to shoot; sub-65% caps the shooting ceiling
- Qualitative language: "knockdown shooter," "pure stroke," "shoots it off the catch," "quick release," "lights out from three" — directional; midpoint rule applies
- Shooting performance at camps and combines — directional when season-level data is unavailable

Negative Balance anchors:
- Sub-33% catch-and-shoot 3PT percentage at competition level — below this threshold the player is a net negative as a spot-up shooter
- Mechanical inconsistency — varying release point, hitching, off-balance attempts
- Low FT% combined with low 3PT% — mechanical limitation confirmed by two independent signals
- "Needs a gather," "slow release," "gets his shot blocked on the catch" — catch-to-release inefficiency anchors the score
- Corner-only shooter — a defensive adjustment eliminates this; score at the midpoint regardless of percentage

---

### #5 — Off-dribble shooting
What it captures: Pull-up, step-back — creating own shot off the dribble. Separation moves and release over a contest.

Observable dimensions:
- Pull-up jump shot percentage — primary quantitative signal
- Step-back effectiveness — does the step-back create genuine separation, or is it cosmetic?
- Release point over contest — how high and how quickly does the player get the ball up?
- Shot creation variety — does the player have multiple ways to create a pull-up?
- Change of pace — deliberate deceleration and acceleration within a dribble sequence
- Change of level — intentional shoulder and hip drop to change the defender's focal point
- Performance in late-shot-clock situations — can the player create and convert their own shot when the offense breaks down?

Proxy signals (data-sparse / international):
- Pull-up jump shot percentage from any available advanced database
- Points per isolation possession — strong proxy for self-creation ability
- Free throw rate in half-court settings — drawing fouls off the dribble demonstrates the same creation skill
- Qualitative language: "creates his own shot," "pull-up threat," "step-back jumper," "difficult to guard off the dribble" — directional; midpoint rule applies
- FT% as mechanical proxy — sub-65% caps the shooting ceiling

Negative Balance anchors:
- Sub-38% on pull-up attempts at competition level
- Single-move dependency — one pull-up trigger that defenders can take away; score at the midpoint
- Poor balance at release — consistently fading, falling, or off-balance
- "Can't create his own shot," "pull-up is unreliable" — direct language anchors the score
- Low shot selection score — cross-reference #24

Cross-references: #5 → #8, #24

### #6 — Mid-range
What it captures: Elbow, baseline, pull-up off two feet in the midrange zone. A diminished but real weapon at the NBA level.

Observable dimensions:
- Mid-range field goal percentage — primary quantitative signal
- Elbow efficiency — the most valuable mid-range zone; opens passing lanes to cutters and the post simultaneously
- Footwork off the catch at the elbow — catch, face up, and shoot sequence
- Baseline pull-up — stopping on the baseline and rising into a mid-range jumper
- Shot creation in the mid-range zone off the dribble
- Consistency across game situations — mid-range is the zone most affected by fatigue and pressure

Proxy signals (data-sparse / international):
- Mid-range percentage from any available source
- Two-point percentage excluding rim attempts — reasonable proxy when mid-range splits are unavailable
- FT% as mechanical proxy
- Qualitative language: "reliable mid-range scorer," "elbow specialist," "money from the mid-range" — directional; midpoint rule applies
- Usage context: a player in a system that runs elbow actions has more opportunity signal

Negative Balance anchors:
- Sub-40% mid-range percentage at competition level
- "Avoids the mid-range," "no pull-up in the mid-range zone," "catch-and-shoot only" — absence anchors to low end
- Single-zone dependency — only the elbow but not the baseline, or vice versa; score at the midpoint
- Poor shot selection in the mid-range zone — cross-reference #24

Cross-references: #6 → #24

### #7 — Free throw
What it captures: Stroke reliability, routine, execution under pressure. Indicator of shooting mechanics and mental consistency.

Observable dimensions:
- Free throw percentage — primary and most direct quantitative signal; no adjustment required for competition level
- Routine consistency — does the player follow the same pre-shot routine on every attempt?
- Late-game free throw performance — does the percentage hold in the fourth quarter of close games?
- Two-shot conversion sequence — does the player convert the second shot after making the first?
- Mechanical repeatability — same arc, same release point, same follow-through on every attempt

Proxy signals (data-sparse / international):
- Free throw percentage from any available source — universally available; no competition temper required
- Multi-season FT% trend — improvement signals teachable mechanics and coachability
- Qualitative language: "reliable at the line," "automatic from the stripe," "struggles from the line" — directional; midpoint rule applies

Negative Balance anchors:
- Sub-65% career free throw percentage — a hard mechanical or mental ceiling
- Declining FT% as competition level increases — mechanics or composure that do not hold under pressure
- "Struggles at the line," "automatic hack-a target," "cannot be trusted in the fourth quarter" — direct language anchors
- Inconsistent routine — visible pre-shot variation predicts performance variance
- Low late-game conversion rate — the most damaging signal

## Domain 3: Ball Skills

### #8 — Handling / creation
What it captures: Dribble tightness, moves, creating off live dribble. Driving is initiated here — see R7.

Observable dimensions:
- Dribble tightness under pressure — does the player keep the ball close through traffic?
- Move variety — crossover, between-the-legs, behind-the-back, hesitation, spin; a player with one move is readable
- Live dribble creation — can the player create a scoring opportunity off an unscripted dribble drive?
- Speed maintenance through ball-handling sequences — does the player slow down to execute moves?
- Change of pace and change of level — deliberate deceleration and acceleration; intentional shoulder and hip drop
- Weak-hand creation — equal threat going left and right, or defenders shade one way?

Proxy signals (data-sparse / international):
- Turnover rate per possession — handling at high usage without turning it over demonstrates control
- Assist rate off live dribble — assists generated from dribble penetration
- Drives per game from FIBA or domestic European data
- Qualitative language: "tight handle," "creative with the ball," "gets into the lane at will," "weak handles," "loose with the ball" — directional; midpoint rule applies
- Steal rate allowed — getting stripped repeatedly documents a handle limitation

Negative Balance anchors:
- High turnover rate in ball-handling situations — turnovers from live dribble are the most direct negative signal
- "Gets his dribble picked," "loose handle," "turnover prone in traffic" — direct language anchors
- Weak-hand avoidance — never going left is not ball handling
- Single-move dependency — one dribble trigger is readable; score at the midpoint
- Slowing to execute moves — decelerating to dribble telegraphs the action

---

### #9 — Touch / feel
What it captures: Fingertip control, softness — Kyrie backboard finishes, fingerroll, pass touch. What happens at the point of contact between hands and ball. Distinct from handling, which is about control in space.

Observable dimensions:
- Fingerroll and backboard finish frequency and conversion — the most direct observable expressions of touch
- Pass touch and accuracy on lobs and skip passes — does the ball arrive catchable on difficult passes?
- Off-hand touch on finishes — touch finishing with the non-dominant hand requires fingertip control
- Soft catch under pressure — does the player absorb difficult passes cleanly in traffic?
- Touch on floaters — precise arc calibration at a moving release point
- Spin and english on finishes — deliberate use of backspin or sidespin

Proxy signals (data-sparse / international):
- Free throw percentage — cleanest mechanical proxy for touch; the free throw requires pure fingertip feel with no athleticism component
- Paint scoring efficiency on non-dunk attempts — converting at high rates on non-athletic finishes demonstrates touch
- Qualitative language: "soft hands," "great touch," "feathery finish," "silky," "brick hands," "can't catch," "hard finisher" — directional; midpoint rule applies
- Catch rate on difficult passes — dropped passes at competition level are a negative proxy

Negative Balance anchors:
- "Brick hands," "can't catch," "hard finisher," "no touch around the rim" — direct language is the clearest anchor
- High rate of dropped catchable passes — documented catching problem is a touch and feel deficiency
- Absence of fingerroll or backboard finishes in a drive-heavy player — consistently finishing with power signals touch limitations
- Low FT% combined with poor paint finishing on non-dunk attempts — two independent signals confirming a touch ceiling
- Pass delivery that consistently requires receivers to adjust

---

### #10 — Ball security
What it captures: Protecting possession in traffic, turnover avoidance, securing catches under pressure.

Observable dimensions:
- Turnover rate per possession — primary quantitative signal; normalise for usage rate and role
- Turnover type distribution — live-ball turnovers (strips, stolen dribbles) versus dead-ball turnovers
- Two-hand securing on catches in traffic — catch with two hands and immediately protect?
- Ball protection in the post and on drives — ball high and away from the defender's reach?
- Turnover response under pressure — does turnover rate spike in high-pressure situations?
- Pivot foot discipline — illegal dribbles and travel violations

Proxy signals (data-sparse / international):
- Turnover rate from FIBA or domestic European box scores — universally available
- Assist-to-turnover ratio — above 2:1 is directional for ball security at guard and playmaking forward positions
- Steal rate allowed — frequency of turnovers caused by opponent steals specifically
- Qualitative language: "takes care of the ball," "rarely turns it over," "careless with the ball," "turnover prone" — directional; midpoint rule applies

Negative Balance anchors:
- High live-ball turnover rate — strips and stolen dribbles are the most damaging type
- Turnover rate that rises with competition level — ball security that works at mid-major but breaks down in Power Conference is not NBA-ready
- "Careless," "turnover machine," "can't be trusted with the ball in traffic" — direct language anchors
- Low assist-to-turnover ratio in a playmaking role
- Pivot foot and travel violations — signals loss of body control under pressure

## Domain 4: Playmaking

### #11 — Court vision
What it captures: Reading the floor, seeing secondary options, anticipating defensive rotations before they happen. Vision is what you see.

Observable dimensions:
- Assist rate relative to usage — primary quantitative signal
- Secondary pass identification — does the player see the pass that creates the open shot?
- Anticipatory passing — passes delivered before the receiver has completed their cut
- Defensive rotation reading — does the player identify which defender has rotated out of position?
- Pick-and-roll read quality — correctly identifying the defense's coverage at the right moment
- Weak-side awareness — does the player track players and defenders away from the ball?

Proxy signals (data-sparse / international):
- Assists per game (APG) — most universally available; first directional indicator
- Assist-to-turnover ratio from FIBA or domestic European box scores
- Secondary assist data where available — passes that lead to the pass that leads to the basket; the purest vision signal
- Qualitative language: "sees the floor," "finds the open man," "one pass ahead," "anticipates rotations" — directional; midpoint rule applies
- System context: high assists in a structured half-court system demonstrates vision under constraint

Negative Balance anchors:
- Low assist rate in a high-usage ball-handling role
- "Tunnel vision," "doesn't see the open man," "predictable with the ball" — direct language anchors
- First-option dependency — consistently going to the first read without scanning the floor
- High turnover rate on passes — attempted passes that result in turnovers signal seeing openings that do not exist
- Declining assist rate as competition level increases

---

### #12 — Decision-making
What it captures: Executing the right play under pressure — not just seeing it, doing it. Includes downhill driving decision-making: reading when to attack the paint, initiating drive-kick-swing-catch-and-shoot sequences. Decision-making is what you do with what you see.

Observable dimensions:
- Assist-to-turnover ratio in live-game half-court situations
- Drive-kick timing — on downhill drives, does the player kick to the open shooter at the moment the defense collapses?
- Shot versus pass choice at the point of attack — does the player correctly identify when to finish versus kick out?
- Late-shot-clock decision quality — forced decisions under time pressure are the highest-stress test
- Transition decision-making — does the player correctly read numbers advantages?
- Decision speed — how quickly does the player process and execute? The right play two seconds late is the wrong play
- Tempo management — does the player deliberately vary pace? Push when an advantage exists, slow the game when structure is needed?

Proxy signals (data-sparse / international):
- Assist-to-turnover ratio from FIBA or domestic European box scores
- Late-shot-clock field goal percentage — above-average rates demonstrate decision quality under maximum pressure
- Turnover type breakdown — decision turnovers (wrong play chosen) versus execution turnovers (good decision, poor delivery)
- Qualitative language: "makes the right play," "never forces it," "high basketball IQ," "forces shots," "bad decisions late" — directional; midpoint rule applies

Negative Balance anchors:
- High decision turnover rate — choosing the wrong play repeatedly is a cognitive limitation
- "Forces it," "plays too fast," "can't slow the game down" — direct language is the clearest anchor
- Transition over-dribbling — consistently destroying numbers advantages by dribbling into traffic
- Crunch-time regression — good decisions at 70% of possessions but bad ones at 90% of crunch-time possessions
- Poor drive-kick timing — holding the ball through defensive recovery on drives

Cross-references: #12 → #11

### #13 — Passing execution
What it captures: Accuracy, threading passes in traffic, timing and touch on delivery. Vision is seeing it. Execution is delivering it.

Observable dimensions:
- Assists per game (APG) — most universally available passing output signal
- Pass completion rate on difficult passes — threading passes through traffic, hitting cutters in stride
- Pass velocity calibration — does the ball arrive at the right speed?
- Lob accuracy — timing and placement on lob passes
- Pass touch on delivery to cutters — does the ball arrive where the receiver can catch and go?
- One-handed and off-hand passing — accurate passes with either hand without telegraphing direction
- Pass deception and disguise — pump fakes, head fakes, and look-away passes

Proxy signals (data-sparse / international):
- Assist rate relative to usage — assists require both vision and execution
- Execution turnover rate — turnovers on good decisions with poor delivery; separates execution failures from decision failures
- Touch / feel score — cross-reference #9; pass touch and finishing touch share a mechanical root
- Qualitative language: "accurate passer," "threads the needle," "lobs arrive perfectly," "passes are off," "stiff delivery" — directional; midpoint rule applies
- Assist data from structured European systems — FIBA and domestic leagues with structured half-court offenses produce reliable assist data

Negative Balance anchors:
- High execution turnover rate — good decisions with poor delivery is an execution problem
- "Off on his passes," "receivers have to adjust," "delivery is inconsistent" — direct language anchors
- Lob inaccuracy — consistently overthrown or poorly timed lobs
- Velocity miscalibration — consistently too hard or too soft
- One-hand pass avoidance — only passing with two hands set is limited in traffic

Cross-references: #13 → #9

---

### #14 — Off-ball movement
What it captures: Reading and using screens, cutting, creating space without the ball. Requires both IQ (reading the action) and motor (running the action). Off-ball movement is a skill, not a trait.

Observable dimensions:
- Catch-and-shoot percentage off screens — primary quantitative signal where available
- Screen navigation timing — does the player use the screener at the right moment?
- Cut timing and decisiveness — on backdoor and basket cuts, does the player commit fully and arrive in rhythm?
- Curl versus fade decision — does the player correctly read the defender's position?
- Off-ball spacing maintenance — does the player hold their spot, or drift into occupied areas?
- Screen-setting quality as a read — does the player set screens that create real separation?

Proxy signals (data-sparse / international):
- Catch-and-shoot 3PT percentage — a player converting off the catch at high rates in a movement-heavy system
- Points off screens per game from any available database
- Qualitative language: "moves well without the ball," "great off-ball cutter," "uses screens effectively," "always in the right spot" — directional; midpoint rule applies
- System context: a player in a structured motion offense has more off-ball signal
- Assist-to-basket-cut ratio where available

Negative Balance anchors:
- "Stands around without the ball," "disappears off the ball," "doesn't move in the half-court" — the most direct anchor
- Ball-watching tendency off the ball
- Poor screen navigation — consistently arriving too early, too late, or choosing the wrong action
- Low catch-and-shoot efficiency in a movement-heavy system
- Spacing violations — habitually drifting into occupied areas or collapsing into the paint

## Domain 5: Defense

### #15 — On-ball pressure
What it captures: Guarding ball handlers — length, footwork, intensity, making it difficult. Includes shot contesting: making shooters uncomfortable, altering release, closing out with control.

Observable dimensions:
- Opponent field goal percentage when this player is the primary on-ball defender
- Contested shot rate — how frequently does the player contest shots versus sag or help?
- Close-out control — does the player arrive under control with a hand high, or over-run and foul?
- Defensive foot speed relative to the ball handler — can the player stay in front through a full possession?
- Deflection and steal rate — deflections per possession measures active hands and anticipation
- Switch effectiveness — when switched onto a different position, does the player hold?

Proxy signals (data-sparse / international):
- Steal rate per possession from FIBA or domestic box scores
- Foul rate in isolation defense — high foul rate is a negative proxy; low foul rate with documented on-ball assignment is positive directional
- Wingspan relative to height — a player with a +4" wingspan differential has a structural advantage
- Qualitative language: "hounds ball handlers," "active hands," "makes it difficult," "quick feet," "stays in front," "physical defender" — directional; midpoint rule applies
- Lateral quickness score cross-reference — #22 sets a physical ceiling on on-ball coverage

Negative Balance anchors:
- "Gets beat off the dribble," "struggles to stay in front," "poor on-ball defender" — direct language anchors
- High foul rate in on-ball situations — reaching fouls signal lateral limitations
- Over-helping tendency — avoiding on-ball responsibility
- Below-average lateral quickness score — structural ceiling; cross-reference #22
- Short wingspan relative to position — limits shot contesting range even when foot speed is adequate

Cross-references: #15 → #22, #23

---

### #16 — Help defense
What it captures: Rotations, weak-side positioning, switching, team defensive IQ. Reading the play before it happens.

Observable dimensions:
- Rotation timing — does the player arrive at the help position before the offensive player reaches the spot?
- Weak-side positioning frequency — does the player maintain active positioning away from the ball?
- Switching range — how many positions can the player credibly switch onto?
- Charge-taking frequency — willingness to establish legal guarding position in the path of a drive
- Deflection and steal rate in off-ball situations
- Help-and-recover execution — after leaving an assignment to help, does the player recover before the swing pass arrives?

Proxy signals (data-sparse / international):
- Team defensive rating when this player is on the floor versus off — directional for overall defensive contribution
- Block rate from non-primary defensive assignments — blocking shots away from their direct man demonstrates help-side awareness
- Qualitative language: "reads the game defensively," "great rotations," "always in the right spot," "high defensive IQ" — directional; midpoint rule applies
- Coaching system context: defensive praise within a scheme-heavy system carries more weight

Negative Balance anchors:
- Ball-watching tendency — documented failure to track weak-side cutters
- Late rotations — arriving after the offensive player has already scored
- "Gambles for steals," "out of position," "loses assignments" — positional discipline failures anchor
- Switching refusal or inability — hard ceiling in the modern NBA
- High on-ball effort with documented weak-side lapses — effort does not equal IQ; scores at the midpoint

### #17 — Rim protection
What it captures: Shot-blocking, altering shots at the basket, verticality, deterrence — changing what the offense even attempts.

Observable dimensions:
- Opponent FG% at the rim when this player is the primary defender — primary signal where available
- Block rate per possession — primary fallback proxy where opponent FG% tracking is unavailable
- Shot alteration rate — shots affected but not blocked; the higher-value deterrence skill
- Verticality in block attempts — does the player go straight up, or reach, swipe, and foul?
- Positioning before the drive arrives — does the player establish help position early?
- Deterrence effect — does the opponent's shot selection change when this player is in the paint?
- Weak-side block frequency — blocks originating from the weak side require anticipation and range

Proxy signals (data-sparse / international):
- Block rate from FIBA or domestic European box scores
- Standing reach from combine or FIBA physical testing — single most predictive physical measurement for rim protection ceiling
- Wingspan relative to height — elite wingspan differential enables shot altering even without elite vertical
- Foul rate — high foul rate in paint situations is a negative proxy; low foul rate with documented shot-blocking signals verticality and timing
- Qualitative language: "protects the rim," "deters drives," "great timing," "shot blocker" — directional; midpoint rule applies

Negative Balance anchors:
- Low block rate at competition level — a described rim protector who does not produce blocks is not a rim protector
- High foul rate on block attempts — reaching and swiping signals poor verticality and timing
- Below-average standing reach for position — structural ceiling; cross-reference #21
- "Gets out of position chasing blocks," "gambles," "leaves his assignment" — shot-blocking instinct without positional discipline
- Deterrence failure — opponents continuing to attack the rim at team-average rate

Cross-references: #17 → #21, #23

---

### #18 — Post defense
What it captures: Holding position in the post, contesting post moves, box-out strength, denying deep catches.

Observable dimensions:
- Opponent FG% on post-up possessions when this player is the primary defender
- Post entry denial rate — how often does the player prevent the catch entirely?
- Displacement under contact — does the player hold their ground when the offensive player leans?
- Contest quality on post moves — does the player contest the finish without fouling?
- Box-out effectiveness — securing defensive rebounding position after a post attempt
- Denial footwork — active hands and foot positioning to front or three-quarter the post entry

Proxy signals (data-sparse / international):
- Opponent post-up FG% from any available database
- Post-up possession frequency allowed — a defender who rarely allows post catches demonstrates denial skill
- Weight and strength profile relative to position — physically undersized post defender has a structural ceiling; cross-reference #23
- Foul rate in interior defensive situations — high foul rate signals inability to hold position
- Qualitative language: "holds position in the post," "tough to back down," "denies the catch" — directional; midpoint rule applies
- Rebounding rate — a player who consistently secures defensive rebounds after post contests demonstrates positioning and physicality

Negative Balance anchors:
- "Gets backed down," "too weak to hold position," "gives up deep catches easily" — physical inability is the hardest ceiling
- High foul rate in post defense — reaching and holding signal inability to stay in front legally
- Below-average strength score — structural ceiling; cross-reference #23
- Poor rebounding rate after post contests — allowing offensive rebounds off your own contest
- Opponent FG% on post-ups above position average — where data exists, this overrides all positive qualitative language

Cross-references: #18 → #23, #20

## Domain 6: Rebounding

### #19 — Offensive rebounding
What it captures: Reading misses, positioning, aggression, tip-ins — second-chance creation.

Observable dimensions:
- Offensive rebounding rate — primary quantitative signal, normalised for position and usage
- Box-out evasion — does the player get around or through a defender's box-out attempt?
- Tip-in and putback conversion rate — not just getting the ball but finishing the second chance
- Positioning on teammate shot attempts — does the player anticipate the shot and move to rebounding position before the ball is in the air?
- Pursuit range — how far from the basket does the player track and recover an offensive rebound?
- Second-chance points generated — points produced directly from offensive rebounds

Proxy signals (data-sparse / international):
- Offensive rebound rate from FIBA or domestic European box scores
- Total rebound rate relative to position — directional for offensive rebounding capacity
- Qualitative language: "crashes the glass," "relentless on the offensive boards," "great instincts on the offensive glass" — directional; midpoint rule applies
- Physical profile: weight and wingspan relative to position — a physically undersized player at C/PF has a structural ceiling
- Cross-reference #25 (effort/motor) — offensive rebounding at the highest level requires sustained pursuit across full possessions

Negative Balance anchors:
- Low offensive rebound rate at competition level — the most direct anchor; language does not override data
- "Doesn't crash," "floats to the perimeter after shots," "doesn't pursue misses" — passive rebounding anchors low
- Below-average pursuit range — a player who only rebounds what falls directly to them
- Poor tip-in conversion — getting to the ball but not finishing
- Below-average weight and wingspan for position — structural physical ceiling particularly at C/PF

### #20 — Defensive rebounding
What it captures: Boxing out, securing, outlet — ending opponent possessions.

Observable dimensions:
- Defensive rebounding rate — primary quantitative signal, normalised for position
- Box-out execution — does the player make contact and establish position before the ball arrives?
- Contested rebound security — securing the ball through contact without fumbling
- Outlet pass quality — speed and accuracy of the first pass after securing the board
- Rebounding consistency across game situations — does the player box out in close games?
- Positioning on opponent shot attempts — does the player locate their man before the ball?

Proxy signals (data-sparse / international):
- Defensive rebound rate from FIBA or domestic European box scores
- Total rebound rate relative to position — directional for defensive rebounding capacity
- Foul rate on rebound attempts — fouling repeatedly while rebounding signals poor box-out technique
- Qualitative language: "secures the defensive glass," "strong rebounder," "boxes out well" — directional; midpoint rule applies
- Physical profile: height, weight, and wingspan relative to position; cross-reference #23 for strength component

Negative Balance anchors:
- Low defensive rebound rate at competition level — the most direct anchor
- "Doesn't box out," "gets out-rebounded," "loses position on the glass" — direct language anchors
- Ball-watching tendency — going straight for the ball without locating their man
- Poor outlet passing — securing the rebound but failing to initiate the break efficiently
- Inconsistency in high-pressure situations — requires qualitative sourcing to identify

## Domain 7: Athleticism

### #21 — Burst / explosion
What it captures: First step, vertical leap, above-rim ability, acceleration — how quickly a player reaches top speed out of a stance, off a screen, or into a drive.

Observable dimensions:
- Measured vertical leap (standing and max) from combine or pro day — most reliable single data point
- Time to top speed off a standing start — observable in first-step situations
- Above-rim finishing frequency — ratio of dunks and high-contact above-rim finishes relative to total rim attempts
- Lob and alley-oop catch rate — ability to time and finish above the rim in live game action
- Acceleration into the first dribble off a pin-down, flare screen, or baseline cut
- Transition finishing — frequency of above-rim or explosive finishes in the open floor
- Floor-running frequency for big men — does the C/PF sprint the floor in transition and create scoring opportunities as a lob or fastbreak threat? Distinct from halfcourt explosion — this is a repeatable effort and athleticism combination; frequency and conversion rate in transition directly measures the will and ability to run

Proxy signals (data-sparse / international):
- Combine vertical measurements — standing reach, max vertical, standing vertical
- Sprint time from combine / pro day agility drills
- FIBA physical testing data from national team camps and European youth competitions — sprint times and agility measurements are documented at FIBA U17/U19 World Cup, EuroBasket U20, and major qualification camp events; when available, these are primary signals equivalent to combine data
- Fastbreak points per game relative to teammates — directional when vertical data is unavailable
- Recruiting service athleticism grades for HS/AAU players — directional only, not primary

Negative Balance anchors:
- Combine vertical at or below position average — a structural limitation
- Inability to convert above-rim in open floor situations — consistently finishing with floaters or layups despite open lanes
- "Straight-line speed but no burst," "no first step" — applies the midpoint rule
- Late-arriving cuts — timing suggests reading, not exploding
- Repeatedly beaten to the spot above the rim by rim protectors

### #22 — Lateral quickness
What it captures: Change of direction, defensive slide, hip mobility, agility in space.

Observable dimensions:
- Lane agility time from combine — single most reliable measurement for this sub-domain
- Ability to stay in front of a ball handler on the perimeter across multiple dribble moves
- Hip drop and redirect speed in close-out situations
- Defensive slide distance before losing position
- Change-of-direction quickness in the open floor — cutting angle, deceleration, and re-acceleration
- Foot speed in pick-and-roll coverage

Proxy signals (data-sparse / international):
- Lane agility and three-quarter sprint times from combine, pro day, or FIBA physical testing
- FIBA physical testing data from national team camps and European youth competitions — sprint times and agility measurements are documented at FIBA U17/U19 World Cup, EuroBasket U20, and major qualification camp events; when available, these are primary signals equivalent to combine data
- Steals and deflections per possession — frequency of ball contact correlates with range and reaction speed in space
- Opponent FG% at the rim when this player is the primary on-ball defender
- Qualitative language: "fluid," "hips," "stays attached," "mirrors the ball handler," "quick feet," "fast feet" — directional; midpoint rule applies

Negative Balance anchors:
- Combine lane agility time above position average — measurable ceiling on lateral range
- Documented tendency to reach rather than slide — reaching fouls are a signature of lateral limitations
- Repeated beat-off-the-dribble sequences at competition level
- Hip stiffness or straight-line bias — "straight-line athlete" in reports
- High foul rate in isolation defense — compensating for lateral limitations with hand activity
- "Heavy feet," "slow feet," "flat-footed" — any language anchoring lack of quickness in space

### #23 — Strength
What it captures: Functional basketball strength — holding position, absorbing contact, finishing through physicality.

Observable dimensions:
- Measured weight and body composition relative to position — a 6'8" SF at 230 lbs of documented muscle is a direct physical indicator
- Ability to hold post position against a stronger defender
- Finishing through contact at the rim — converting layups and dunks when a defender initiates contact
- Screen quality — does the screener create displacement, or do defenders walk through them?
- Ability to stay in front of a larger player on a switch
- Box-out effectiveness — securing rebounding position against physical opposition
- Physical play in transition — absorbing contact without losing control

Proxy signals (data-sparse / international):
- Measured weight and wingspan from combine or FIBA physical testing — weight relative to position is the primary proxy when film is unavailable
- Free throw rate — players who draw contact consistently have functional strength to put themselves in contact situations
- Age-relative body development: a 17-year-old above position-average weight is a projectable physical signal
- Qualitative language: "plays through contact," "physically mature," "uses body well," "strong base" — directional; midpoint rule applies
- Competition-relative displacement: physical dominance at U18 Euro level requires competition temper

Negative Balance anchors:
- Documented weight below position average with no projection pathway
- "Gets pushed off his spot," "too slight," "needs to add strength" — direct language flagging inability to hold position
- High rate of contested shot avoidance — consistently redirecting away from contact
- Foul-drawing failure on drives where contact is initiated
- Inability to finish against zone defenses where physicality in the paint is required

## Domain 8: IQ / Motor

### #24 — Shot selection
What it captures: Taking the right shots — reading the defense, knowing when to shoot vs. pass vs. attack.

Observable dimensions:
- True shooting percentage relative to shot diet — a player taking difficult shots at high volume who shoots efficiently demonstrates good selection; a player taking easy shots at low efficiency documents poor selection
- Shot quality distribution — what percentage of attempts are at the rim, open 3s, mid-range pull-ups, and contested long-twos?
- Contested versus open shot ratio
- Shot clock discipline — does the player shoot with time on the clock when the shot is right, or force attempts as the clock expires?
- Post-turnover shot selection — how does the player respond after a turnover? Forcing a quick shot to compensate is a documented pattern
- Transition shot discipline — does the player take the right shot in transition?

Proxy signals (data-sparse / international):
- True shooting percentage from FIBA or domestic European box scores
- Shot attempts per game by zone where available
- Assist-to-shot attempt ratio — a player who creates for others proportional to their own attempts demonstrates restraint
- Qualitative language: "takes the right shots," "knows his spots," "never forces it," "forces up bad shots" — directional; midpoint rule applies
- Usage rate context: high usage with high efficiency is strong positive signal; high usage with low efficiency documents a selection problem

Negative Balance anchors:
- High usage combined with below-average true shooting percentage
- "Forces shots," "shoots too many bad shots," "doesn't know when to give it up" — direct language is the clearest anchor
- High proportion of contested long-twos — the lowest-value shot taken at high frequency
- Shot selection that deteriorates late in games
- Ignoring open teammates to manufacture own attempts

Cross-references: #24 → #11

### #25 — Effort / motor
What it captures: Relentless pursuit — loose balls, sprints back in transition, deflections, plays that don't appear in the box score but change games.

Observable dimensions:
- Deflection rate per possession — most direct quantitative signal for active motor
- Loose ball recovery rate
- Transition sprint rate — does the player sprint back in transition defense on every possession, or jog selectively?
- Second-effort plays — putbacks, tip-outs, offensive charges taken, diving on the floor
- Minutes-to-output consistency — does the player's activity level hold in the fourth quarter and in back-to-back games?
- On-ball defensive engagement — does the player make every defensive possession difficult, or coast on possessions that feel decided?

Proxy signals (data-sparse / international):
- Deflections and loose balls recovered per game where available
- Hustle stat aggregates — charges taken, contested shots, box-outs
- Minutes per game relative to performance output — heavy minutes in a winning program demonstrates sustained effort
- Qualitative language: "never stops," "first to the floor," "relentless," "high motor," "elite effort," "coasts," "selective effort," "disappears in stretches" — directional; midpoint rule applies
- Character sourcing: coach quotes about effort in practice — one of the few sub-domains where coaching context language carries more than directional weight

Negative Balance anchors:
- "Coasts," "selective effort," "disappears in stretches," "not always engaged" — the most direct anchor
- Declining output in the fourth quarter or back-to-back games — conditional motor, not genuine motor
- Low deflection and loose ball rate at competition level — a player described as high-motor who does not produce hustle stats is not high-motor
- Bench body language — disengagement on the bench is the same trait as disengagement on a possession that feels decided
- Single-game effort spikes — going all-out in showcase games but coasting in regular season; awareness of observation, not genuine motor

### #26 — Competitive character
What it captures: Physicality and intensity in contested situations. Willingness to compete on both ends when it is hard. The year-3 differentiator. "It sucks to play against this player."

Observable dimensions:
- Performance in high-stakes situations — does the player's output hold or improve in close games, rivalry matchups, and elimination scenarios?
- Response to adversity on the court — after a turnover, a bad call, or a scoring run by the opponent, does the player compete harder or withdraw?
- Physicality in contested situations — does the player initiate and absorb contact willingly, or avoid it?
- Defensive intensity in losing situations — does the player guard hard when the game is out of reach, or disengage?
- Competitive body language — how does the player respond to being scored on, benched, or criticised?
- Peer and opponent testimony

Proxy signals (data-sparse / international):
- Performance trajectory in tournament and championship settings — FIBA knockout rounds, conference tournaments, rivalry games
- Coach sourcing in non-recruiting context — a coach who describes competitive character post-game or post-season is sourcing something observed; higher credibility than recruiting language
- Qualitative language: "competes every possession," "hates to lose," "brings intensity," "folds under pressure," "disappears in big moments," "soft" — directional; negative signals carry more weight than positive ones per R1
- Longevity and availability — consistently preparing, staying available, maintaining performance across a full season
- Character sourcing from multiple independent contexts — the same description from a coach, an opposing scout, and a teammate is a strong signal

Negative Balance anchors:
- Documented fading in high-stakes moments — "disappears in big games," "can't be trusted in crunch time," "folds under pressure" — the single most damaging character signal
- Adversity withdrawal — a player who disengages after bad moments is demonstrating a pattern; one documented example anchors; multiple is a hard ceiling
- "Soft" — a word scouts use precisely and sparingly; in a multi-source context it overrides positive character language
- Declining effort in losing situations
- Off-court character flags — transfers under negative circumstances, documented attitude issues, poor relationships with coaching staff; multiple flags across multiple programs is a ceiling that talent rarely overcomes
